# Research Pass: Home Remodeling Contracts, Required Notices, and Cancellation Windows (Oregon CCB Exam)

## Topic selected
**Home-remodeling contract compliance** (written contract threshold, required notices, and 1-day vs 3-day cancellation rights).

Why this topic: It is highly relevant to residential remodeling jobs and is explicitly represented in the Oregon PSI exam blueprint under **Contracts** and **Oregon Const. Contractor Laws and Regulations**.

## 2) Topic facts with citation anchors

### Confirmed facts (supported directly by Tier 1 text)

1. The PSI Oregon contractor exam is **open-book**, uses **80 scored items**, has a **180-minute** scored segment, and requires **70%** to pass. (`T1-PSI-CIB-2023`)
2. The PSI outline allocates **9 items** to **Contracts** and **18 items** to **Oregon Construction Contractor Laws and Regulations**. (`T1-PSI-CIB-2023`)
3. Under ORS 701.305, a contractor may not perform qualifying residential work without a written contract when the aggregate contract price exceeds **$2,000**; if the job starts below that amount but later exceeds it, the contractor must deliver a written contract within **five days** after knowing (or when they should know) it will exceed $2,000. (`T1-ORS-701`)
4. ORS 701.305 states that failure to have the required written contract **does not automatically void the contract**. (`T1-ORS-701`)
5. ORS 701.310 provides a **next-business-day** cancellation right for a property owner entering an initial written residential contract, with exceptions including substantial commencement and agreed early start. (`T1-ORS-701`)
6. CCB guidance distinguishes the ORS 701.310 one-day right from a separate **three-business-day** cancellation right for qualifying home-solicitation contracts and links that framework to ORS 83.720. (`T1-CCB-Contractor-Tools`, `T1-ORS-83`)
7. CCB contractor guidance states required residential contract terms include contractor identifying info (including CCB number), customer/worksite identification, work description, price/payment terms, owner-rights explanation, and mediation/arbitration disclosure if present. (`T1-CCB-Contractor-Tools`)
8. CCB contractor guidance states that when total residential contract value exceeds $2,000, contractors must provide specified consumer notices and keep proof of owner receipt for **two years**. (`T1-CCB-Contractor-Tools`)

### Inference (reasonable synthesis, but not a direct quote from one single controlling sentence)

1. A high-yield exam-prep segment for remodelers should combine: written-contract trigger + notices/proof + cancellation windows + solicitation scenario recognition. (Inferred from PSI weighting plus CCB/ORS content.)
2. “One-day vs three-day” confusion is likely a test trap because both rights can appear in residential/remodeling scenarios but are triggered differently. (Inferred from CCB side-by-side guidance and ORS 701/83 structure.)
3. Course language should avoid promising that every remodeling contract automatically receives a 3-day cancellation period; that depends on solicitation context and statutory applicability. (Inference constrained by ORS 83 framework and CCB explanation.)

## 3) Exact claims safe to teach (course-ready wording)

1. “For Oregon residential construction, improvement, or repair, a written contract is required once the aggregate contract price exceeds $2,000; if the price crosses that threshold during performance, the written contract must be delivered within five days after the contractor knows or should know it will exceed $2,000.” (`T1-ORS-701`)
2. “Under ORS 701.310, property owners generally have until midnight of the next business day to cancel an initial written residential construction contract, subject to statutory exceptions.” (`T1-ORS-701`)
3. “A separate 3-business-day cancellation framework exists for qualifying home-solicitation contracts under ORS 83.720; do not assume it applies to every job.” (`T1-ORS-83`, `T1-CCB-Contractor-Tools`)
4. “CCB contractor guidance identifies required terms in residential written contracts, including contractor identity/license details, project/worksite description, price/payment terms, and owner-rights/ADR disclosures.” (`T1-CCB-Contractor-Tools`)
5. “For qualifying residential projects over $2,000, CCB guidance requires delivery of specific consumer notices and retention of proof of delivery for two years.” (`T1-CCB-Contractor-Tools`)
6. “On the PSI exam, Contracts is a meaningful tested area (9 items) and appears alongside Oregon laws/regulations content (18 items).” (`T1-PSI-CIB-2023`)

### Claims that should **always** carry a citation on the course page

- Any numeric threshold/time rule: “$2,000,” “five days,” “next business day,” “three business days,” “two years.”
- Any statement beginning “Oregon law requires…”
- Any statement about cancellation exceptions (e.g., substantial commencement / early start agreement).
- Any statement describing required contract terms or required notices.
- Any statement about exam structure/weighting/passing score/open-book status.

## 4) Unresolved ambiguities / source gaps

1. **Current OAR canonical text access gap:** public web indexing of the exact current OAR 812-012-0110 text is inconsistent; the accessible CCB compilation is dated (2014). Need fresh Oregon SOS OAR pull before publishing strict “must include” wording as legal text.
2. **Scope boundaries between ORS 701.310 and ORS 83.720 in edge cases:** public summaries explain practical differences, but some fact patterns (who initiated contact, place of acceptance, emergency waivers) need scenario-specific legal reading to avoid overbroad teaching claims.
3. **Penalty consequences for notice/contract defects:** CCB pages reference potential consequences (including lien-related consequences and possible fines), but exam-safe teaching should cite the exact statute/rule text per consequence rather than relying only on summary bullets.
4. **Change-order formalities:** strong practical guidance exists, but this research pass did not locate a single current primary source sentence in Tier 1 that cleanly supports broad, absolute wording like “all change orders must be signed before work begins” across every context.

## 6) Recommended boundaries for assessment scope

### What the course **should** assess

- Identifying when Oregon’s written residential contract requirement is triggered.
- Distinguishing 1-day ORS 701.310 cancellation from 3-day home-solicitation cancellation under ORS 83 framework.
- Recognizing high-level required contract elements and notice-delivery/proof-retention obligations from CCB guidance.
- Applying PSI blueprint priorities (contracts + Oregon laws/rules) in exam-style fact patterns.

### What the course **should not** assess (unless newly sourced)

- Detailed, verbatim OAR clause memorization unless current OAR text is directly captured from Oregon SOS rules and pinned.
- Broad absolutes on change-order enforceability across all project types without a direct controlling source.
- Attorney-level legal strategy or litigation outcomes from contract defects.
- Penalty dollar amounts unless tied to current, verified rule text.
