## Definition

Programming through CDs is the disciplined process of turning owner needs into buildable, coordinated drawings and notes. “Programming” starts with function, budget, and constraints; “through CDs” means carrying those decisions into dimensioned plans, details, and specifications used for permitting, pricing, and construction.

## Why it matters

Many residential failures come from drift between early intent and final documents. A GC who manages programming through CDs reduces redesign, prevents scope gaps, and keeps bids comparable.

## Where it appears in a real project

- Discovery meetings with owner and designer.
- Schematic and design development check-ins.
- Permit-set readiness review before submittal.
- Preconstruction handoff to field supervision and subs.

## Inputs needed

- Owner goals (use, durability priorities, quality level).
- Budget target and schedule milestones.
- Site constraints (zoning, utilities, access, slope, wildfire context).
- Code assumptions (ORSC path, local amendments, planning conditions).
- Existing conditions for remodel/addition projects.

## Outputs produced

- Program brief with prioritized requirements.
- Scope matrix tied to drawing sheets and key specs.
- Permit-ready CD package with coordination notes.
- Bid-ready clarifications to lower allowance risk.

## Key decisions

1. Must-have vs. nice-to-have scope.
2. Cost control strategy (value engineering now vs. change orders later).
3. Level of detail required before permit and bid release.
4. Decision deadlines for selections impacting structure/envelope/MEP.

## Basic workflow

1. Define owner outcomes in plain language.
2. Convert outcomes into measurable design criteria.
3. Check criteria against budget and code constraints.
4. Review CDs with a GC coordination lens (buildability, sequencing, scope ownership).
5. Issue pre-bid clarifications and lock assumptions.
6. Submit permit set and track revision log.

## Risks and edge cases

- Program creep after CDs are “90% complete.”
- Owner decisions delayed on long-lead products.
- Remodels with hidden conditions that invalidate early assumptions.
- Multi-agency comments that reopen previously settled decisions.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

In Oregon, code-cycle and jurisdiction process details affect how complete a permit set must be at intake versus correction rounds. In Ashland, hillside constraints, wildfire context, and planning sensitivities often make early program clarity especially important.

## Example scenario

A learner-GC receives a remodel/addition project with a tight budget. By writing a one-page program brief, then tracing each requirement to specific CD sheets before permit submittal, they catch an omitted mudroom ventilation detail and a door-clearance conflict. Those fixes happen before bid, not as field rework.

## Checklist

- [ ] Program brief approved by owner.
- [ ] Budget alignment check completed at each design phase.
- [ ] Critical code assumptions documented.
- [ ] Scope matrix maps each system to drawing/spec ownership.
- [ ] CD coordination review completed before permit intake.
- [ ] Revision log issued to all bidders and trades.

## Quiz questions

1. What is the main purpose of “programming through CDs”?  
   A) Choose paint colors early.  
   B) Translate owner goals into coordinated, buildable documents.  
   C) Delay decisions until framing starts.  
   D) Replace permits with private inspection.

2. Which is the strongest GC control point before bidding?  
   A) Waiting for subcontractor RFIs.  
   B) Issuing a scope matrix tied to CDs.  
   C) Skipping owner signoff.  
   D) Holding all selections as allowances.

3. What is a common edge case in remodel programming?  
   A) No existing-condition surprises.  
   B) Hidden conditions that force redesign.  
   C) Zero jurisdiction comments.  
   D) No budget impact from changes.

**Answer key:** 1-B, 2-B, 3-B.
