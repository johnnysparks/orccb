## Definition

**Prohibited plants** are plant species you cannot intentionally install, preserve, or move on a project site when state, local, HOA, utility, or fire-safety rules restrict them.

For residential GC competence, treat this as a **site compliance + risk control** topic, not just a landscaping preference issue.

## Why it matters

Plant restrictions can block permit approval, fail inspections, create wildfire exposure, damage adjacent property, and trigger expensive rework after final walkthrough.

On real projects, prohibited-plant mistakes often happen because plant decisions are made late by multiple parties (owner, designer, nursery, installer) without one final compliance check.

## Where it appears in a real project

- **Pre-design / feasibility:** Existing vegetation survey and early constraints review.
- **Permit set development:** Landscape sheets, erosion-control plans, defensible-space notes, and utility clearance details.
- **Procurement:** Nursery substitutions and availability-driven species swaps.
- **Installation / closeout:** Field verification before inspection and owner handoff.

## Inputs needed

- Project jurisdiction (city/county) and parcel-specific constraints.
- Current local prohibited/invasive/noxious plant guidance.
- Fire-risk and defensible-space requirements where applicable.
- Utility and right-of-way planting restrictions.
- HOA/CC&R planting rules (if applicable).
- Landscape plan and approved substitutions log.

## Outputs produced

- Compliant plant schedule (approved species list).
- Marked “do-not-install” list in project file.
- Inspection-ready landscape as-built notes.
- Owner maintenance handoff notes (what to avoid planting later).

## Key decisions

1. **Remove vs. retain existing vegetation** during site prep.
2. **Native/noninvasive replacements** when preferred species are disallowed.
3. **Who owns final approval** for plant substitutions (GC, designer, owner, AHJ).
4. **How strict to set procurement controls** (pre-approval required vs. field approval).
5. **When to re-verify rules** if schedule extends across seasons or code-cycle updates.

## Basic workflow

1. Build a **jurisdiction checklist** early (city/county + fire + utility + HOA).
2. Identify any species that are prohibited or conditionally restricted.
3. Tag existing site plants: retain, remove, or further review.
4. Align designer and installer on an approved plant schedule.
5. Lock substitution rules before procurement.
6. Verify installed species before requesting final landscape-related inspections.
7. Include owner-facing notes to prevent post-closeout noncompliant plantings.

## Risks and edge cases

- Plant delivered under common name, but scientific species differs from approved list.
- A nursery substitution is “close enough” visually but noncompliant by rule.
- Existing prohibited species remain because demolition/clearing scope was unclear.
- Mixed authority conflict (city guidance vs. HOA standard vs. utility easement rule).
- Project delay spans a new season/code update and prior assumptions become outdated.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

In Oregon, contractors should confirm which requirements are set by statewide code adoption versus local jurisdiction process at the time of permit and inspection coordination. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.

For Ashland-area work, treat prohibited plants as a **live local-check item**: verify current City of Ashland and Jackson County requirements, wildfire/defensible-space expectations, and any neighborhood-level controls before procurement and again before final inspection.

## Example scenario

A GC is building a custom home in Ashland with a landscape plan approved six months earlier. During procurement, the original shrubs are unavailable, and the nursery proposes substitutions.

The GC requires scientific-name verification, runs the substitution list through the project jurisdiction checklist, and rejects two species that conflict with local restrictions. The team installs approved alternatives, updates the as-built landscape notes, and passes final inspection without rework.

## Checklist

- [ ] Jurisdiction and parcel constraints confirmed.
- [ ] Current prohibited/restricted plant lists checked.
- [ ] Existing vegetation disposition documented (retain/remove/review).
- [ ] Approved plant schedule includes scientific names.
- [ ] Substitution approval workflow documented.
- [ ] Utility/ROW and defensible-space clearances confirmed.
- [ ] Installed species field-verified before final inspection.
- [ ] Owner handoff includes prohibited-plant caution notes.

## Quiz questions

1. **What is the best definition of a prohibited-plant control task for a residential GC?**  
   A) A design preference the owner can decide at closeout.  
   B) A compliance and risk-control check tied to permit, inspection, and site constraints.  
   C) A nursery purchasing issue only.  
   D) A task handled only after final inspection.

2. **When is the most effective time to catch prohibited-plant issues?**  
   A) After installation.  
   B) During warranty period.  
   C) During early constraints review and again before procurement/inspection.  
   D) Only if a neighbor files a complaint.

3. **What is the highest-value control to reduce substitution mistakes?**  
   A) Accept common-name matches from supplier invoices.  
   B) Require scientific-name verification and documented approval workflow.  
   C) Defer all substitution decisions to field labor.  
   D) Remove all original plan references.

**Answer key:** 1-B, 2-C, 3-B.
