## Summary

**CPM pull-planning** combines two scheduling ideas: (1) CPM (Critical Path Method), which identifies the activities that directly control project completion, and (2) pull-planning, where trades collaboratively plan backward from milestone dates to define realistic handoffs. Source: nascla-contractors-guide-or-2e.

For a residential general contractor, this matters because the exam and real projects both reward disciplined sequencing, not just a static Gantt chart. A strong GC sets milestone targets, confirms constraints before work starts, and updates the near-term plan every week as site realities change. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors, nascla-contractors-guide-or-2e.

## Lesson

### Definition

**CPM pull-planning** is a hybrid scheduling approach:
- **CPM layer:** model activities, durations, predecessors, and float to identify the critical path.
- **Pull-planning layer:** gather superintendent, key subs, and suppliers to plan backward from fixed milestones (inspection dates, dry-in, substantial completion).

CPM answers, “What tasks control finish date?” Pull-planning answers, “What must be ready immediately before each milestone, and who commits to that handoff?” Source: nascla-contractors-guide-or-2e.

### Why it matters

- It converts schedule ownership from “the office scheduler” to “the whole field team.”
- It catches missing prerequisites early (submittals, materials, utility clearances, weather windows).
- It reduces idle time and trade stacking.
- It improves forecast credibility for owners and lenders.

In contractor-competence terms, this is a core transition from novice planning (“list tasks”) to professional control (“manage dependencies and commitments”). Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

### Where it appears in a real project

You will use CPM pull-planning at least four times:
1. **Preconstruction baseline:** set milestone structure and first critical path.
2. **Phase planning:** framing phase, rough-in phase, and finish phase each get focused pull sessions.
3. **Weekly work planning:** confirm what is truly ready to start in the next 1–2 weeks.
4. **Recovery planning:** after delays (inspection failure, weather event, late truss delivery), re-pull the sequence from the next hard milestone.

See also [[working-with-subcontractors]], [[change-order]], and [[estimating]].

### Inputs needed

Minimum inputs for a usable pull-planning session:
- Contract milestone dates and completion requirements.
- Permit/inspection sequence and lead times.
- Scope breakdown by trade.
- Durations (best estimate) and predecessor logic.
- Material procurement lead times.
- Crew capacity assumptions.
- Site constraints (access, staging, weather exposure).
- Risk assumptions (what could stop work).

If these inputs are weak, your CPM network may look clean but fail in the field.

### Outputs produced

A good session should produce:
- Updated milestone map.
- Activity network with critical path and near-critical paths.
- 2–6 week look-ahead plan by trade.
- Constraint log with owner and due date for each constraint.
- Commitment list (“who will do what by when”).
- Recovery actions for any missed commitments.

These outputs become your control package for superintendent meetings.

### Key decisions

During planning, the GC must decide:
- Which milestones are fixed vs negotiable.
- What level of float buffer is needed for weather and inspection uncertainty.
- Whether to resequence work or add resources when behind.
- When to lock decisions vs keep options open.
- Which constraints are “stop work” critical.

The most important judgment call is whether a task is **truly ready** (design, materials, access, labor, and predecessor completion all confirmed).

### Basic workflow

1. **Set milestones:** lock required dates (permit issuance, rough inspections, CO/occupancy goal).
2. **Build CPM draft:** list tasks, durations, predecessors; identify initial critical path.
3. **Run pull session:** start at next milestone and plan backward with each trade.
4. **Validate constraints:** for each promised handoff, verify submittals, materials, labor, access, and inspection prerequisites.
5. **Publish look-ahead:** issue 2–6 week work plan plus constraint owners.
6. **Weekly update cadence:** compare planned vs done; re-sequence and re-forecast.
7. **Recovery trigger:** when milestones slip or float is consumed, run focused re-planning immediately.

### Risks and edge cases

Common failure modes:
- **Paper-ready but field-not-ready tasks** (missing material, unavailable crew).
- **Inspection bottlenecks** not reflected in predecessor logic.
- **Over-optimistic durations** from estimator carryover.
- **Weather exposure** ignored in envelope sequencing.
- **Trade stacking** in small residential footprints.
- **Change orders** inserted without network impact analysis.

Edge case: if one long-lead item (for example a custom window package) controls dry-in, that procurement task may become the true critical driver even before field mobilization.

### Oregon/Ashland relevance

For Oregon residential work, scheduling quality supports compliance and predictable inspection flow under state/local administration, and it is directly useful for open-book exam scenarios that test practical project-management judgment. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

For Ashland-area projects, seasonality, wildfire smoke periods, and hillside/site-access constraints can materially affect production rates and sequencing. Your pull plan should explicitly include weather windows, fire-season contingencies, and access logistics rather than assuming “standard” suburban production pace.

### Example scenario

A 2,200 sf custom home in Ashland targets dry-in by **October 15** before heavier fall weather.

- CPM draft shows framing → roof sheathing → underlayment as the critical path.
- Pull-planning session reveals window package lead time is 12 weeks, and dry-in definition includes installed windows for interior moisture protection goals.
- Team changes logic: window submittal approval becomes an early critical activity.
- Constraint log assigns architect/owner approval deadline in week 2.
- Weekly reviews track submittal status as a milestone protection item.

Result: the team protects the October dry-in target by managing a preconstruction constraint that CPM alone might underemphasize.

### Checklist

Use this before each weekly planning meeting:
- [ ] Milestones still realistic and communicated.
- [ ] Critical path updated after last week’s actual progress.
- [ ] 2–6 week look-ahead issued by trade.
- [ ] Each planned task passes a readiness check (design, materials, labor, access, predecessors).
- [ ] Constraint log has an owner and due date for every open item.
- [ ] Recovery actions assigned for any missed commitments.
- [ ] Weather/inspection risks reviewed for next two weeks.

### Quiz questions

1. In CPM pull-planning, what is the primary purpose of the pull session?
   - A) Replace CPM calculations entirely
   - B) Plan forward from day one without milestones
   - C) Plan backward from milestones to confirm real handoffs and readiness
   - D) Delegate scheduling to one subcontractor

   **Answer:** C.

2. A task appears on your 2-week look-ahead but materials are not released. What is the best classification?
   - A) Ready work
   - B) Constraint-locked work
   - C) Completed work
   - D) Optional work

   **Answer:** B.

3. If weather delay consumes all float on a near-critical path, what should the GC do first?
   - A) Wait until month-end report
   - B) Run immediate recovery pull-planning from next fixed milestone
   - C) Remove weather assumptions from future plans
   - D) Freeze updates to avoid confusion

   **Answer:** B.

4. Which output is most useful for accountability in weekly meetings?
   - A) A generic bar chart with no names
   - B) Constraint log with owner and due date
   - C) An unsigned meeting note
   - D) A list of completed tasks only

   **Answer:** B.

## Audio Script

<!-- Estimated listening time: 8 minutes -->

Today’s topic is CPM pull-planning, a practical scheduling method every residential general contractor should know.

Start with the distinction. CPM tells you which activities mathematically control the finish date. Pull-planning brings the people doing the work into the schedule conversation and asks them to plan backward from milestones. Together, these methods make your schedule both logical and buildable.

Think about what usually breaks a residential schedule. It is often not the absence of a schedule file. It is handoffs that were assumed instead of confirmed. Materials not released. Inspections not sequenced. Or multiple trades promised in the same tight workspace.

In a CPM pull workflow, your first job is to lock the milestone dates that matter. Then you map predecessor logic and identify your critical path. After that, the field team validates whether each handoff is actually ready.

Readiness is the key concept. A task is not ready just because it appears next in sequence. It is ready only when design information is complete, materials are available, labor is committed, access is clear, and predecessor tasks are done.

Every week, you compare planned work to completed work, update the network, and revisit constraints. If float disappears or a milestone slips, you do not wait for the monthly update. You run a focused recovery pull session right away.

For Oregon and Ashland residential jobs, the same method applies, but local realities matter. Inspections, weather windows, and site logistics can all control production pace. Put those realities directly into your constraint log.

If you remember one thing, remember this: a professional schedule is not just a chart. It is a commitment system that gets the right work ready at the right time.

## Key Terms

**Estimating** — The process of forecasting costs, labor, material quantities, and durations used to plan and price project work. Better estimates support more realistic activity durations in CPM and pull-planning. Source: nascla-contractors-guide-or-2e.

**Change Order** — A formal change to contract scope, cost, and/or time that should trigger schedule impact review and possible resequencing of critical and near-critical work. Source: nascla-contractors-guide-or-2e.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating CPM as a one-time document.** Schedules that are never updated stop reflecting site reality and lose decision value.

**Confusing activity sequence with task readiness.** “Next in line” does not mean a task can start safely or productively.

**Running pull sessions without decision-makers present.** If the people controlling labor, procurement, and approvals are absent, commitments are weak.

**Ignoring near-critical paths.** Teams that monitor only one path often miss secondary delays that later become critical.
