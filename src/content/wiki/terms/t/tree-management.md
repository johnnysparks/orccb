## Definition

**Tree management** in residential construction is the planning, protection, pruning, removal coordination, and documentation work around existing trees before and during site work.

For contractor study purposes, treat it as a cross-domain skill that combines [[building-permits]], [[demolition-and-clearing]], [[project-scheduling]], and [[jobsite-safety]]. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

## Why it matters

- Trees can directly change site layout, access, excavation sequencing, and utility routes.
- Root zones can be damaged by equipment traffic, trenching, and stockpiles, creating liability and rework risk.
- Many jurisdictions apply local rules to tree removal and protection, so missing permit or notice steps can delay the job.

Study guidance: For exam readiness, think of tree management as a risk-control and planning discipline rather than a landscaping task.

## Where it appears in a real project

You will see tree management decisions in:

1. **Pre-bid walk** (identify trees likely to affect driveway, foundation, or grading).
2. **Estimating** (include protection fencing, pruning/removal subcontracts, stump/root handling, disposal, and restoration allowances).
3. **Permit planning** (confirm city/county tree rules and any arborist report triggers).
4. **Preconstruction meeting** (mark tree protection zones and no-go equipment paths).
5. **Earthwork and utilities** (control trench paths and spoil placement around roots).
6. **Closeout** (verify required replacement planting or final inspection items, if applicable).

## Inputs needed

- Site survey with tree locations/diameters (or best available field markups).
- Grading and utility plans.
- Jurisdiction permit requirements and local tree-rule checks.
- Scope assumptions from owner and design team (save/remove/prune intent).
- Qualified trade input (arborist or tree service, when needed).

## Outputs produced

- Tree inventory/marking sheet for construction planning.
- Tree protection plan (fencing limits, access routes, material staging exclusions).
- Scope line items in estimate and contract clarifying what is included/excluded.
- Permit application attachments or notes required by local jurisdiction.
- Field verification checklist used by superintendent/foreman.

## Key decisions

- **Preserve vs remove** each significant tree based on design impact, risk, and owner priorities.
- **Protection boundary size** and enforcement method.
- **Timing** of pruning/removal relative to demolition, excavation, and utility trenching.
- **Who performs specialized work** (self-perform vs licensed/qualified subcontractor).
- **Contract language** for unknown underground conditions (roots, stumps, hidden conflicts).

## Basic workflow

1. **Identify**: Walk site, tag trees likely to affect construction.
2. **Screen rules**: Check local jurisdiction requirements early.
3. **Align scope**: Confirm owner/designer intent and document assumptions.
4. **Price accurately**: Carry separate tree-management cost items.
5. **Plan controls**: Install protection before heavy equipment mobilizes.
6. **Monitor**: Inspect protection zones during excavation and utility work.
7. **Document changes**: Use change-order process if unknown conditions alter scope.
8. **Close out**: Capture final compliance evidence for project records.

## Risks and edge cases

- Unmapped roots discovered during trenching.
- Neighbor disputes over boundary-line trees.
- Storm-damaged trees creating immediate safety hazards mid-project.
- Utility conflicts forcing last-minute redesign near protected trees.
- Owner-driven late design changes that turn “preserve” trees into removal candidates.

Study guidance: These are classic change-order triggers; tie them to [[change-order]] discipline and written documentation.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon projects are reviewed through local building jurisdictions, so contractors should verify city/county-specific submittal and review requirements at permit planning time. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.
- In Ashland-area work, expect local land-use and site-development context to matter; tree requirements are often handled at the city level rather than as a statewide one-size-fits-all construction rule.

Study guidance: Before bidding, confirm current Ashland municipal tree requirements directly with the City of Ashland planning/building resources and record that verification in your bid notes.

## Example scenario

A contractor bids a home addition in Ashland. Two mature trees sit near the planned driveway widening and one near a proposed sewer trench.

The contractor:

1. Flags tree-risk scope during pre-bid.
2. Carries a separate allowance for protection fencing and potential root-conflict mitigation.
3. Confirms jurisdiction requirements before permit submittal.
4. Assigns a no-equipment zone around retained trees in the site logistics plan.
5. Issues a change-order request when trench rerouting is required after root conflict is exposed.

Result: fewer surprises, cleaner documentation, and better schedule control.

## Checklist

- [ ] Site walk includes tree/conflict notes and photos.
- [ ] Local tree-rule check completed before final estimate.
- [ ] Contract scope states included/excluded tree work.
- [ ] Tree protection zones shown in site logistics plan.
- [ ] Crew briefed on no-go zones and staging limits.
- [ ] Daily/weekly field checks documented.
- [ ] Change-order workflow ready for root/utility conflicts.
- [ ] Closeout packet includes tree-related compliance evidence as required.

## Quiz questions

1. **When should tree-rule verification happen for best risk control?**  
   A) After framing starts  
   B) During final punch  
   C) During pre-bid and permit planning  
   D) Only if a neighbor complains  
   **Answer:** C

2. **Which item most directly reduces accidental root-zone damage?**  
   A) Waiting for final inspection  
   B) Installing and enforcing protection boundaries before heavy equipment use  
   C) Removing all trees by default  
   D) Deferring decisions to closeout  
   **Answer:** B

3. **A trench hits major roots not shown on plans. What is the best contractor control action?**  
   A) Continue trenching and decide later  
   B) Stop, document conditions, evaluate alternatives, and process change-order impacts  
   C) Ignore and bill lump sum at the end  
   D) Cancel the project immediately  
   **Answer:** B
