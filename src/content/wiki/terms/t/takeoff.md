**Takeoff (pre-construction)** is the step-by-step process of measuring and counting the labor, materials, equipment, and specialty scopes needed to build a project from the plans, specifications, and scope notes before work starts. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors, oregon-ccb-education-providers.

For residential GC competence, takeoff is where you convert drawings into a buildable shopping list and production plan, so your estimate, schedule, and subcontract scope all start from the same assumptions. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors, oregon-ccb-contractor-tools.

## Definition

A **takeoff** is a quantified breakdown of project scope made before construction, usually organized by trade and building system (sitework, concrete, framing, roofing, finishes, etc.).

A solid takeoff answers three practical questions:
1. What exactly is included?
2. How much of it is required?
3. Under what assumptions (waste, production rates, alternates, exclusions)?

## Why it matters

- It prevents underbidding by catching missing scope before contract signing.
- It improves gross margin by reducing change-order surprises caused by quantity misses.
- It aligns purchasing, subcontractor bids, and crew planning.
- It supports cleaner client communication because pricing is tied to explicit quantities and assumptions.

## Where it appears in a real project

1. **Opportunity review**: quick conceptual takeoff to decide whether to pursue the job.
2. **Bid development**: detailed takeoff used to build estimate line items.
3. **Pre-award clarifications**: revised takeoff after RFIs/addenda.
4. **Buyout and procurement**: takeoff used to compare vendor and subcontractor proposals.
5. **Preconstruction planning**: quantities translated into phase plans and delivery schedules.

## Inputs needed

- Current plan set and revision log (architectural, structural, MEP as applicable).
- Specifications, scope narrative, and owner preferences.
- Local code-driven requirements that affect quantities (insulation, egress, fire separation, etc.).
- Site observations (access, slope, existing conditions, staging limits).
- Unit cost assumptions, production rates, and waste factors.
- Known exclusions, allowances, and alternates.

## Outputs produced

- Quantity worksheet by scope/trade.
- Assumptions log (waste factors, rounding rules, exclusions).
- Estimate-ready bill of materials and labor hours baseline.
- Bid package inputs for subcontractors/suppliers.
- Versioned snapshot tied to a specific plan date.

## Key decisions

- **Granularity**: assembly-level vs component-level takeoff detail.
- **Contingency approach**: where to place risk (waste vs contingency line).
- **Self-perform vs subcontract**: which scopes need internal vs external pricing.
- **Allowance strategy**: what is fixed, what is allowance, what is excluded.
- **Revision control**: when plan changes trigger a full or partial re-takeoff.

## Basic workflow

1. **Confirm drawing set**: lock the exact plan/spec version to prevent mixed assumptions.
2. **Segment scopes**: divide by CSI-style scope buckets or company trade packages.
3. **Measure/count systematically**: follow a repeatable order (foundation to roof, exterior to interior).
4. **Apply factors**: waste, laps, overlaps, productivity modifiers, and minimum order quantities.
5. **Cross-check**: compare against historical projects or a second-pass review.
6. **Map to estimate codes**: connect takeoff quantities to cost codes and bid lines.
7. **Freeze + publish**: issue the version used for pricing, then track changes against it.

## Risks and edge cases

- **Plan conflicts** between architectural and structural sheets.
- **Addenda timing** that changes quantities after pricing is mostly complete.
- **Hidden existing conditions** in remodel work (rot, out-of-plumb framing, undocumented utilities).
- **Unit mismatch errors** (LF vs SF vs CY).
- **Double counting** shared scope at trade boundaries.
- **“Allowance creep”** where too much unknown work sits outside hard quantities.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

In Oregon residential work, open-book exam and field success both reward disciplined documentation. A clean takeoff package helps support compliant contracts, defensible change orders, and better owner communication when scope evolves. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors, oregon-ccb-contractor-tools.

For Ashland-area projects, hillside lots, wildfire-related resilience upgrades, and retrofit-heavy housing stock can increase preconstruction uncertainty. Practical takeaway: spend more effort on verification walks, assumptions logging, and contingency placement before committing fixed-price numbers. This is a field-practice inference and should be validated on each project.

## Example scenario

A GC is pricing a 1,650 SF Ashland single-family remodel plus 420 SF addition.

- The first takeoff shows 92 sheets of drywall.
- A second-pass review catches a missed garage fire-separation upgrade and raises quantity to 101 sheets.
- Roofing scope is split between tie-in repair and new addition area to prevent overlap with the roofer’s bid.
- The estimator tags three owner-selection allowances (tile, vanity lighting, appliances) and excludes premium upgrades from base bid.

Result: The proposal is clearer, subcontractor comparisons are apples-to-apples, and margin risk from quantity misses is reduced.

## Checklist

- [ ] Confirm latest drawing/spec/addenda set and date-stamp it.
- [ ] Build scope-by-scope quantity sheet with units clearly labeled.
- [ ] Record waste factors and why they were chosen.
- [ ] Flag assumptions, allowances, exclusions, and owner selections.
- [ ] Run an independent cross-check (peer review or historical benchmark).
- [ ] Map quantities to estimate cost codes and subcontract bid scopes.
- [ ] Archive the issued takeoff version for audit trail and change tracking.
