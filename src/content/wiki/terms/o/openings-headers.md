## Definition

**Openings/headers** covers how doors, windows, and large wall penetrations are framed so loads bypass the opening safely.

A **header** is the structural member above an opening that transfers gravity load to jack/trimmer studs and then to supports below.

For contractors, this domain also includes sequencing, layout accuracy, and protecting lateral wall performance when openings grow or shift.

## Why it matters

- Incorrect header sizing or support is a direct structural risk.
- Opening changes are common in residential work and can invalidate both gravity and lateral assumptions.
- Door/window rough-opening errors cause expensive callbacks for multiple trades.

## Where it appears in a real project

1. Plan review and takeoff for window/door schedules.
2. Wall layout and rough opening framing.
3. Shear wall coordination near large openings (garage/front elevations).
4. Window/door install prep and exterior envelope integration.
5. Framing inspection and correction pass.

## Inputs needed

- Approved plans with opening schedule and structural details.
- Current Oregon code references and local interpretation where applicable. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption.
- Product dimensions/manufacturer install tolerances.
- Wall role map (bearing, shear, nonbearing).
- Field verification of as-built dimensions.

## Outputs produced

- Correct rough openings and installed header/support conditions.
- Preserved load path around openings.
- Better fit for windows/doors and fewer envelope rework issues.
- Inspection-ready framing with documented field adjustments.

## Key decisions

- Header strategy by span/load condition.
- Support member placement and continuity to foundation.
- Whether a field opening change needs engineered review.
- Coordination between framing tolerance and manufacturer install requirements.

## Basic workflow

1. Cross-check opening schedule against structural details.
2. Mark wall lines for opening locations and support members.
3. Frame openings/headers and verify bearing continuity.
4. Recheck rough-opening dimensions before sheathing or wrap progression.
5. Perform lateral-impact check when modifying opening size/location.
6. Document corrections before requesting inspection.

## Risks and edge cases

- “Value engineering” opening increases without structural confirmation.
- Mislocated king/jack studs reducing header bearing.
- Large stacked openings causing load concentration conflicts.
- Garage and multi-panel slider openings reducing shear capacity.
- Retrofit remodels where existing wall role is uncertain.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon code-cycle changes and local administration make current-reference checks essential before finalizing opening/header assumptions. Source: oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption.
- In Ashland projects, remodel work in older housing stock often reveals as-built deviations that must be resolved before enlarging openings.
- Seismic context means opening decisions should always include lateral-system implications.

## Example scenario

A client requests a wider patio slider after permit approval.

You pause field framing, compare requested width to structural sheets, identify reduced wall return at one side, and obtain revised detail direction before proceeding.

Result: opening change is incorporated without creating a hidden lateral weakness or failed inspection.

## Checklist

- [ ] Opening schedule aligned with structural details.
- [ ] Header/support members match approved plan intent.
- [ ] Rough-opening dimensions verified before wrap/window install.
- [ ] Bearing and load-path continuity confirmed below opening supports.
- [ ] Lateral impacts reviewed for any opening change.
- [ ] Corrections documented prior to inspection request.
