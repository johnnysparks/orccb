## Definition

**Unusual geometry** means building shapes or layouts that depart from simple rectangular, stacked residential patterns (offsets, large cantilevers, stepped plans, complex roof intersections, curved walls, split levels, or significant level changes).

For a GC, this domain is about turning complex geometry into buildable sequencing, accurate layout control, and reliable structural/envelope outcomes.

## Why it matters

- Geometric complexity increases error probability in layout, framing, and finish tolerances.
- Structural and waterproofing details become less forgiving at intersections and offsets.
- Small early mistakes propagate into major downstream fit and schedule problems.

## Where it appears in a real project

1. Concept and permit-set development with irregular plans.
2. Foundation layout for stepped or offset footprints.
3. Framing and roof intersection construction.
4. Window/door and cladding interfaces at angled or curved conditions.
5. Final trim/finish where cumulative tolerance is exposed.

## Inputs needed

- Fully coordinated plan and detail set (architectural + structural).
- Layout control strategy (datums, control lines, elevation benchmarks).
- Current Oregon code framework and local review requirements. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption.
- Prefab/engineered component lead times and tolerances.
- Enhanced QC plan with extra hold points.

## Outputs produced

- Buildable sequence for complex geometry.
- Verified dimensional control at key transitions.
- Reduced clash/rework between framing, envelope, and finish trades.
- Improved inspection readiness despite atypical shapes.

## Key decisions

- Which geometric conditions require engineering clarification.
- Where to place extra benchmark checks before committing production framing.
- Panelized/prefab vs site-built approach for complex intersections.
- How to phase work to keep critical conditions visible for QC.

## Basic workflow

1. Identify all non-standard geometry conditions on plan set.
2. Create a dedicated layout and benchmark control map.
3. Hold pre-task coordination with framing, roof, and envelope leads.
4. Build high-risk geometry zones in controlled sequence with interim checks.
5. Verify as-built dimensions before irreversible next-trade installs.
6. Complete inspection and closeout with photo/document record of complex nodes.

## Risks and edge cases

- Compound angle errors at roof/wall intersections.
- Step-foundation alignment drift affecting upper framing.
- Curved or angled wall transitions conflicting with standard products.
- Complex drainage paths creating hidden water traps.
- Scope gaps when no one owns intersection detailing between trades.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Ashland-area hillside lots and custom-home patterns can increase unusual-geometry frequency.
- Oregon code compliance still applies fully to irregular designs; complexity does not reduce documentation or inspection burden.
- Local interpretation and inspection expectations should be confirmed early for atypical details and sequencing.

## Example scenario

A custom home uses an offset footprint with intersecting shed and gable roofs plus a stepped foundation.

You establish additional benchmarks, stage framing by geometry zone, and require sign-off measurements before each transition is enclosed.

Result: the team avoids compounded angle and elevation errors that would otherwise trigger major rework.

## Checklist

- [ ] Interim measurement checks scheduled before concealment.
- [ ] Envelope and drainage details reviewed at complex nodes.
- [ ] As-built documentation captured for inspection and closeout.
