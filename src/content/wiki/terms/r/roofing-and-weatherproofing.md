## Summary

Roofing and weatherproofing is the trade domain focused on building a roof assembly that keeps water out, sheds water correctly, handles ventilation and flashing transitions, and supports long-term durability. For residential general-contractor competence, this is not just a shingle-install task; it is systems coordination across framing, underlayment, flashing, penetrations, gutters, and inspection timing. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, ors-chapter-701.

In Oregon, the practical approach is to learn roofing as both code-governed work and risk management work. Code cycles can update details, so a competent GC checks current adopted-code status before relying on memory, then plans trade sequencing to avoid leaks, fall hazards, and costly rework. Source: oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption, oar-437-003-subdivision-m-fall-protection.

## Lesson

### Definition

Roofing and weatherproofing is the planning, installation, and verification of roof-layer components that protect the structure from rain and weather exposure over the life of the home.

### Why it matters

- Roof failures can damage framing, insulation, interiors, and finishes quickly.
- Roofing mistakes often create hidden moisture problems that appear months later.
- In project management terms, dry-in is a major schedule milestone that unlocks interior work.

### Where it appears in a real project

- Late framing / sheathing completion.
- Dry-in phase before broad rough-in and interior finish acceleration.
- Exterior completion and punch phases for final drainage and penetration checks.

### Inputs needed

- Approved plans and roof details.
- Current Oregon code references adopted by jurisdiction.
- Material submittals and manufacturer installation instructions.
- Weather window and site logistics plan.
- Safety plan for falls, ladders, and edge exposure.

### Outputs produced

- Dried-in roof assembly.
- Verified flashing at transitions and penetrations.
- Documented corrections from inspections and quality checks.
- A roof system ready for downstream exterior and interior work.

### Key decisions

- Roofing material type and slope compatibility.
- Underlayment approach and sequencing.
- Penetration and valley detailing strategy.
- Coordination timing with mechanical/electrical penetrations.
- Repair-versus-replace decisions when existing substrate conditions are found.

### Basic workflow

1. Confirm plans, code cycle, and jurisdiction-specific requirements.
2. Verify deck readiness and moisture condition before install.
3. Stage materials and execute safety controls.
4. Install underlayment and critical transitions first.
5. Install roof covering and accessory components.
6. Complete flashing and penetration seals.
7. Perform quality walk, correct deficiencies, and document completion.

### Risks and edge cases

- Wet substrate installed over without correction.
- Incomplete flashing integration at walls/chimneys/skylights.
- Weather interruption between layers.
- Late trade penetrations after roofing complete.
- High-wind exposure areas needing tighter fastening discipline.

### Example scenario

A small single-family project in Ashland reaches sheathing completion in late fall. The GC schedules roofing in a two-day weather window, prioritizes underlayment and valley/penetration protection on day one, then completes roof-covering and final flashing checks on day two. By documenting dry-in completion and remaining punch items the same week, the GC reduces interior moisture risk and keeps rough-in sequencing intact.

## Audio Script

<!-- Estimated listening time: 8 minutes -->

Roofing and weatherproofing is one of the highest-leverage domains for a residential general contractor. Think of it as a systems milestone, not a single trade task.

When your roof assembly is done correctly, you create a clean handoff into interior work because the building is far less exposed to rain and moisture events. When roofing is done poorly, problems can stay hidden until they become expensive.

Your operating rhythm is simple: verify code and plan assumptions first, verify deck readiness second, run a disciplined install sequence third, and do a documented quality walk at the end.

In Oregon, remember that adopted code cycles can change. A competent contractor verifies current references rather than relying on memory from a prior cycle. In field terms, this means checking your details before crews start.

In Ashland practice, weather timing can quickly change priorities. If you have a short weather window, protect critical transitions early, then close out the rest of the assembly with punch documentation.

Keep repeating the same principle: roofing quality is coordination quality. If framing, roofing, and MEP penetrations are synchronized, your risk drops and your schedule stabilizes.

## Key Terms

**[[flashing]]** — Integrated detailing at penetrations and interfaces used to direct water to the exterior as part of a weather-resistant wall-envelope system. Source: oregon-bcd-2021-orsc-significant-changes.

**[[drainage-plane]]** — A moisture-management concept where incidental water is directed to drain out of the wall assembly rather than becoming trapped. Source: oregon-bcd-2021-orsc-significant-changes.

**[[exterior-wall-envelope]]** — The exterior wall system concept that must resist weather and manage moisture, including drainage and flashing integration under Oregon residential code references. Source: oregon-bcd-2021-orsc-significant-changes.

## Common Mistakes

Treating roofing as an isolated subcontract task instead of a GC-level systems coordination milestone.

Starting installation without verifying current code-cycle assumptions or local jurisdiction nuances.

Allowing late penetrations after roofing completion without formal detailing and quality reinspection.

Skipping post-install documentation and relying on memory for final closeout.
