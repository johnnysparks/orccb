## Definition

Roof geometry is the shape-and-measurement framework of a roof system: pitch, span, run, rise, ridge/valley layout, hips, dormers, eaves, and all transitions where planes intersect. For a residential GC, it is both a technical layout concept and a coordination language shared by design, framing, roofing, and inspection teams. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

In practical contractor terms, roof geometry answers three field questions early: **What shape are we building? How does water leave it? Where are the risk transitions?** Source: oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

## Why it matters

Roof geometry affects structural load paths, drainage behavior, material quantities, labor complexity, fall exposure, and the likelihood of leaks at intersections. Errors in geometry interpretation can cascade into framing rework, flashing failures, delayed dry-in, and inspection setbacks. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oar-437-003-subdivision-m-fall-protection.

For contractor competence, this is a high-leverage domain: a GC who reads roof geometry clearly can prevent avoidable conflicts between plans, truss packages, framing layout, and roofing details before crews mobilize. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

## Where it appears in a real project

Roof geometry appears throughout the residential lifecycle:

1. **Design review/preconstruction:** verify plan geometry, roof notes, and transition details.
2. **Estimating/procurement:** convert geometry into takeoff logic (area, slope factor, ridge/valley length, waste allowances).
3. **Framing execution:** lay out rafters/trusses and align ridge/valley/hip relationships.
4. **Weatherproofing and roofing:** detail underlayment/flashing at geometry change points.
5. **Inspection/handoff:** confirm installed geometry matches approved intent and downstream scopes (gutters, penetrations, ventilation) can proceed. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

## Inputs needed

- Approved plans, sections, and roof details.
- Roof dimensions (span, run, ridge lengths, overhangs) and pitch/slope specs.
- Structural assumptions (rafter/truss design package and loading context).
- Climate and exposure assumptions that affect drainage and detailing priorities.
- Product constraints (minimum slopes, manufacturer transition details).
- Site constraints (setbacks, adjoining structures, access staging).
- Safety plan for roof access, edge protection, and fall controls. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oar-437-003-subdivision-m-fall-protection.

## Outputs produced

- Geometrically accurate framing layout plan.
- Takeoff quantities and production assumptions for roofing and accessories.
- Identified critical transitions (valleys, crickets, dormer tie-ins, penetrations, edge conditions).
- Sequenced install plan for dry-in and weatherproofing.
- Inspection-readiness package showing conformance to approved roof intent. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

## Key decisions

1. **Roof form strategy:** simple gable/hip vs. complex multi-plane architecture.
2. **Pitch selection:** balancing appearance, drainage, product compatibility, and constructability.
3. **Transition detailing priority:** which intersections get preplanned high-control details.
4. **Framing method:** site-cut rafters vs. truss-driven geometry (or hybrid).
5. **Drainage routing:** valleys, edge conditions, and runoff management at vulnerable elevations.
6. **Schedule approach:** one-shot completion window vs. phased weather-tight strategy in uncertain weather.
7. **Safety setup:** access and fall-protection controls that match geometry complexity. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-m-fall-protection, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

## Basic workflow

1. **Read geometry from plans deliberately.** Mark every roof plane, pitch, intersection, and elevation change.
2. **Run a geometry sanity check.** Confirm dimensions close, ridges align, and transitions are buildable.
3. **Map risk intersections.** Flag valleys, dead-level pockets, dormer ties, and high-penetration zones.
4. **Coordinate framing package.** Resolve conflicts between plan intent and truss/rafter execution before field installation.
5. **Preplan weatherproofing sequence.** Assign detail ownership and installation order at geometry changes.
6. **Execute with hold points.** Verify critical geometry checkpoints before covering work.
7. **Close out with documentation.** Capture as-built notes/photos for inspection and future service clarity. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

## Risks and edge cases

- **Complex roof intersections:** more leak-prone tie-ins and sequencing errors.
- **Low-slope transitions inside otherwise steep roofs:** hidden drainage vulnerability.
- **Field dimension drift:** truss/rafter mismatch with plan geometry.
- **Late MEP penetrations:** geometry-safe detailing missed after dry-in.
- **Wind-driven rain exposure zones:** geometry that needs stronger transition detailing.
- **Historic/remodel tie-ins:** existing structure out-of-square, requiring geometry adaptation.
- **Safety exposure increase:** steep pitches and broken roof lines raise fall-risk management demands. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-m-fall-protection, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

In Oregon, roof geometry decisions should be confirmed against the currently adopted residential-code framework and local permit/inspection process before production assumptions are locked. Code cycles can change, so a competent GC verifies current adoption status rather than relying on old details. Source: oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

For Ashland projects, geometry complexity often intersects with weather windows, hillside/context constraints, and tight sequencing between framing and roofing crews. The practical standard is to resolve geometry ambiguity in preconstruction and keep a documented transition-detail plan before roof-cover installation starts. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

## Example scenario

A GC in Ashland is building a one-story home with a main gable roof, two dormers, and a garage tie-in that creates a long valley.

- During preconstruction review, the GC identifies that the valley alignment in the framing notes conflicts with a dormer elevation callout.
- The GC resolves the geometry conflict before framing day, updates layout marks, and confirms the truss package assumptions.
- On framing completion, the GC performs a roof-geometry checkpoint walk focusing on valley lines, dormer transitions, and eave continuity.
- Before roofing starts, the superintendent runs a short trade huddle: valley detail order, dormer flashing sequence, and penetration restrictions after dry-in.
- Result: dry-in completes on schedule with no rework at the highest-risk intersections, preserving interior-start dates.

## Checklist

Use this before and during roof production:

- [ ] Roof planes, pitches, and transitions are clearly marked from approved plans.
- [ ] Geometry sanity check completed (dimensions close; intersections buildable).
- [ ] Critical risk intersections flagged and assigned detail owners.
- [ ] Framing package and field layout fully aligned.
- [ ] Dry-in sequence planned for complex transition points.
- [ ] Fall-protection/access plan reviewed against actual roof complexity.
- [ ] Hold-point verification completed before covering critical transitions.
- [ ] Post-install walk verifies geometry-dependent flashing/edge details.
- [ ] As-built notes/photos captured for inspection and closeout.
