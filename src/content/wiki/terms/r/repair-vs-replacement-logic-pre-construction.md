## Definition

**Repair vs replacement logic (pre-construction)** is the structured decision process a residential GC uses before work starts to determine whether an existing component should be repaired, partially rebuilt, or fully replaced.

At study-guide level, this means evaluating safety, code compliance, remaining service life, lifecycle cost, schedule impact, and owner goals before scope, price, and contract language are finalized. Source: oregon-ccb-guide-to-hiring-a-contractor, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

## Why it matters

- It prevents under-scoped bids that become disputes later.
- It reduces callbacks caused by short-term "patch" fixes on failing assemblies.
- It improves schedule reliability by identifying hidden-condition risk early.
- It helps owners understand tradeoffs (lower first cost now vs lower total cost over time).
- It supports defensible documentation if scope changes are later required. Source: oregon-ccb-guide-to-hiring-a-contractor.

For contractor competence, this is a core pre-construction skill because pricing, contract quality, and field outcomes all depend on this early decision logic.

## Where it appears in a real project

- **Initial walkthrough:** first diagnosis of observed deficiencies.
- **Estimating and proposal drafting:** selecting repair allowances vs replacement line items.
- **Permit planning:** determining whether the intended scope triggers permit/inspection implications.
- **Owner alignment meeting:** deciding acceptable performance target and budget ceiling.
- **Final scope lock:** writing clear inclusions/exclusions before contract signature.

## Inputs needed

- Existing-condition data (photos, measurements, moisture readings, age/history if known).
- Access to relevant code pathway and local jurisdiction process.
- Owner priorities (budget, durability, appearance, timeline, occupancy constraints).
- Cost model (labor, material, subcontractor availability, contingency assumptions).
- Risk tolerance (what level of unknowns is acceptable at contract signing).
- Any known permit/inspection requirements tied to the affected system. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

## Outputs produced

- Written recommendation: **repair**, **replace**, or **defer pending destructive verification**.
- Scope narrative with assumptions and exclusions.
- Costed options (good/better/best or repair-vs-replace alternates).
- Preliminary schedule effect statement.
- Documentation package for owner decision (photos, notes, decision log).
- Contract-ready language and change-order triggers if hidden conditions appear.

## Key decisions

1. **Safety and code baseline:** Is repair legally and technically acceptable, or is replacement the safer/compliant path?
2. **Remaining life threshold:** If repaired today, how much useful life is realistically expected?
3. **Cost ratio:** At what percentage of replacement cost does repair stop being economical?
4. **Intrusiveness and occupancy:** Can replacement be staged without unacceptable disruption?
5. **Risk ownership:** Who carries hidden-condition risk in the contract, and how is that explained?
6. **Quality target:** Is the project target "functional stabilization" or "long-term reset"?

## Basic workflow

1. **Triage the condition.** Classify each issue: cosmetic, performance-impacting, or safety/code-critical.
2. **Collect minimum evidence.** Photograph, measure, and document uncertainty zones.
3. **Build options.** Prepare at least two technically valid paths (repair and replacement when feasible).
4. **Compare options consistently.** Use the same criteria: first cost, lifecycle cost, risk, schedule, owner disruption.
5. **Run a permit/process check.** Confirm whether the selected path changes permit needs or inspection sequence.
6. **Align with owner in writing.** Record selected option, assumptions, and consequence of unknowns.
7. **Lock scope and pricing.** Draft contract language that reflects chosen logic and change conditions.
8. **Pre-mobilization re-check.** Confirm no new findings have invalidated the decision before field start.

## Risks and edge cases

- **Hidden damage behind finishes** can invalidate a repair plan after demolition starts.
- **Owner budget pressure** can bias toward repair even when replacement is lower lifecycle risk.
- **Partial replacement interfaces** can create warranty ambiguity between old and new work.
- **Lead/asbestos-era homes** may trigger compliance steps that alter cost and schedule.
- **Insurance-driven scopes** may not match best technical path; documentation quality becomes critical.
- **Availability shocks** (materials, specialty labor) can temporarily flip the economical choice.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- In Oregon, statewide code governance exists, but project execution still depends on current local jurisdiction process (permit intake, inspection logistics, sequencing). Verify process details at project start rather than relying on prior-job memory. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.
- For Ashland-area residential work, pre-construction decisions should account for weather-window constraints, retrofit complexity in older housing stock, and owner occupancy expectations during phased work.
- When owners participate in decisions or portions of work, role clarity and written responsibility boundaries should be explicit before contract finalization. Source: ccb-owner-construction-responsibilities-notice.

## Example scenario

A GC evaluates a 1990s Ashland home with recurring window-area leaks and interior drywall staining.

- **Option A (repair):** localized flashing repair, sealant renewal, selective drywall patching.
- **Option B (replacement):** remove/reinstall affected windows, rebuild surrounding water-management details, then interior restoration.

Pre-construction evidence shows repeated prior patch history and moisture spread beyond visible staining. The GC explains that Option A has lower first cost but higher recurrence risk, while Option B has higher first cost but lower lifecycle risk and clearer warranty boundaries. Owner selects Option B after receiving written comparison, schedule impact, and contingency notes for hidden sheathing damage.

Result: scope is locked before start, pricing includes a defined hidden-condition trigger, and the team avoids a mid-project dispute over why replacement was recommended.

## Checklist

- [ ] Confirm symptom history and gather baseline condition evidence.
- [ ] Separate cosmetic issues from safety/code-critical findings.
- [ ] Build repair and replacement options with explicit assumptions.
- [ ] Compare options using first cost + lifecycle + risk + schedule.
- [ ] Verify permit/process implications of chosen option.
- [ ] Document owner decision and remaining unknowns in writing.
- [ ] Align contract scope, exclusions, and change-order triggers.
- [ ] Reconfirm decision at pre-mobilization meeting.
