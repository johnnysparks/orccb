## Definition

**Right-of-way improvements** are construction activities performed in the public corridor (street, sidewalk, planting strip, alley, curb area, and related public-access zone) to support or restore legal, safe access to a residential project. Typical work includes curb/sidewalk replacement, driveway approach construction, street cuts and patching, utility service tie-ins, traffic-control setup, and restoration to jurisdiction standards.

## Why it matters

- It is often a permit-within-a-permit: a residential project may be approved, but right-of-way work can still require separate authorization and inspections.
- It affects public safety directly because crews and equipment interact with pedestrians, cyclists, vehicles, and adjacent properties.
- It can become a critical path item near closeout; delays in right-of-way approvals can block certificate/occupancy milestones and project turnover.

## Where it appears in a real project

- **Preconstruction:** confirm whether planned utility connections, driveway revisions, or frontage upgrades trigger right-of-way permits.
- **Design and permitting:** submit right-of-way details (grades, slopes, curb return geometry, restoration standards, traffic control notes) to the jurisdiction that administers that corridor.
- **Construction:** install temporary traffic/pedestrian controls, perform excavation/cut/replace work, and request required inspections.
- **Closeout:** complete punch-list corrections in public areas and secure final sign-off from the responsible authority.

## Inputs needed

- Civil/site plan set with property lines, frontage limits, utility crossing points, and proposed elevations.
- Jurisdiction-specific standards for sidewalks, curb ramps, driveway approaches, paving restoration, and inspection sequencing. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.
- Utility company/service provider requirements for new or modified service connections.
- Traffic and pedestrian protection plan appropriate to crew tasks and site conditions. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety.
- Schedule integration with private-site work so access is maintained while public work is open.

## Outputs produced

- Code- and standard-compliant frontage improvements accepted by the jurisdiction.
- Permanent pedestrian/vehicle access that matches approved grades and tie-ins.
- Restored public surfaces (pavement, curb, walk, striping/signage if required) with documented inspection approvals.
- Reduced rework risk from late-stage access conflicts at project handoff.

## Key decisions

1. **Permit strategy:** whether right-of-way work can be phased or must be fully approved before mobilization.
2. **Scope boundary:** exact line between private property scope and public-corridor scope.
3. **Access continuity:** how to keep safe pedestrian routes and site deliveries functioning during excavation and restoration.
4. **Trade packaging:** self-perform vs subcontract specialty crews familiar with municipal standards.
5. **Inspection timing:** when to call for subgrade, form, compaction, paving, and final inspections to avoid tear-out.

## Basic workflow

1. Verify frontage conditions and utility constraints during site due diligence.
2. Confirm jurisdiction ownership/authority over the corridor and pull right-of-way permit requirements early. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.
3. Build the right-of-way scope package (drawings, traffic control notes, restoration details) and align with overall project schedule.
4. Pre-task plan safety and public-interface controls before opening pavement or walk areas. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety.
5. Execute demolition/excavation, utility tie-ins, and replacement work in planned sequence.
6. Request intermediate inspections and correct deficiencies immediately.
7. Complete final restoration, remove temporary controls, and secure final right-of-way sign-off.

## Risks and edge cases

- Jurisdiction mismatch (city vs county vs state corridor) causing permit resubmittals and delays.
- Grade conflicts at property line leading to ponding, trip hazards, or driveway scraping.
- Emergency utility conflicts discovered after saw-cutting/opening the corridor.
- Weather or seasonal paving windows delaying final asphalt/concrete restoration.
- Public-liability exposure if temporary traffic/pedestrian controls are incomplete or poorly maintained.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- In Oregon, statewide building code administration exists, but right-of-way administration commonly sits with the local jurisdiction or road authority, so contractors must verify who controls the corridor before work starts. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.
- For Ashland-area residential projects, right-of-way improvements frequently involve downtown/access-sensitive pedestrian routes, hillside drainage transitions, and coordination with local public works standards. Treat this as early preconstruction work, not a late closeout afterthought.
- Practical exam-prep mindset: know that “building permit approved” does **not** automatically mean “right-of-way work cleared.” Always confirm separate permits, bonds/fees (if required), and inspection sequence with the authority having jurisdiction.

## Example scenario

A GC is building an ADU in Ashland with a new driveway location and upgraded water service. The building permit is approved, but the city requires a separate right-of-way permit for curb cut, sidewalk panel replacement, and pavement patching where the service crosses the street.

The GC holds a preconstruction meeting with utility and flatwork trades, then phases work so pedestrian detours and delivery access remain active. During field layout, the crew catches a slope mismatch between the proposed driveway apron and existing gutter flow line; they revise form elevations before pour day. Inspections pass in sequence, final restoration is accepted, and the project avoids a costly tear-out that would have delayed occupancy.

## Checklist

- [ ] Confirm which agency controls the right-of-way at the project frontage.
- [ ] Verify whether right-of-way work needs a permit separate from the building permit.
- [ ] Align right-of-way scope and schedule with utility tie-ins and private-site access.
- [ ] Prepare and communicate traffic/pedestrian control measures before mobilization.
- [ ] Verify grade tie-ins at property line, curb, and sidewalk transitions before placement.
- [ ] Complete required intermediate and final inspections with documented approvals.
- [ ] Restore disturbed public surfaces to jurisdiction standards before closeout.
