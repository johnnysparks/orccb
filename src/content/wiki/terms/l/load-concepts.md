## Definition

**Load concepts** are the methods used to estimate how much heating, cooling, electrical, and hot-water demand a house will place on its systems. They convert building size, envelope quality, orientation, occupancy, and appliances into design numbers.

## Why it matters

Right-sized load assumptions prevent oversized equipment, short cycling, noise complaints, and wasted money. For a GC, load concepts also protect schedule and budget because downstream scopes (ducts, panels, and equipment pads) depend on early load assumptions.

## Where it appears in a real project

- Concept design and budgeting
- Permit-set coordination with mechanical/electrical scopes
- Equipment submittals and value-engineering conversations
- Commissioning troubleshooting if comfort complaints appear

## Inputs needed

- Floor area, room layout, and orientation
- Envelope assumptions (insulation, windows, air sealing)
- Climate assumptions for Southern Oregon
- Internal gains (occupants, lighting, appliances)
- Utility service constraints and owner preferences

## Outputs produced

- Peak heating and cooling estimates
- Estimated electrical demand profile
- Preliminary hot-water demand assumptions
- Basis-of-design notes for trade partners

## Key decisions

1. What calculation method/detail level is appropriate for project phase.
2. How conservative to be for unknowns in early design.
3. When to lock assumptions versus update during design development.
4. How to document assumptions so bid scope stays comparable.

## Basic workflow

1. Gather plan and envelope inputs.
2. Set climate and occupancy assumptions.
3. Run preliminary load estimates.
4. Review implications with HVAC/electrical/plumbing trade partners.
5. Update loads after major envelope or layout changes.
6. Freeze design loads before final equipment selection.

## Risks and edge cases

- Early assumptions become stale after plan changes.
- Over-conservative assumptions lead to costly oversizing.
- Remodels with unknown existing conditions can skew results.
- Mixed-use spaces (ADUs, home offices) can shift demand profiles.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

Ashland climate swings and smoke-season operation patterns increase the value of realistic seasonal assumptions. Oregon permit reviewers and inspectors may not check full calculations line-by-line, but projects still fail in practice when loads are poorly estimated.

## Example scenario

A 2,100 sq ft Ashland home starts with generic rule-of-thumb sizing. During preconstruction, the team tightens envelope specs and re-runs loads. The revised numbers support smaller equipment and a quieter duct plan, reducing first cost and improving comfort.
