## Summary

Ladder, scaffold, and electrical safety controls are everyday competencies for residential contractors. These hazards are common because workers frequently use temporary access systems, energized tools, and extension power.

This lesson gives a practical pre-task framework so learners can check condition, setup, and separation before work exposure begins.

## Lesson

### Definition
Ladder, scaffold, and electrical safety is the set of planning and field controls that keeps workers stable at elevation and protected from electrical shock, arc, or fire hazards.

### Why it matters
- These hazards are frequent in nearly every trade.
- Many incidents occur during routine “quick tasks.”
- Small setup mistakes can produce severe outcomes.

### Where it appears in a real project
- Exterior siding and trim access.
- Interior finish work at ceilings and upper walls.
- Temporary power distribution and corded-tool use.

### Inputs needed
- Task height and duration.
- Ladder/platform type and load needs.
- Power source locations and condition of cords/tools.
- Ground/weather conditions.

### Outputs produced
- Access-method choice (ladder vs platform vs alternative).
- Setup verification and defect tagging.
- Electrical control plan for temporary power and wet conditions.

### Key decisions
- Whether ladder use is appropriate or a platform is safer.
- Whether equipment defects require immediate removal from service.
- Whether power routing should be changed before work starts.

### Basic workflow
1. Choose the safest access method for the task duration and reach.
2. Inspect ladders/platforms and remove damaged items from use.
3. Establish stable setup and safe access zones.
4. Inspect cords, tools, and temporary power protections.
5. Brief crew on 3-point contact, overreach limits, and electrical no-go zones.
6. Monitor for drift in setup as the day progresses.

### Risks and edge cases
- Uneven grade causing ladder kick-out.
- Improvised platform use beyond design intent.
- Cord damage hidden under materials or through door pinch points.

### Oregon/Ashland relevance
Oregon construction rules provide ladder requirements and broader accident-prevention duties. In Ashland, rain and seasonal moisture can raise electrical risk on mixed indoor/outdoor sites, making cord management and protected power distribution especially important.

### Example scenario
A small crew installs exterior lighting and fascia trim. Morning checks find one ladder with cracked rung hardware and temporary cords crossing a high-traffic path. The lead removes the ladder from service, reroutes power overhead with protection, and assigns a spotter during final fixture wiring near damp surfaces.

### Checklist
- [ ] Correct access method chosen for task and duration.
- [ ] Defective ladders/platform components removed from service.
- [ ] Stable setup and controlled work zone established.
- [ ] Cords/tools inspected and protected from damage/wet exposure.
- [ ] Crew briefed on overreach and electrical no-go practices.

### Quiz questions
1. When is ladder use the wrong choice even if available?
2. What should happen to a ladder with structural defects?
3. Name two common temporary-power hazards on residential sites.

## Audio Script

<!-- Estimated listening time: 8 minutes -->

Most ladder and electrical incidents happen during familiar work. Because the task feels routine, teams skip setup discipline. Your advantage is to make setup non-negotiable.

First, choose access intentionally. If a task is long, high, or requires side reach, a platform may be safer than a ladder. Second, inspect every access item before use. If something is damaged, remove it from service immediately.

Then manage electrical exposure. Check cords, tools, and connection points. Keep power paths out of pinch points and wet zones whenever possible. If conditions change, stop and reset instead of pushing through.

Finally, use short crew reminders: maintain contact and balance, avoid overreach, and respect no-go zones around temporary power. These basics prevent a large share of serious incidents.

## Key Terms

**Ladder safety** — The safe selection, setup, inspection, and use of ladders for construction tasks.

**Jobsite safety** — The coordinated system of hazard controls and worker behaviors that protect people and work operations.

## Common Mistakes

Using ladders for tasks better suited to platforms or repositioned access.

Keeping damaged equipment in circulation “for one more day.”

Ignoring electrical routing details because power setup appears temporary.
