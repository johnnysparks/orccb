## Definition

Demolition and clearing is the controlled removal of existing structures, building elements, vegetation, debris, and surface obstacles so a residential site can safely move into grading, utilities, foundations, and new construction. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors, oar-437-division-3-construction.

For residential general-contractor competence, treat this domain as a planning-and-control phase, not just “tear-out.” It combines scope definition, hazard identification, permitting checks, utility coordination, work-zone control, and material handling/disposal decisions. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety, oar-437-003-subdivision-e-ppe.

## Why it matters

A weak demolition/clearing plan creates chain-reaction failures: injuries, stop-work orders, damaged utilities, disposal violations, neighbor complaints, and foundation-phase delays. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety, oar-437-division-3-construction.

A strong plan reduces rework and protects project margin by making the site “construction-ready” on time and with known constraints documented before excavation crews mobilize. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations, psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

## Where it appears in a real project

Typical sequence in a residential project:
1. Preconstruction due diligence and permit checks.
2. Hazard and utility identification.
3. Selective interior demolition (if remodel/addition) or full structure demolition.
4. Vegetation and surface clearing.
5. Rough site cleanup and disposal verification.
6. Handoff to grading/excavation/foundation teams. Source: oar-437-division-3-construction, oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.

In remodels, demolition and clearing can repeat in phases (for example, interior strip-out first, then later exterior hardscape removal). In new infill builds, it is usually front-loaded before major earthwork. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

## Inputs needed

- Approved project scope, plans, and defined limits of disturbance.
- Jurisdiction permit status and inspection sequencing assumptions.
- Utility locate/verification records and shutoff coordination plan.
- Site safety plan (PPE, access control, competent-person oversight, daily hazard checks).
- Hazard-material screening assumptions (especially lead/asbestos triggers).
- Equipment and hauling plan, including disposal routing and documentation expectations. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety, oar-437-003-subdivision-e-ppe, deq-asbestos-information, oha-lead-contractors-and-painters.

## Outputs produced

- Site cleared to defined limits and ready for next trade.
- Documented debris disposition and cleanup completion.
- Updated site constraints log (what was discovered versus assumed).
- Safety and compliance records supporting continuation of work.
- Handoff package for grading/excavation/foundation sequencing. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations, oar-437-division-3-construction.

## Key decisions

1. **Selective vs. full demolition:** What stays, what goes, and what must be protected in place.
2. **Manual vs. mechanical methods:** Productivity vs. control near adjacent structures and utilities.
3. **Hazard pathway triggers:** Whether material disturbance requires lead/asbestos compliance pathways.
4. **Traffic and neighbor management:** Haul routes, staging, dust/noise timing.
5. **Disposition strategy:** Salvage/reuse/recycle/disposal decisions and associated schedule impacts.
6. **Readiness gate to next phase:** Objective criteria for “cleared and safe to excavate.” Source: deq-asbestos-information, oar-340-248, oha-lead-contractors-and-painters, oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety.

## Basic workflow

1. **Define the demolition and clearing scope.** Mark limits and protection zones.
2. **Confirm legal/safety prerequisites.** Verify permits, controls, and supervision roles.
3. **Coordinate utilities and hazard assumptions.** Resolve unknowns before destructive work.
4. **Execute demolition in planned sequence.** Start with stabilization and isolation, then removal.
5. **Execute clearing operations.** Remove vegetation, debris, and obstructions per scope.
6. **Clean, verify, and document.** Confirm site conditions and capture changes/unknowns discovered.
7. **Release to next trade.** Handoff to grading or foundation with an explicit readiness checklist. Source: oar-437-division-3-construction, oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety, oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.

## Risks and edge cases

- Hidden utilities or undocumented lines discovered after demolition starts.
- Unplanned hazardous-material triggers (for example suspect asbestos-containing material).
- Shared-property boundaries, tight access, or protected adjacent structures.
- Weather windows that convert cleared areas into erosion/mud bottlenecks.
- Over-clearing that removes required vegetation buffers or destabilizes slopes.
- Scope drift from “clear and prep” into unpermitted grading or structural removal. Source: deq-asbestos-information, deq-asbestos-for-businesses, oar-340-248, oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

In Oregon, demolition and clearing competence should include recognizing state-level safety and environmental rule triggers before disturbance work begins, especially for asbestos (DEQ/OAR 340-248) and lead-safe renovation contexts (CCB/OHA pathways). Source: deq-asbestos-information, oar-340-248, oha-lead-contractors-and-painters, oregon-ccb-specialty-licenses-lead-based-paint.

For Ashland-specific execution, verify local permit package expectations, tree/vegetation constraints, haul-route restrictions, and inspection sequencing with the current city process at time of permit intake. State rules set the floor; local process often determines timing risk. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

## Example scenario

A residential GC in Ashland is preparing a lot with an old detached garage and dense overgrowth for a new single-family home.

- The GC first separates work into two scopes: structure demolition and site clearing.
- Before disturbance, the GC confirms utility shutoffs/locates and documents who is the competent person for daily site checks.
- During pre-demolition walkdown, suspect legacy materials are identified; the GC pauses destructive activity in that zone and verifies applicable compliance pathways before continuing.
- Clearing proceeds in mapped zones to keep one clean access corridor for hauling and emergency response.
- At closeout, the GC issues a handoff packet showing area-by-area completion, disposal tracking, and known remaining constraints for excavation. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety, oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations, deq-asbestos-information, oha-lead-contractors-and-painters.

## Checklist

Use this as a pre-excavation gate:

- [ ] Scope boundaries marked and protection zones identified.
- [ ] Permit status and sequence reviewed with superintendent.
- [ ] Utility locate and shutoff confirmations in project file.
- [ ] Site safety controls active (PPE, access control, daily inspections).
- [ ] Hazard-material triggers reviewed before disturbance.
- [ ] Demolition executed to planned sequence; no uncontrolled tear-out.
- [ ] Clearing complete to plan limits; haul/debris staging controlled.
- [ ] Disposal/salvage records captured as required by project controls.
- [ ] Site-readiness walk performed with grading/foundation lead.
- [ ] Open issues logged with owner/PM before phase handoff.

## Quiz questions

1. **Which statement best describes demolition and clearing in residential project delivery?**  
   A) It is mostly a labor activity with minimal planning.  
   B) It is a controlled preconstruction/early-construction phase that sets safety, compliance, and schedule conditions for all downstream work.  
   C) It begins after excavation is complete.  
   D) It only applies to full tear-down projects.

2. **What is the best sequence before mechanical demolition begins?**  
   A) Start removal immediately, then confirm utilities and hazard assumptions.  
   B) Confirm scope limits, safety controls, utility coordination, and hazard triggers first; then execute demolition.  
   C) Clear vegetation first, regardless of access and utilities.  
   D) Wait until framing to document discovered conditions.

3. **A crew discovers suspect hazardous material mid-demolition. What is the best contractor response pattern?**  
   A) Continue work to avoid schedule delay.  
   B) Pause disturbance in the affected zone, verify applicable compliance pathway, then resume with controls.  
   C) Ignore unless an inspector is present.  
   D) Push decision to final cleanup.

4. **What makes a demolition/clearing handoff “construction-ready”?**  
   A) Debris is mostly gone.  
   B) Demolition is finished but no documentation exists.  
   C) Work area is cleared to scope, hazards controlled, constraints logged, and next trade can mobilize with known conditions.  
   D) Owner verbally approves progress.

**Answer key:** 1-B, 2-B, 3-B, 4-C.
