## Definition

**Deck and stair design** is the planning and detailing process for exterior walking surfaces and vertical circulation so they are structurally sound, durable in weather exposure, and usable/safe for occupants.

For residential general-contractor competence, this domain includes both technical detailing (spans, supports, connections, rise/run geometry, guard transitions, drainage, and material compatibility) and management execution (permit scope, inspection timing, subcontractor coordination, and quality control checkpoints).

## Why it matters

- Exterior deck and stair failures are high-consequence because they combine structural loading, moisture exposure, and fall risk in one assembly.
- Stair geometry and guard/handrail transitions are frequent rework points when planning is rushed.
- Deck attachment and water-management details directly affect durability and liability.
- For GC readiness, this domain tests your ability to convert code language and plan intent into field-verifiable details and inspection-ready work.

## Where it appears in a real project

1. **Preconstruction/design development:** define deck use, occupancy assumptions, stair paths, and relationship to doors/grade.
2. **Permit set development:** produce structural and dimensional details for deck framing, stair layout, guards, and drainage strategy.
3. **Framing phase:** build support structure, ledger/connection details, stair framing, landings, and guard blocking.
4. **Exterior finish phase:** install decking surface, guard systems, handrails, and weather-exposed hardware.
5. **Final inspection and turnover:** confirm geometry, safety features, drainage behavior, and closeout documentation.

## Inputs needed

- Approved architectural and structural plans with deck/stair callouts.
- Current Oregon adopted-code references and local jurisdiction review requirements. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption.
- Site conditions: finished grade relationships, drainage flow paths, and existing-structure connection conditions.
- Material selections: framing species/grade, connectors, fasteners, decking, guard system, and corrosion-protection strategy.
- Trade plan and sequencing: framing crew, rail installer, waterproofing/envelope interfaces, inspection windows.
- Safety plan for elevated work and stair access during construction. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-x-stairways-ladders.

## Outputs produced

- Complete layout package for deck footprint, elevations, stair geometry, and guard/handrail transitions.
- Built assembly with verified structural connections and weather-exposure detailing.
- Inspection-ready documentation and correction log.
- Handoff package for owner use and maintenance expectations.

## Key decisions

- **Support strategy:** freestanding vs attached configuration and load path continuity.
- **Connection detailing:** ledger/interface approach, connector specification, and corrosion compatibility.
- **Stair geometry control:** rise/run consistency, landing placement, and headroom/clearance planning.
- **Guard and handrail integration:** resolve transitions early to avoid field improvisation.
- **Drainage and durability approach:** slope/gap strategy, flashing interfaces, and water-shedding at connection points.
- **Inspection sequencing:** where hold points should be set so corrective work is still accessible.

## Basic workflow

1. **Scope and intent review:** confirm deck use case, occupancy assumptions, and design constraints.
2. **Code/jurisdiction check:** verify current Oregon code cycle and local interpretation before detailing. Source: oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption, ors-chapter-455.
3. **Geometry setup:** establish elevations, stair path, landing relationships, and critical clearances.
4. **Structural detailing:** finalize members, supports, connectors, and attachment strategy.
5. **Moisture/durability detailing:** define flashing, drainage, and corrosion-control details for exposed conditions.
6. **Pre-build QA pass:** review dimensions, hardware schedule, and coordination with adjacent envelope/site scopes.
7. **Build and inspect:** execute framing and finish install, then complete jurisdiction inspections.
8. **Closeout:** document corrections, verify safe use conditions, and capture maintenance notes.

## Risks and edge cases

- Deck-to-building interfaces that trap moisture and accelerate hidden decay.
- Stair layouts that fit on plan but conflict with real grade transitions or door swings in field conditions.
- Inconsistent riser heights from cumulative small layout errors.
- Mis-specified fasteners/connectors in corrosive or mixed-metal environments.
- Retrofit projects with unknown existing framing capacity or concealed damage.
- Wildfire-prone areas where material choices and detailing may have additional performance implications (verify jurisdiction requirements).

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon residential projects should be planned against the currently adopted state code framework, then confirmed with the local permitting authority handling the address. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.
- Ashland-area projects commonly involve slopes, drainage sensitivity, and weather exposure patterns that increase coordination pressure on exterior stairs/landings and deck drainage detailing.
- Because code cycles and local administration can change over time, verify the active code edition and local submittal expectations before finalizing your deck/stair detail set. Source: oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption.
- During construction, temporary stair/ladder access and elevated-work controls must still meet Oregon OSHA expectations. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-x-stairways-ladders.

## Example scenario

You are managing a rear-yard deck with a stair run down to a sloped backyard in Ashland.

- Initial concept places stairs directly off the deck corner, but field grade shots show a steeper-than-expected landing transition.
- You pause fabrication and rework the layout: shift the stair run, add an intermediate landing, and adjust guard transitions so handrail continuity is maintained.
- You revise connector and hardware counts for the updated geometry, then run a pre-inspection checklist before covering key connections.
- Result: the project passes inspection with minimal rework, and the owner receives an exterior access path that drains correctly and feels stable under use.
