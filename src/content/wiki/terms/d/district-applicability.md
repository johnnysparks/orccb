## Definition

**District applicability** is the process of confirming which rules, review bodies, and permit pathways apply to a residential project based on the property's exact location and overlays (city/county boundaries, zoning districts, historic areas, wildfire overlays, etc.).

For GC training, it means answering one core question early: **"Which district rules govern this address, and what does that change in scope, schedule, and risk?"**

## Why it matters

- It prevents designing or bidding to the wrong standard.
- It avoids late permit surprises that cause redesign and delay.
- It helps sequence planning review and building permit review correctly.
- It protects trust with owners by surfacing constraints before contract lock.

## Where it appears in a real project

- Intake and feasibility calls.
- Preconstruction due-diligence memo.
- Design kickoff with architect/designer.
- Permit strategy (planning first vs direct building route).
- Change management if parcel assumptions prove incorrect.

## Inputs needed

- Exact site address and parcel identifier.
- GIS and jurisdiction maps for city/county authority boundaries.
- Applicable district/overlay records for the parcel.
- Current Oregon code administration references and local process notes. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.
- Project intent (addition, remodel, ADU, exterior alteration, new build).

## Outputs produced

- A confirmed district applicability summary in the preconstruction file.
- A review-path decision (planning review, building permit, or both).
- Early scope constraints to feed estimating and schedule logic.
- A list of required agency touchpoints and timing assumptions.

## Key decisions

1. Is this parcel governed by city, county, or a split-jurisdiction process?
2. Which overlays materially affect design or construction method?
3. Should the team run a pre-application meeting before design lock?
4. What contingencies belong in budget and schedule for discretionary review risk?

## Basic workflow

1. Confirm legal address and parcel data.
2. Verify jurisdiction authority for plan review and inspections.
3. Identify district/overlay constraints tied to the site.
4. Convert constraints into concrete design and sequencing assumptions.
5. Validate uncertain items with planning/building staff.
6. Update estimate, schedule, and owner decision memo.

## Risks and edge cases

- Parcel sits near a boundary and staff interpretation differs from map assumptions.
- Overlays are discovered late (historic, hillside, wildfire, or tree-related triggers).
- Team confuses zoning/planning applicability with building-code applicability.
- Previous-owner work suggests one path, but current rules require another.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

Oregon uses statewide building code adoption with local administration, so district applicability always has two layers: **state code framework + local process implementation**. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

In Ashland-area projects, district and overlay checks are especially important for hillside lots, visible exterior changes, and planning-related triggers that can affect schedule before permit issuance.

## Example scenario

A GC is asked to estimate an upper-level addition near central Ashland. Early parcel review shows the site is within a district that requires extra exterior compatibility review. Instead of pricing as a standard permit-only job, the GC adds pre-application time, design revision allowance, and owner decision gates. The owner approves a realistic schedule, avoiding a major reset later.

## Checklist

- [ ] Parcel and jurisdiction confirmed.
- [ ] District and overlay applicability documented.
- [ ] Planning-vs-building review path identified.
- [ ] High-risk unknowns sent to agency for written clarification.
- [ ] Estimate and schedule contingencies updated.
- [ ] Owner sign-off captured on assumptions.
