## Definition

**Duct design** is planning duct routes, sizes, fittings, and terminal placement so airflow reaches each room as intended with acceptable noise and pressure loss.

## Why it matters

Even good equipment performs poorly with bad distribution. Duct mistakes can cause hot/cold rooms, noise complaints, and difficult punch-list cycles.

## Where it appears in a real project

- Plan coordination before framing completion
- Rough-in and framing inspections
- Insulation/air-sealing interface
- Startup balancing and final commissioning

## Inputs needed

- Room-by-room load targets
- Framing constraints and chase opportunities
- Equipment airflow capacity
- Register/grille placement constraints
- Accessibility and maintenance needs

## Outputs produced

- Duct routing plan and sizing strategy
- Framing coordination notes for chases/soffits
- Sealing and insulation requirements
- Balancing targets for commissioning

## Key decisions

1. Trunk-and-branch versus radial layout approach.
2. Route priorities: shortest path vs constructability.
3. Duct location relative to conditioned space.
4. Noise mitigation details at critical rooms.

## Basic workflow

1. Convert room loads into airflow targets.
2. Sketch routing alternatives and assess framing conflicts.
3. Select fitting and size strategy to limit pressure loss.
4. Coordinate penetrations and support details.
5. Install, seal, insulate, and test.
6. Balance airflow at startup.

## Risks and edge cases

- Last-minute framing changes force poor reroutes.
- Long runs with many fittings reduce delivered airflow.
- Leaky ducts in unconditioned zones hurt efficiency.
- Historic remodels may have almost no chase space.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

In Ashland, mixed heating/cooling seasonal demands make room-by-room comfort expectations more visible to homeowners. Good duct design is a key risk-control point before drywall.

## Example scenario

A new plan routes a main trunk through a narrow hallway soffit, but lighting and framing conflicts appear. The GC hosts a pre-rough coordination walk, shifts register locations, and preserves airflow targets without rework after drywall.
