## Summary

Hazard identification is the discipline of spotting unsafe conditions and unsafe acts before someone gets hurt, equipment is damaged, or work is stopped. In residential construction, this means actively scanning each phase of work for fall, struck-by, caught-in, electrical, trench, weather, and access hazards.

For Oregon contractor prep, this skill supports both exam success and field competence. It helps you connect broad employer safety duties to daily site planning, toolbox talks, and competent-person inspections.

## Lesson

### Definition
Hazard identification is the structured process of finding, documenting, and prioritizing conditions that can cause injury, illness, property damage, or schedule disruption.

### Why it matters
- Prevents incidents before they happen.
- Reduces rework, delays, and insurance/loss costs.
- Strengthens communication between superintendent, foreman, and trades.

### Where it appears in a real project
- Preconstruction walk of lot conditions and access.
- Daily pre-task planning before framing, roofing, trenching, or demo.
- Change conditions: weather, new subcontractor, equipment move, or design revision.

### Inputs needed
- Scope of work and current task plan.
- Site map, utility info, and permit constraints.
- Crew skill levels and available protective systems.
- Recent near-miss notes and inspection findings.

### Outputs produced
- Pre-task hazard analysis (written or app-based).
- Controls list (eliminate, substitute, engineer, admin, PPE).
- Assigned corrective actions with owner and due time.
- Updated briefing notes for the crew.

### Key decisions
- Which hazards are immediate stop-work issues.
- Which controls are required before work starts.
- Whether a competent person must re-inspect after condition changes.

### Basic workflow
1. Define today’s tasks by location and trade.
2. Walk the area and identify hazards by category.
3. Rate risk (severity × likelihood).
4. Select controls in hierarchy order, then assign owners.
5. Brief crew, verify controls, and start work.
6. Reassess after breaks, weather shifts, or scope changes.

### Risks and edge cases
- “Normalization of deviance” (people get used to repeated unsafe conditions).
- Hidden hazards from overlapping trades.
- New-worker assumptions (crew says they were trained, but method is different).

### Oregon/Ashland relevance
In Oregon, employers have broad duties to provide a safe workplace and comply with Oregon OSHA construction rules. In Ashland projects, tight lots, smoke season, and mixed weather can quickly change jobsite risk conditions and should trigger renewed hazard scans.

### Example scenario
A GC starts a siding replacement project in Ashland. Morning scan finds unstable extension-cord routing across wet ground, a ladder set on uneven soil, and deliveries blocking emergency access. The foreman halts start-up, reroutes temporary power with GFCI protection, resets ladder footing, and clears an access lane before task release.

### Checklist
- [ ] Today’s tasks and locations are clearly defined.
- [ ] Hazard scan completed before work begins.
- [ ] Controls selected and assigned to specific people.
- [ ] Crew briefed on top 3–5 risks for the shift.
- [ ] Re-check triggers identified (weather, new trade, changed scope).

### Quiz questions
1. What should happen first: crew starts work or hazard scan? Why?
2. Name two condition changes that require a new hazard review.
3. Give one engineering control and one administrative control for a fall hazard.

- Visual: “Daily hazard scan” one-page flowchart.
- Diagram: Risk matrix (likelihood vs severity) with example construction hazards.
- Audio: 5-minute “pre-task huddle” script with call-and-response prompts.

## Audio Script

<!-- Estimated listening time: 8 minutes -->

Hazard identification is how a competent contractor stays ahead of problems. Instead of waiting for an incident, you run a structured scan before work begins and again whenever conditions change.

Start by naming the work clearly. Who is doing what task, in which area, and with which tools? Then walk the area and look for category risks: falls, struck-by hazards, caught-in points, electrical exposure, excavation concerns, access issues, and weather factors.

Next, prioritize. Ask which risks can seriously injure someone today, and what control must be in place before work starts. Use the control hierarchy. If you can eliminate the hazard, do that first. If not, add engineering controls, then administrative controls, and finally PPE as the last layer.

Assign each control to a person and a time. If no owner is assigned, the control usually does not happen. Finish with a short crew briefing and confirm everyone knows the stop-work triggers.

In Ashland and similar Oregon settings, weather and smoke can change conditions quickly. A safe morning setup can become unsafe by afternoon. That means hazard identification is continuous, not a one-time form.

## Key Terms

**Competent person** — A person capable of identifying existing and predictable hazards and authorized to take prompt corrective measures.

**Jobsite safety** — The system of duties, controls, inspections, and safe work practices used to protect workers and the public.

## Common Mistakes

Treating hazard identification as paperwork instead of a field process. If the walk is skipped, major hazards are often missed.

Relying only on PPE. PPE helps, but higher-level controls usually reduce risk more effectively.

Failing to reassess after conditions change. New trades, weather, and deliveries can create fresh hazards within hours.
