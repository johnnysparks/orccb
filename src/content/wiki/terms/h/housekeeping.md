## Summary

Housekeeping is a core safety and productivity control, not a cosmetic task. Organized materials, clear access paths, and routine debris removal reduce common incidents and keep work flowing.

For residential GCs, housekeeping quality also influences inspection readiness, subcontractor coordination, and neighbor impact.

## Lesson

### Definition
Housekeeping is the continuous management of debris, materials, tools, cords, and access routes so the site remains safe, efficient, and compliant.

### Why it matters
- Prevents slips, trips, falls, and struck-by incidents.
- Improves emergency access and evacuation reliability.
- Reduces material damage and trade interference.

### Where it appears in a real project
- End-of-day cleanup and next-day setup.
- Work-area turnover between trades.
- Material delivery staging and waste removal.

### Inputs needed
- Daily production plan.
- Waste stream plan (trash, recyclable, hazardous).
- Staging map and access requirements.
- Crew and subcontractor responsibilities.

### Outputs produced
- Clean, walkable access routes.
- Segregated waste and safer storage conditions.
- Improved inspection and client walk-through readiness.

### Key decisions
- Where materials can be staged without blocking egress.
- How often cleanup checkpoints occur during the shift.
- Who owns each zone’s housekeeping standard.

### Basic workflow
1. Assign zones and housekeeping owners.
2. Stage materials and waste containers before tasks begin.
3. Run midday and end-of-shift cleanup checkpoints.
4. Verify egress, access, and fire-risk controls.
5. Document outstanding cleanup actions for next shift.

### Risks and edge cases
- Mixed-trade congestion causing blocked paths.
- Wind or rain spreading loose debris into public areas.
- Temporary power cords becoming trip hazards as layout changes.

### Oregon/Ashland relevance
Oregon’s general safety framework supports maintaining safe work areas and access conditions. In Ashland neighborhoods, tight site boundaries and wildfire-season sensitivity increase the importance of debris, dust, and ignition-source control.

### Example scenario
A remodel crew working on a narrow lot accumulates offcuts near the primary egress route. Afternoon walkthrough catches the blockage before inspection prep. The superintendent reassigns cleanup zones, repositions bins, and adds a midday housekeeping check to prevent repeat congestion.

### Checklist
- [ ] Walkways and egress paths remain clear.
- [ ] Materials are stacked/stored safely.
- [ ] Debris and waste are removed at defined intervals.
- [ ] Temporary cords/hoses are controlled.
- [ ] Zone owners are assigned and accountable.

### Quiz questions
1. Why is housekeeping part of safety, not just appearance?
2. What is one sign that staging and housekeeping are misaligned?
3. How can weather create new housekeeping hazards?

## Audio Script

<!-- Estimated listening time: 6 minutes -->

Housekeeping is one of the simplest and most reliable safety controls on a residential jobsite. When access is clear and materials are organized, crews make fewer mistakes and move faster.

Start by assigning ownership by zone. If everyone owns housekeeping, usually no one owns it. Then align staging with work flow so tools, materials, and waste containers are where they are needed.

Use scheduled checkpoints, not just end-of-day cleanup. Mid-shift correction catches hazards before they compound. Focus on egress routes, trip hazards, debris accumulation, and ignition risks.

In dense neighborhoods, housekeeping also protects public safety and contractor reputation. Clean sites communicate control and professionalism.

## Key Terms

**Jobsite safety** — Coordinated field practices, including housekeeping, that reduce injury and disruption risk.

## Common Mistakes

Waiting until day-end to clean, allowing hazards to persist for hours.

Stacking materials in access paths during “temporary” pauses.

Failing to assign cleanup ownership by area and shift.
