## Definition

Finish specifications are written requirements for visible materials and installation quality (paint, flooring, tile, trim, cabinetry, hardware, fixtures), including product standard, prep, tolerances, and acceptance criteria.

## Why it matters

Vague finish scope causes owner dissatisfaction, budget overruns, and dispute risk. Clear specs make bids comparable and punch lists shorter.

## Where it appears in a real project

- Bid packages and subcontract scopes.
- Selection tracking and allowance management.
- Mockups and quality-control walkthroughs.
- Punch and closeout acceptance.

## Inputs needed

- Design intent and quality tier targets.
- Product selections or allowance assumptions.
- Manufacturer installation requirements.
- Substrate readiness criteria and schedule constraints.

## Outputs produced

- Room-by-room finish schedule/spec.
- Allowance vs. fixed-spec list.
- QA acceptance checklist by finish type.

## Key decisions

1. Standard product baseline vs. alternates.
2. Where allowances are acceptable and how they are capped.
3. Mockup requirements for high-visibility finishes.
4. Acceptance tolerances and warranty expectations.

## Basic workflow

1. Define finish scope by room/zone.
2. Write performance and quality criteria.
3. Separate fixed selections from allowances.
4. Align procurement and installation sequencing.
5. Verify substrate readiness before installation.
6. Perform punch using explicit acceptance criteria.

## Risks and edge cases

- “Equivalent” substitutions without written criteria.
- Moisture-incompatible finish choices in wet zones.
- Late owner upgrades that disrupt sequence.
- Incomplete mockups leading to rework.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

In Oregon residential contracting, clear written scope and change handling are central to reducing disputes. Ashland remodels especially benefit from explicit finish standards because existing substrates can vary and affect quality outcomes.

## Example scenario

A learner-GC changes a vague “premium tile” note into a full finish spec with tile size, grout type, substrate prep, and layout pattern. Bid variance narrows, and punch issues drop.

## Checklist

- [ ] Room-by-room finish schedule complete.
- [ ] Fixed selections separated from allowances.
- [ ] Installation and prep requirements included.
- [ ] Substitution rules documented.
- [ ] Mockups approved where needed.
- [ ] Punch checklist tied to acceptance criteria.

## Quiz questions

1. The best way to reduce finish disputes is to:  
   A) Keep notes broad and flexible.  
   B) Define specific quality and acceptance criteria in writing.  
   C) Avoid mockups entirely.  
   D) Delay decisions to punch.

2. Which item should be clearly labeled in finish specs?  
   A) Allowance-based selections.  
   B) Weather forecast.  
   C) Payroll tax rates.  
   D) Bond forms.

3. A common finish risk is:  
   A) Substrate readiness ignored before install.  
   B) Mockups reviewed early.  
   C) Written substitution criteria.  
   D) Room-by-room schedules.

**Answer key:** 1-B, 2-A, 3-A.
