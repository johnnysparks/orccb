## Summary

Fall and excavation hazards are two of the highest-consequence risks on residential projects. Falls can occur during roof, framing, and opening-edge work, while excavation incidents can involve cave-ins, access failures, and utility strikes.

This lesson combines both domains into one field-ready planning model so learners can identify triggers quickly, choose controls, and run safer work sequencing.

## Lesson

### Definition
Fall and excavation safety is the coordinated system of planning, controls, inspections, and supervision used to prevent falls from elevation and collapse-related injuries in trenches or excavations.

### Why it matters
- Severe injury and fatality potential is high in both domains.
- One project often includes both risks at once (foundation, utility trenching, roof work).
- Mistakes usually occur when crews rush sequencing.

### Where it appears in a real project
- Foundation phase, utility trenches, retaining walls.
- Framing/roofing and work near unprotected edges.
- Remodels with temporary openings and below-grade service work.

### Inputs needed
- Site logistics and phasing plan.
- Depth and soil assumptions for excavation tasks.
- Access and egress layout.
- Fall exposure locations and planned guard/anchor systems.

### Outputs produced
- Daily combined risk plan (height + trench risks).
- Inspection logs by competent person.
- Task release decision (go/no-go) for each area.

### Key decisions
- Whether controls are complete before exposure begins.
- Whether trench conditions require protective systems immediately.
- Whether simultaneous activities should be separated by schedule.

### Basic workflow
1. Map all elevation and excavation tasks for the day.
2. Verify competent-person inspection status.
3. Confirm fall controls, trench access, and protective systems.
4. Separate conflicting activities where possible.
5. Brief crew on stop-work triggers and rescue communications.
6. Re-inspect after rain, vibration, or visible condition changes.

### Risks and edge cases
- Rain-softened soil after morning inspection.
- Temporary cover removal at floor openings.
- Equipment operating too close to trench edges.

### Oregon/Ashland relevance
Oregon OSHA has detailed construction rules for fall protection and excavation controls. Ashland’s seasonal rains and heat cycles can alter ground conditions and worker fatigue, so conservative re-inspection practices are important.

### Example scenario
During an ADU build, a crew is trenching for utilities while another crew prepares roof sheathing. The superintendent pauses roof edge work until guardrail corrections are completed and shifts excavation spoil farther from the trench edge. A competent person re-inspects trench walls after midday rain before trench entry resumes.

### Checklist
- [ ] Fall exposures identified and protected before work starts.
- [ ] Trench egress and protective systems confirmed.
- [ ] Competent-person inspections documented.
- [ ] Equipment, spoil piles, and material loads kept clear of trench edge risk zones.
- [ ] Re-inspection triggers communicated to all crews.

### Quiz questions
1. Why is a morning trench inspection alone often insufficient?
2. What sequencing conflict can occur when roof and trench crews work at once?
3. Give one stop-work trigger for fall risk and one for excavation risk.

## Audio Script

<!-- Estimated listening time: 9 minutes -->

When you combine fall and excavation work on one site, planning quality becomes the difference between control and chaos. Both hazards can escalate quickly, and both demand clear triggers.

Begin with visibility. List every task that puts workers at height and every task that places workers in or near excavations. Then verify who is responsible for inspections and release authority.

For falls, check edge protection, access systems, and worker readiness before exposure. For excavations, check trench condition, egress, soil behavior, nearby loads, and equipment movement. Confirm protective measures are in place before entry.

Now manage interference. If one crew’s operations can destabilize another crew’s risk controls, separate their tasks by time or area. Good sequencing is a safety control.

Finally, set re-inspection triggers. Rain, vibration, visible cracking, cover removal, and layout changes all justify reassessment. The safest teams treat inspections as continuous decisions, not a checkbox.

## Key Terms

**Fall protection** — Controls and training practices used to prevent workers from falling from elevations and openings.

**Excavation safety** — Inspection and protective-system practices used to prevent cave-ins and related below-grade hazards.

**Competent person** — A qualified and authorized individual who can identify hazards and require immediate corrections.

## Common Mistakes

Assuming one plan covers all day conditions. Weather and site traffic can invalidate the plan quickly.

Treating trench and roof work as independent when they share supervision and logistics constraints.

Allowing production pressure to override no-go decisions when controls are incomplete.
