## Definition

**Foundation/floor/roof systems** are the three major structural layers that create a house’s vertical stack:

- **Foundation system:** transfers loads into soil.
- **Floor system:** distributes loads and provides working platforms between levels.
- **Roof system:** spans and sheds weather while delivering loads down into walls and supports.

A competent GC treats these as one coordinated system, not three separate trades.

## Why it matters

- Sequencing errors between these systems create rework and inspection failures.
- Tolerance drift at each stage compounds into framing, finish, and envelope problems.
- Good coordination here improves cost control, schedule reliability, and durability.

## Where it appears in a real project

1. **Preconstruction:** selecting structural approach and sequencing.
2. **Foundation install:** footings, stem walls/slab, anchors, moisture and drainage prep.
3. **Floor framing:** beams/joists/subfloor with alignment to load paths.
4. **Roof framing and dry-in:** trusses/rafters, sheathing, weatherproofing handoff.
5. **Inspection milestones:** foundation, framing, and final structural confirmations.

## Inputs needed

- Approved plans and structural details.
- Current Oregon adopted code resources and jurisdiction process. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption.
- Site data (grade, drainage, soil assumptions, exposure).
- Material lead times (concrete, framing package, trusses/engineered products).
- Crew/inspection schedule with hold points.

## Outputs produced

- Constructed and aligned foundation-to-roof structural stack.
- Verified interfaces between concrete, framing, and roofing scopes.
- Inspection-ready work packages at each milestone.
- Reduced downstream conflicts for MEP and finish trades.

## Key decisions

- Foundation type and coordination with drainage/site conditions.
- Floor framing method and service-routing strategy.
- Roof geometry/framing strategy and weatherproofing sequencing.
- Tolerance control method (benchmarking and as-built checks between stages).

## Basic workflow

1. Confirm system choices and sequencing in preconstruction meeting.
2. Build/verify foundation geometry and anchor interfaces.
3. Install floor framing and validate level/square before progressing.
4. Frame roof system and complete sheathing/dry-in transitions.
5. Run system-interface QC (foundation-to-floor, floor-to-wall, wall-to-roof).
6. Complete required inspections and correction closeout.

## Risks and edge cases

- Foundation out-of-square creating framing drift.
- Floor deflection/vibration concerns from span misapplication.
- Roof framing conflicts at valleys/dormers/intersections.
- Weather exposure between framing and dry-in causing material damage.
- Remodel tie-ins where existing framing conditions are unknown.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon code-cycle updates require current-reference checks before final decisions. Source: oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption.
- Ashland-area slope and drainage conditions can strongly affect foundation strategy and exterior interface detailing.
- Seasonal weather windows increase the value of clean roof dry-in sequencing and inspection timing.

## Example scenario

On a sloped lot, survey shots show slight variance from original assumptions.

You adjust foundation benchmark controls, verify floor system elevations before wall production, and re-sequence roof framing so dry-in happens immediately after sheathing.

Result: the project avoids cumulative geometry errors and stays inspection-ready at each stage.
