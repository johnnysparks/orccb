## Definition

Framing is the construction phase where the building’s structural skeleton is laid out and assembled so loads can transfer safely from roof and floors to the foundation.

For residential general-contractor preparation, framing includes both technical execution (layout, sequencing, connections, and bracing) and management execution (inspection timing, subcontractor coordination, and code-check workflow).

## Why it matters

- Framing sets structural reliability, geometric accuracy, and downstream trade fit.
- Early framing errors often cascade into schedule delays, change orders, and expensive rework.
- In Oregon, framing work sits inside a statewide code framework, so field decisions should align with the currently adopted code edition and local inspection administration process. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.

## Where it appears in a real project

In a typical residential sequence, framing follows excavation/foundation work and precedes most major rough-in and enclosure steps:

1. Foundation cured and anchor/interface conditions verified.
2. Floor framing and subfloor platform.
3. Wall framing, openings, temporary bracing.
4. Upper floor/roof framing.
5. Structural sheathing and dry-in preparation.
6. Framing inspection(s), then MEP rough-in progression.

(Sequence is study guidance; project-specific phasing can vary.)

## Inputs needed

- Approved plans/specs and scope assumptions.
- Current code references and amendment checks for the applicable Oregon cycle. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption.
- Jurisdiction process details (inspection authority, submission expectations, and scheduling constraints). Source: oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.
- Foundation as-built verification (dimensions, elevations, anchor locations, squareness).
- Material package (lumber/engineered components, connectors, fasteners, sheathing).
- Crew plan, safety controls, and lift/logistics plan.

## Outputs produced

- Complete structural frame per plans and approved revisions.
- Framing punch list and corrected deficiencies.
- Inspection-ready package (field notes, any required engineered details, and schedule confirmation).
- Handoff conditions for roof, weatherproofing, and MEP rough-in activities.

## Key decisions

- **Layout control approach:** how to establish datums, benchmark checks, and squareness tolerances.
- **Sequencing strategy:** panelized vs stick-framed workflow, crane windows, and critical-path impacts.
- **Connection detailing oversight:** when to escalate ambiguous details to design professionals before installation.
- **Inspection strategy:** batch vs rolling inspections, including reinspection risk planning.
- **Risk allocation:** where to add contingency for material lead times, weather exposure, and crew availability.

## Basic workflow

1. **Pre-framing review:** verify plans, scope, and latest code cycle references. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption.
2. **Site/layout controls:** confirm foundation geometry and establish control lines.
3. **Floor and wall assembly:** complete framing with temporary stability measures.
4. **Roof/upper framing:** install structural members and bracing per design intent.
5. **Alignment and correction pass:** check plumb/level/square and resolve deviations.
6. **Inspection prep:** organize documentation and schedule authority review.
7. **Release to next trades:** coordinate transition requirements with MEP and enclosure teams.

## Risks and edge cases

- Out-of-square foundations causing compounded framing misalignment.
- Field conflicts between architectural intent and structural practicality.
- Connector/fastener substitutions without approval.
- Weather exposure before dry-in, affecting materials and schedule.
- Reinspection delays due to incomplete correction tracking.
- Wildfire/urban-interface or site-specific constraints that change detailing expectations in some jurisdictions (verify locally before execution).

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon operates under a statewide specialty code system and adopted-code publication model, so framing decisions should be validated against currently effective code text rather than memory. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.
- ORSC adoption updates are ongoing by cycle; study and field planning should include a code-cycle/date check before finalizing framing assumptions. Source: oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption.
- Local program administration can vary by jurisdiction under Oregon’s program framework; in Ashland-area work, confirm which authority is handling plan review/inspection for the specific address before scheduling critical framing inspections. Source: oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.

## Example scenario

You are managing a single-family build near Ashland. Foundation dimensions are within tolerance on two sides but diagonal measurements show slight racking.

Step-by-step, you:
1. Pause wall layout on affected lines.
2. Re-establish control lines from a consistent benchmark.
3. Compare framing plan geometry to as-built foundation dimensions.
4. Decide whether to absorb minor variance at non-critical locations or escalate for design clarification.
5. Document corrective layout decisions before crew restart.
6. Update inspection sequencing so the framing review occurs after all corrections are complete.

Result: you protect schedule predictability, reduce reinspection risk, and preserve downstream fit for roof and MEP scopes.
