## Definition

**Ventilation** is the intentional movement of outdoor air into and stale indoor air out of a home, using passive or mechanical strategies to maintain healthy indoor conditions.

## Why it matters

As homes get tighter, uncontrolled leakage no longer guarantees enough fresh air. Planned ventilation supports health, moisture control, and odor management while reducing callback risk.

## Where it appears in a real project

- Mechanical design and permit documentation
- Framing and rough-in for fan/duct pathways
- Commissioning and balancing
- Owner orientation (operation modes and maintenance)

## Inputs needed

- Occupancy assumptions and bedroom count
- Envelope airtightness target
- Climate and smoke-season concerns
- Noise tolerance and energy priorities
- Local code and inspection expectations

## Outputs produced

- Ventilation strategy (exhaust-only, supply, balanced)
- Equipment and duct layout requirements
- Controls sequences and user settings
- Commissioning checklist and owner instructions

## Key decisions

1. Which ventilation strategy fits the house and budget.
2. Whether filtration and smoke resilience require upgraded components.
3. How to integrate ventilation controls with heating/cooling controls.
4. How to balance simplicity for owners with performance goals.

## Basic workflow

1. Define required airflow targets and operating modes.
2. Select ventilation approach and major equipment.
3. Coordinate routing, penetrations, and noise control details.
4. Install and verify airflow performance.
5. Train owner on normal and special-event operation.

## Risks and edge cases

- Under-ventilation in tight homes causes IAQ complaints.
- Over-ventilation raises energy bills and comfort complaints.
- Poor control logic leads to systems being disabled by occupants.
- Wildfire smoke periods require adaptive operating guidance.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

Ashland projects often need seasonal operation strategies: normal fresh-air operation versus smoke-event modes. Oregon code adoption cycles can change ventilation details, so teams should verify the active cycle at permit start.

## Example scenario

A remodel adds air sealing and new windows. Occupants report stuffiness after completion. The GC adds a controlled ventilation plan with clear owner settings and maintenance instructions, resolving complaints.
