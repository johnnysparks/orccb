## Definition

**Mechanical/HVAC** in residential construction is the planning, installation, startup, and verification of heating, cooling, ventilation, and related controls so the home is safe, code-compliant, and comfortable. In contractor study terms, it spans system selection, permit/inspection workflow, coordination with other trades, and final owner handoff. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, ors-chapter-455, psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

## Why it matters

A mechanically sound house protects occupant health (fresh air and moisture control), reduces callbacks, and lowers operating cost for the owner over the life of the home.

For a residential general contractor, HVAC decisions also affect schedule risk, insulation strategy, framing clearances, electrical loads, and permit closeout timing. Source: oregon-ccb-contractor-tools, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

## Where it appears in a real project

- **Preconstruction:** early load assumptions, equipment location planning, and scope definition.
- **Design/permit set:** mechanical layouts and any required permit documents.
- **Rough-in:** ducts, refrigerant line routing, venting, and equipment support/clearances.
- **Insulation and air-sealing phase:** balancing enclosure tightness with required ventilation.
- **Startup/commissioning:** functional checks, airflow setup, controls verification.
- **Final inspection and turnover:** permit final, owner orientation, and maintenance expectations.

## Inputs needed

- Project scope (new build vs remodel and performance goals).
- Current adopted Oregon code path and local jurisdiction requirements.
- Floor plans, envelope assumptions, and space constraints.
- Trade partner capabilities (HVAC, electrical, framing coordination).
- Owner priorities (comfort, noise, indoor air quality, first cost vs lifecycle cost).
- Permit and inspection timeline targets.

## Outputs produced

- HVAC scope package and trade coordination notes.
- Permit-ready mechanical documentation required by jurisdiction.
- Installed and tested HVAC/ventilation system.
- Inspection records and final approvals.
- Owner handoff items (basic operation and maintenance guidance).

## Key decisions

1. **System type and distribution strategy** (for example, ducted vs ductless approach).
2. **Equipment location** for serviceability, noise, and constructability.
3. **Ventilation approach** to match envelope performance and occupancy use.
4. **Sequencing choices** so rough-in and inspections do not stall other trades.
5. **Documentation depth** needed to reduce ambiguity and change-order risk.

## Basic workflow

1. Confirm current Oregon code cycle and local permitting expectations before detailing scope.
2. Align owner goals with a preliminary HVAC concept and budget range.
3. Coordinate framing, electrical, and HVAC space claims early (chases, clearances, equipment pads).
4. Lock rough-in sequencing milestones and inspection hold points in the master schedule.
5. Install and verify system components per approved documents and manufacturer requirements.
6. Complete startup checks and corrections before requesting final sign-off.
7. Close out permits and perform owner walkthrough focused on filter changes, controls, and service intervals.

## Risks and edge cases

- **Undersized/oversized equipment:** causes comfort complaints, cycling problems, and avoidable energy use.
- **Late coordination:** can force framing rework or duct reroutes.
- **Tight-envelope mismatch:** inadequate ventilation planning can lead to indoor air quality or moisture issues.
- **Wildfire smoke events:** filtration and fresh-air strategy become more critical during regional smoke periods.
- **Remodel constraints:** existing framing and legacy systems may limit ideal routing and equipment placement.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon uses an adopted statewide code framework, with cycle updates that contractors must re-check before relying on old details. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption.
- Mechanical work ties directly into permit and inspection process discipline; Oregon contractor workflows should confirm jurisdiction-specific steps at project start. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-ccb-contractor-tools.
- In Ashland and similar Southern Oregon climates, design choices should account for seasonal temperature swings and smoke-season resilience as part of owner-value planning (comfort + IAQ + maintainability).
- Owner/contractor paperwork and permit roles should be explicit, especially when projects involve owner participation or mixed-scope permit activity. Source: ccb-owner-construction-responsibilities-notice.

## Example scenario

A GC in Ashland is building a 1,900 sq ft single-family home on a tight lot.

The first HVAC plan placed equipment in a location that conflicted with framing and created difficult service access. During preconstruction coordination, the GC moved equipment location, clarified duct routing zones, and added a schedule checkpoint before insulation.

At startup, the team found one airflow imbalance, corrected it before final inspection, and completed owner training on controls and filter maintenance. The project closed with fewer punch items because HVAC coordination was treated as a full project-management domain, not just a subcontractor handoff.
