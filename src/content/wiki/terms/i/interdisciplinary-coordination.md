## Definition

Interdisciplinary coordination is the process of aligning architecture, structure, MEP, envelope, and finishes so the work can be built in sequence without clashes.

## Why it matters

Most field delays come from handoff and interface failures between trades, not individual trade incompetence. Strong coordination protects schedule, budget, and quality.

## Where it appears in a real project

- Preconstruction constructability reviews.
- Weekly look-ahead meetings and pull-plan sessions.
- RFI/submittal turnaround and revision management.
- Hold-point inspections and turnover between scopes.

## Inputs needed

- Current drawing set and revision log.
- Trade scopes and exclusions.
- Project schedule with milestone dependencies.
- Site constraints and access plan.

## Outputs produced

- Clash/risk log with owner and due date.
- Coordinated look-ahead plans.
- Decision register for unresolved issues.
- Reduced rework and smoother inspections.

## Key decisions

1. Which interfaces need formal hold points.
2. Who owns each coordination risk and by when.
3. When to escalate unresolved RFIs.
4. Sequence adjustments required by lead times or weather.

## Basic workflow

1. Run interdisciplinary drawing review before mobilization.
2. Build an interface matrix (trade A to trade B handoff).
3. Hold weekly coordination meetings with action logs.
4. Verify in field at each major turnover.
5. Close lessons learned into next-phase planning.

## Risks and edge cases

- Unclear scope boundaries (“I thought they were doing it”).
- Late design revisions not reaching all trades.
- Schedule compression that removes needed hold points.
- Multi-prime communication gaps on small projects.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

Ashland projects often combine constrained sites, weather windows, and remodel complexity. Interdisciplinary routines help smaller residential teams maintain commercial-grade coordination discipline.

## Example scenario

A trainee superintendent builds an interface matrix for framing, HVAC, and plumbing in a tight attic zone. Weekly coordination catches duct conflicts before rough-in inspection, preventing demolition and resequencing.

## Checklist

- [ ] Interface matrix complete.
- [ ] Weekly coordination cadence established.
- [ ] Action log with owners/dates maintained.
- [ ] Revision updates distributed to all trades.
- [ ] Turnover hold points performed and documented.

## Quiz questions

1. Interdisciplinary coordination primarily prevents:  
   A) Trade licensing requirements.  
   B) Interface clashes and sequencing failures.  
   C) Weather events.  
   D) Permit fees.

2. Which tool is most helpful for handoffs?  
   A) Interface matrix with ownership.  
   B) Verbal reminders only.  
   C) Untracked group texts.  
   D) End-of-project debrief only.

3. A frequent coordination edge case is:  
   A) Immediate RFI resolution on every issue.  
   B) Late revisions not reaching all trades.  
   C) Too many documented hold points.  
   D) Excess clarity in scope language.

**Answer key:** 1-B, 2-A, 3-B.
