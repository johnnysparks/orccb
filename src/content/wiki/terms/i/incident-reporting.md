## Summary

Incident reporting turns events and near misses into actionable learning. Without clear reporting, the same hazards are likely to repeat under slightly different conditions.

For residential GCs, strong reporting supports worker protection, legal readiness, insurer communication, and better operational control.

## Lesson

### Definition
Incident reporting is the timely capture, communication, and analysis of unsafe events, injuries, property damage, and near misses.

### Why it matters
- Supports immediate care, scene control, and hazard correction.
- Creates traceable records for internal and external review.
- Reveals recurring patterns that need system-level fixes.

### Where it appears in a real project
- Minor injury and first-aid events.
- Near misses involving falls, electrical exposure, or struck-by risks.
- Property damage involving tools, vehicles, or neighboring property.

### Inputs needed
- Event facts: who, what, when, where.
- Photos, witness statements, and environmental conditions.
- Task plan and controls in place at time of event.
- Supervisor and competent-person observations.

### Outputs produced
- Incident report and immediate corrective-action log.
- Root-cause notes and prevention actions.
- Follow-up training or process changes.

### Key decisions
- Whether work should stop in affected area.
- Which hazards must be corrected before restart.
- Which stakeholders need immediate notification.

### Basic workflow
1. Stabilize scene and provide care.
2. Preserve facts and collect initial evidence.
3. Complete report while details are fresh.
4. Identify immediate and root contributors.
5. Assign corrective actions with deadlines.
6. Verify closure and share lessons learned.

### Risks and edge cases
- Underreporting near misses because “no one got hurt.”
- Late reporting that weakens factual accuracy.
- Blame-focused language that suppresses future reporting.

### Oregon/Ashland relevance
Oregon’s safety framework emphasizes prevention and compliance with construction safety rules. Ashland projects often involve close neighbor interfaces, so reporting property-impact events promptly can reduce conflict and claims escalation.

### Example scenario
A worker trips over a temporary cord and narrowly avoids a fall down steps. No injury occurs, but the superintendent records a near miss, documents routing failure, and updates cord-control standards across all active sites. The next toolbox talk uses photos from the event.

### Checklist
- [ ] Incident/near miss reported promptly.
- [ ] Facts captured clearly (who/what/when/where).
- [ ] Scene conditions documented with photos/notes.
- [ ] Corrective actions assigned and tracked.
- [ ] Lessons shared with relevant crews.

### Quiz questions
1. Why should near misses be reported even without injury?

## Audio Script

<!-- Estimated listening time: 6 minutes -->

Incident reporting is not just paperwork after something goes wrong. It is the mechanism that converts one event into better prevention across the company.

First, stabilize the situation and protect people. Then capture facts quickly while memory is fresh. Keep language objective: describe what was observed, not who to blame.

Near misses matter. They are early-warning signals. If you ignore them, the next version may include an injury. Good reporting includes immediate fixes and deeper follow-up so recurring patterns are removed, not just patched.

Close the loop by sharing lessons with crews and confirming corrective actions were completed. Reporting only works when it changes future behavior.

## Key Terms

**Jobsite safety** — Ongoing practices that include reporting, investigation, and corrective action to prevent repeat incidents.

## Common Mistakes

Treating near misses as unimportant because no injury occurred.

Delaying reports until details are forgotten.

Writing blame-focused reports that discourage honest future reporting.
