## Definition

**Inspections (Ashland specific)** is the practical skill of planning, sequencing, and closing required residential permit inspections for projects in Ashland while staying aligned with Oregon's statewide code framework and the local authority's current process rules. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.

For residential general contractor competence, this is not only a code topic. It is also a production-control topic: you must know when to call inspections, how to prepare each stage, how to close corrections quickly, and how to protect downstream schedule commitments. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors, oregon-ccb-contractor-tools.

## Why it matters

- Inspection failures or late scheduling can stop work and create trade stacking.
- Missed rough-ins can force expensive demolition and rework after close-in.
- Inspection discipline is one of the clearest markers of GC readiness because it combines compliance, sequencing, documentation, and subcontractor coordination.
- In Ashland-area work, local administrative steps can change over time, so a contractor must verify current local intake/scheduling mechanics instead of relying on old habits. Source: oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.

## Where it appears in a real project

Inspection control appears across almost every phase:

1. **Preconstruction:** verify permit path, authority, and required inspection sequence.
2. **Site and foundation:** schedule footing/foundation-related milestones as required.
3. **Framing and rough-in:** coordinate framing, plumbing, mechanical, and electrical rough inspections before insulation/drywall close-up.
4. **Insulation and close-in:** confirm inspection sign-off before concealment.
5. **Final stage:** run final inspection(s), close corrections, and complete permit closeout package.

## Inputs needed

- Approved permit plans and scope documents.
- Current Oregon code-cycle reference habits (never assume from memory). Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.
- Confirmed authority having jurisdiction for the address and current local process details. Source: oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.
- Trade-ready field status (work actually complete at each requested stage).
- Internal quality-control checklist before inspector arrival.
- Responsible party for correction tracking and reinspection requests.

## Outputs produced

- Passed stage inspections documented in project records.
- Correction notices closed with evidence and dates.
- Updated schedule with released successor tasks.
- Permit closeout-ready file (inspection history, approvals, key sign-offs).
- Reduced rework risk and cleaner owner turnover.

## Key decisions

- **Authority check:** Who is the active permitting/inspection authority for this exact Ashland address?
- **Inspection batching vs rolling calls:** Request larger combined inspections or more frequent stage calls?
- **Correction strategy:** Can corrections be closed same day/next day, and who owns each action?
- **Close-in protection:** What must be physically verified before insulation and drywall start?
- **Schedule buffering:** How much contingency is needed around long-lead inspection windows?

## Basic workflow

1. **Confirm authority and sequence:** At job startup, verify local process and required inspection milestones.
2. **Build an inspection map:** Attach inspection gates to the project schedule and trade scopes.
3. **Pre-inspection QC:** Run internal checks before every inspection request.
4. **Request and coordinate:** Submit inspection requests with complete, accurate scope notes.
5. **Host inspection professionally:** Ensure safe access, plans on site, and the right lead person present.
6. **Close corrections fast:** Document every correction, assign owners, and schedule reinspection immediately.
7. **Archive and hand off:** Keep inspection records current and release next activities only after confirmed approvals.

## Risks and edge cases

- Inspector arrives but work is incomplete or inaccessible.
- Rough work covered before required inspection approval.
- Scope drift causes inspection mismatch with approved permit set.
- Repeated corrections from poor pre-inspection QC.
- Ambiguous responsibility between GC and subcontractor for correction ownership.
- Jurisdiction process changes mid-project (forms, scheduling portal, lead-time changes).

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon provides statewide code governance and permit/inspection legal framework. Source: ors-chapter-455.
- Oregon BCD maintains adopted-code resources that should be checked as part of current-cycle verification habits. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.
- Municipal inspection administration runs through local jurisdiction programs under Oregon's framework; practical Ashland performance depends on verifying current local process mechanics for the specific project address. Source: oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.
- CCB contractor guidance reinforces that permit responsibility and compliance discipline are core contractor duties, not optional admin tasks. Source: oregon-ccb-contractor-tools.

## Example scenario

You are managing a remodel plus small addition in Ashland.

- **Week 1:** You map all required inspection gates before demo and rough trade mobilization.
- **Week 4:** Framing finishes one day late. Instead of pushing insulation, you keep the framing/rough inspection gate intact and resequence non-dependent work.
- **Week 5:** Inspection returns one correction on vent routing. You assign ownership the same day, verify correction with photos and field check, and request reinspection immediately.
- **Week 6:** Rough approvals are complete, insulation proceeds without reopen work, and drywall start remains controlled.

Result: the project stays compliant and avoids a common failure mode (covering work before approvals), which protects both schedule and margin.

## Quiz questions

1. **Which decision best protects a project from expensive close-in rework?**
   - A) Start insulation once rough work looks mostly complete
   - B) Wait for passed required rough inspections before concealment
   - C) Skip interior QC and rely on inspector feedback
   - D) Delay all inspections until the final phase
   - **Answer: B**

2. **For Ashland projects, what is the most important startup inspection habit?**
   - A) Reuse the same process from the last job without checking
   - B) Confirm the active local authority process and required sequence for the specific address
   - C) Let each trade choose when to request inspections
   - D) Ask for final inspection date first, then backfill rough steps
   - **Answer: B**

3. **What is the strongest correction-management practice after a failed inspection item?**
   - A) Wait until multiple corrections accumulate
   - B) Assign owner/date, verify fix, and request reinspection quickly
   - C) Hide corrected areas and discuss at final
   - D) Move to finish work and revisit later
   - **Answer: B**
