## Definition

**Weather/neighbor planning in site management** is the process of planning field operations so weather impacts and nearby-property impacts are anticipated, communicated, and controlled before they become schedule, safety, or quality failures. In residential GC practice, this includes weather-window sequencing, temporary protection, access/logistics timing, dust/noise controls, and neighbor-facing communication standards. Source: nascla-contractors-guide-or-2e, oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety.

## Why it matters

- Weather can change safety conditions, inspection timing, and workmanship outcomes in a single day.
- Neighbor impacts (noise, debris, blocked access, drainage runoff, parking conflicts) can escalate into complaints, stop-work pressure, or owner dissatisfaction.
- Good site management protects schedule float by preplanning “if weather shifts” and “if neighbor concerns rise” responses.
- This domain is where contractor professionalism is visible: planning quality, communication quality, and daily control quality.

Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors, nascla-contractors-guide-or-2e, oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety.

## Where it appears in a real project

1. **Preconstruction planning:** identify seasonal weather risk, access limits, and sensitive neighbor interfaces.
2. **Mobilization:** set traffic flow, staging limits, material cover strategy, and first-week communication plan.
3. **High-exposure phases:** excavation, concrete, framing dry-in, roofing, exterior finishes, and site drainage transitions.
4. **Daily production control:** morning weather check, trade resequencing calls, and neighbor-impact checks.
5. **Closeout period:** restore site conditions, remove temporary protections, and resolve lingering neighborhood concerns.

## Inputs needed

- Approved plans, permit conditions, and jurisdiction requirements.
- Baseline schedule with weather-sensitive activities highlighted.
- Local short-range forecast and severe-weather triggers (study guidance: use at least 48- to 72-hour planning windows).
- Site logistics map: deliveries, parking, trash routes, pedestrian conflict points.
- Neighbor context: driveway locations, school/work-hour sensitivities, shared-access constraints.
- Safety and environmental controls: runoff, debris containment, fall-risk controls, housekeeping standards.

Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety, nascla-contractors-guide-or-2e.

## Outputs produced

- A practical weather/neighbor site plan (often one-page) tied to the look-ahead schedule.
- Daily control decisions: proceed, resequence, protect, or pause.
- Communication records: owner updates, subcontractor directives, and neighbor notices when needed.
- Fewer surprise delays, fewer complaint escalations, and cleaner handoffs between phases.

## Key decisions

- **Go/no-go thresholds:** what weather conditions trigger pause vs modified work.
- **Resequencing strategy:** what indoor or low-exposure tasks can replace weather-exposed work.
- **Protection level:** when to add temporary cover, drainage controls, or overnight stabilization.
- **Communication depth:** when standard updates are enough vs when proactive neighbor outreach is required.
- **Escalation timing:** when a concern moves from field issue to owner/jurisdiction issue.

## Basic workflow

1. Build a two-week look-ahead and mark weather-sensitive activities.
2. Define trigger points (rain, wind, freeze, smoke, access conflict) with specific field actions.
3. Align trades on fallback tasks before the week starts.
4. Confirm temporary protections and housekeeping controls each morning.
5. Run work; reassess at midday if forecast or site conditions change.
6. Document decisions, especially resequencing and communication actions.
7. Close each day with next-day weather/neighbor risk preview.

## Risks and edge cases

- Rapid forecast changes after crews are mobilized.
- Overnight rain that creates morning slip/mud/runoff hazards.
- Wind events that make lifts, roofing, or exterior handling unsafe.
- Repeated early-start noise in dense neighborhoods causing complaint escalation.
- Shared-driveway or tight-lot deliveries blocking access for neighbors or emergency vehicles.
- “Minor” runoff or debris crossing property lines and creating claims risk.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon residential projects are commonly affected by rain-window scheduling, so temporary protection and resequencing discipline are core GC skills (study guidance).
- Ashland projects may face tighter lot logistics, slope/drainage complexity, and seasonal smoke/air-quality disruptions; these conditions increase the value of a written trigger-based site plan (study guidance).
- Safety duties remain active in changing weather/site conditions; supervisors should treat weather shifts as active safety-management events, not just schedule events. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety.
- Jurisdiction and permit context still controls what can proceed and when; always verify local conditions tied to approved permits and inspections. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

## Example scenario

A residential remodel in Ashland is entering exterior siding and site-improvement work during a week with mixed rain and wind forecasts.

- The GC flags siding, exterior paint prep, and driveway concrete as weather-sensitive.
- A trigger plan is set: if rain probability exceeds threshold by 5:00 AM, exterior painting shifts to interior punch and documentation tasks.
- Neighbor notices are sent for two concrete-truck windows, with alternate parking guidance.
- Midweek, forecasted wind increases; ladder-dependent exterior tasks are paused and material staging is resecured.
- Result: the project loses one exterior production day but avoids quality defects, safety incidents, and complaint escalation.

## Checklist

- [ ] Mark weather-sensitive tasks in the look-ahead schedule.
- [ ] Define go/no-go thresholds and fallback tasks for each exposed activity.
- [ ] Confirm morning forecast and midday reassessment time.
- [ ] Verify temporary protections (cover, drainage, access safety).
- [ ] Confirm housekeeping and boundary controls (dust/debris/runoff).
- [ ] Communicate expected noise/delivery windows to owner and affected neighbors.
- [ ] Record resequencing decisions and responsible parties.
- [ ] Verify end-of-day stabilization for overnight weather.
- [ ] Log any complaint and response action within 24 hours.
- [ ] Rebaseline next-day plan before crew release.
