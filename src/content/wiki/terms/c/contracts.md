## Lesson

### Exam framing: formation + compliance

You still need the core contract concepts — [[offer]], [[acceptance]], and [[consideration]] — but Oregon exam items frequently layer in CCB-specific compliance duties. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors, ccb-contractor-tools.

A practical way to read scenarios: first decide whether formation/remedy doctrine is being tested, then check whether Oregon statutory contractor rules add extra requirements. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors, ors-701-305.

### Essential elements of a contract

A valid and enforceable contract generally requires:

1. **Offer** — definite proposed terms.
2. **Acceptance** — agreement to those terms without modification.
3. **Consideration** — legal value exchanged by each side.
4. **Mutual assent** — both parties agree to the same bargain.

A contract for an unlawful purpose is not enforceable even if these elements are present. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

### Written-contract trigger for residential work

Under ORS 701.305, covered residential work generally requires a written contract once aggregate contract price exceeds $2,000. Source: ors-701-305.

If a project starts at or below $2,000 and later exceeds that amount, the contractor must deliver or mail the written contract within five days after the contractor knew or reasonably should have known the threshold was exceeded. Source: ors-701-305.

When this framework applies, CCB guidance also pairs contract duties with required consumer notices and proof-retention expectations. Source: ccb-contractor-tools, ccb-required-notices-chart.

See [[written-contract-threshold]].

### Cancellation windows: one day vs three days

For an initial written residential contract under ORS 701.310, a property owner generally has until midnight of the next business day to cancel, subject to statutory exceptions. Source: ors-701-310.

For qualifying [[home-solicitation-sale]] transactions under ORS 83.720 and ORS 83.730, cancellation is generally available through midnight of the third business day, subject to statutory exceptions and notice conditions. Source: ors-83-720, ors-83-730.

This next-business-day versus third-business-day distinction is a common exam trap. Classify the scenario first, then apply the matching statute. Source: ors-701-310, ors-83-720, ccb-contractor-tools.

See [[right-of-rescission]].

### Contract terms and complaint rights

ORS 701.315 bars contract terms that waive or limit a person's right to file a complaint with the CCB, even if mediation or arbitration provisions are included. Source: ors-701-315.

### Consumer notices and records

ORS 701.330 provides statutory authority for required residential consumer notices and related procedures. Source: ors-701-330.

CCB Contractor Tools and the Required Notices Chart state that for qualifying residential contracts over $2,000, required notices must be delivered and proof of delivery must be retained for two years. Source: ccb-contractor-tools, ccb-required-notices-chart.

## Common Mistakes

Using one cancellation deadline for all scenarios. ORS 701.310 and ORS 83.720 do not use the same timeline. Source: ors-701-310, ors-83-720.

Assuming no written contract duties apply when a job starts below $2,000. The five-day catch-up rule can apply once aggregate price rises above the threshold. Source: ors-701-305.

Including language that waives CCB complaint rights. ORS 701.315 prohibits that waiver. Source: ors-701-315.

Failing to keep proof of required notice delivery. CCB guidance and the Required Notices Chart call out two-year proof-retention expectations for qualifying projects. Source: ccb-contractor-tools, ccb-required-notices-chart.
