## Definition

**Contextual infill** is the practice of designing and building new residential units or additions on constrained lots so they fit neighborhood context while meeting code, access, and buildability requirements.

For GC competence, contextual infill means balancing three forces at once: **approval risk, construction practicality, and owner outcomes**.

## Why it matters

- Infill projects often fail from planning friction, not just field execution issues.
- Tight lots magnify sequencing, access, and neighbor-impact risk.
- Good contextual planning reduces redesign cycles and permit churn.
- It improves cost realism by exposing site constraints before contract lock.

## Where it appears in a real project

- Feasibility for ADUs and small-lot homes.
- Site planning and massing alternatives.
- Neighbor communication and staging strategy.
- Permit and review sequencing.
- Construction logistics in constrained access conditions.

## Inputs needed

- Parcel geometry, access, and topographic constraints.
- District applicability and compatibility criteria.
- Existing neighborhood context references (height, scale, setbacks, frontage rhythm).
- Jurisdiction/process confirmation under Oregon's local administration model. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.
- Current building-code reference habits and permit resource checks. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

## Outputs produced

- Infill feasibility memo with site and approval assumptions.
- Preferred concept option with constructability notes.
- Risk-adjusted schedule and budget baseline.
- Logistics plan for access, staging, and neighbor-impact mitigation.

## Key decisions

1. Which concept best balances neighborhood fit and construction efficiency?
2. Is early survey/geotech investment justified before design lock?
3. What staging approach protects safety and minimizes neighbor disruption?
4. Which approvals are critical-path and need early agency engagement?

## Basic workflow

1. Run parcel and district applicability checks.
2. Produce two or more massing/site options.
3. Score options for compatibility, cost, logistics, and schedule risk.
4. Validate high-risk assumptions with agencies and key consultants.
5. Select preferred option and create contingency-backed baseline.
6. Carry contextual constraints into permit documents and field plan.

## Risks and edge cases

- Lot constraints make code-compliant design technically possible but hard to build safely.
- Neighborhood compatibility concerns lead to repeated design revisions.
- Access limitations increase labor time and reduce subcontractor bid quality.
- Utility relocations or easement conflicts collapse preferred concept late.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

Contextual infill in Oregon requires combining statewide code understanding with precise local process management. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

In Ashland-area neighborhoods, hillside conditions, mature vegetation, and visible streetscape transitions can raise both compatibility and logistics complexity, making upfront contextual-infill planning a major success factor.

## Example scenario

A GC is hired to build an ADU behind an existing home on a narrow Ashland lot. Two concepts are developed: one maximizes floor area but creates difficult access and stronger compatibility concerns; the other is slightly smaller but preserves staging flow and reduces review risk. The GC helps the owner choose the second option, producing a faster approval path and fewer field disruptions.

## Checklist

- [ ] Parcel and district constraints documented.
- [ ] At least two infill concepts compared with consistent criteria.
- [ ] Compatibility and trigger risks scored before final concept selection.
- [ ] Access/staging plan drafted for constrained-site execution.
- [ ] Budget and schedule contingencies aligned with unresolved risks.
- [ ] Owner approval captured on preferred concept and tradeoffs.
