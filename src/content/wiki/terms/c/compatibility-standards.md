## Definition

**Compatibility standards** are local design or planning criteria used to evaluate whether proposed residential work fits surrounding neighborhood context (scale, massing, roof form, setback rhythm, facade treatment, garage dominance, and streetscape pattern).

For contractor competence, compatibility standards are a **scope-control topic**, not only a design topic: they influence what can be approved, built, and scheduled.

## Why it matters

- A non-compatible design may trigger denial, redesign, or conditions of approval.
- Early compatibility awareness improves estimating and owner expectation management.
- It reduces churn between design intent and permit reality.
- It helps GCs coordinate with designers on constructible alternatives before submittal.

## Where it appears in a real project

- Concept design review.
- Planning/pre-application conversations.
- Exterior material and massing decisions.
- Permit revisions after comments.
- Owner-driven value engineering (where form changes can retrigger review).

## Inputs needed

- Applicable district rules and local compatibility criteria.
- Existing neighborhood pattern context (photos, nearby built examples).
- Preliminary elevations, site placement, and massing studies.
- Planning and permitting pathway confirmation. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.
- Current statewide/local code context references. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

## Outputs produced

- Compatibility compliance notes attached to design package.
- Pre-submittal risk list (what is likely to draw comments).
- Revision strategy options that preserve constructability and budget.
- Better owner briefing on approval risk and schedule implications.

## Key decisions

1. Which compatibility criteria are hard constraints vs negotiable preferences?
2. Should the team adjust building form now or proceed and defend current concept?
3. Which design features can be simplified without harming compatibility outcomes?
4. When should value engineering be paused to avoid re-triggering compatibility concerns?

## Basic workflow

1. Confirm district and review criteria for the exact site.
2. Build a compatibility checklist tied to concept drawings.
3. Identify highest-risk elements (height perception, garage presence, facade articulation).
4. Review options with owner and designer using cost/schedule impact.
5. Submit with a clear narrative showing how criteria are addressed.
6. Track comments, revise deliberately, and recheck constructability before resubmittal.

## Risks and edge cases

- Owners request late form changes after compatibility alignment.
- Nearby comparables are inconsistent, making interpretation subjective.
- Budget cuts remove design elements that were helping compatibility.
- Review comments conflict across stakeholders and require compromise design moves.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

In Oregon, building code compliance alone does not always satisfy local design compatibility expectations for exterior-facing work; contractors must account for local review reality in preconstruction planning. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

In Ashland-area neighborhoods, compatibility can be a major schedule driver for visible additions or infill, so GCs should carry revision contingencies early rather than assuming one-pass approval.

## Example scenario

A GC supports a small-lot infill house where the initial design has a front-loaded garage and taller apparent street facade than adjacent homes. Compatibility review flags massing concerns. The team revises roof lines, entry emphasis, and garage presentation while preserving floor area target. Approval is achieved with less schedule loss because the GC pre-priced two design alternatives.

## Checklist

- [ ] Compatibility criteria identified and summarized.
- [ ] High-risk exterior elements flagged before submittal.
- [ ] At least one lower-risk design alternate developed.
- [ ] Owner briefed on approval uncertainty and timeline impact.
- [ ] Revision budget/schedule contingency included.
- [ ] Final design checked for constructability after compatibility edits.
