## Definition

**Contract compliance** is the discipline of building and administering residential contracts so they satisfy Oregon-required content, notice, cancellation, and non-waiver expectations. Source: ors-701-305, ors-701-310, ors-701-315.

## Why it matters

A technically strong scope can still fail if contract process controls are weak.

## Where it appears in a real project

It appears at initial agreement, scope changes, notice delivery, payment milestones, and project-close communication.

## Inputs needed

- Current contract template.
- Required notices and timing rules.
- Change-order procedures.
- Responsible reviewer before signature.

## Outputs produced

- Compliant signed agreements.
- Traceable change history.
- Reduced complaint exposure tied to paperwork defects.

## Key decisions

- Threshold-based written contract handling.
- Which changes require formal written updates.
- How to verify required notice delivery each time.

## Basic workflow

1. Classify project and contract requirements.
2. Prepare agreement with required elements.
3. Deliver required notices and capture proof.
4. Execute and store signed documents.
5. Manage changes through controlled change orders.

## Risks and edge cases

- Using old templates without current notice language.
- Verbal scope additions that bypass written change control.
- Contract clauses that conflict with complaint-right protections.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

Ashland residential clients often expect clear paper trails. Strong contract compliance supports trust and decreases friction when schedules or budget assumptions shift.

## Example scenario

During a remodel, hidden framing damage expands scope. The contractor issues a written change order and updates timeline/cost terms before proceeding, preserving compliance and reducing dispute risk.

## Checklist
