## Definition

**Connections** are the fastener-and-hardware interfaces that transfer loads between structural components (for example: joist to beam, wall to floor, roof to wall, wall to foundation).

In GC practice, “connections” means selecting the right connector, using the exact listed fasteners, and installing in the sequence that preserves load-path intent.

## Why it matters

- Structural capacity often fails at joints first, not in the middle of members.
- Connection errors are frequent inspection misses and expensive to fix after concealment.
- Good connection control improves safety, quality, and schedule predictability.

## Where it appears in a real project

1. Foundation anchors and sill/plate interfaces.
2. Floor framing hangers, straps, and beam-seat conditions.
3. Wall and shear boundary hardware.
4. Roof uplift ties and diaphragm edge details.
5. Exterior assemblies where corrosion/compatibility matters.

## Inputs needed

- Structural details and connector schedule from permit set.
- Manufacturer installation data for each connector family.
- Fastener specifications (type, size, count, spacing).
- Site exposure/corrosion assumptions.
- Inspection hold-point plan.

## Outputs produced

- Installed joints that match design intent and listed capacities.
- Fewer corrections for missing/wrong fasteners.
- Documented QC evidence before concealment.
- Improved structural reliability under gravity and lateral demand.

## Key decisions

- Connector model selection when multiple options appear similar.
- Fastener compatibility (do not substitute based on appearance).
- Sequence of install so hardware remains accessible and inspectable.
- Corrosion-protection level for exposed or treated-material conditions.

## Basic workflow

1. Build a connection schedule from the approved detail set.
2. Pre-stage connector/fastener kits by area to reduce substitution risk.
3. Install per detail and manufacturer instructions.
4. Run targeted QC: presence, orientation, fastener count/type/spacing.
5. Photograph high-risk concealed conditions.
6. Call inspection before cover-up; close comments immediately.

## Risks and edge cases

- “Near equivalent” fastener substitutions that void capacity assumptions.
- Missing edge blocking causing incomplete load transfer.
- Mixed-metal corrosion problems at exterior connections.
- Retrofit tie-ins to unknown existing material conditions.
- Congested framing making full nailing patterns difficult without re-sequencing.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon projects should align with currently adopted code and local inspection expectations; do not rely on older habits. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption.
- Ashland weather exposure and hillside conditions can increase connector durability and access challenges.
- Early coordination with inspectors on visibility/hold points helps avoid destructive verification later.

## Example scenario

During pre-inspection QC, a lead finds several hangers installed with incorrect fasteners because packaging was mixed on site.

The team halts concealment, replaces all incorrect fasteners, documents corrected areas, and updates staging controls to keep connector kits separated by zone.

Result: inspection passes and repeat errors decline on later phases.

## Checklist

- [ ] Connection schedule extracted from approved details.
- [ ] Connector models and fasteners verified before installation.
- [ ] Installer sequence preserves accessibility for QC/inspection.
- [ ] Fastener count/type/spacing spot-checked by zone.
- [ ] Corrosion compatibility reviewed for exposure conditions.
- [ ] High-risk concealed connections photo-documented.
