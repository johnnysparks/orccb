## Definition

Scope gap analysis is the process of comparing what the project **needs** against what the contract documents, estimate, and trade scopes **actually include**, so missing work can be found before it causes cost or schedule surprises.

For residential GC competence, this is a preconstruction control habit: identify omissions early, assign ownership, and document how each gap will be covered.

## Why it matters

- Scope gaps are a top cause of change-order conflict, budget overruns, and owner trust breakdown.
- Residential projects have many handoffs (GC ↔ subs ↔ suppliers ↔ owner selections), and each handoff can hide an omitted task.
- On Oregon exam-style business-practice questions, gap control supports correct estimate-to-contract workflow and cleaner contract administration decisions. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors, nascla-contractors-guide-or-2e.
- If scope growth pushes total residential price over Oregon's written-contract trigger, contract documentation duties become critical. Source: ors-701-305.

## Where it appears in a real project

- During takeoff and estimate assembly.
- During bid leveling and subcontractor scope-sheet review.
- During plan/spec updates and addenda.
- At preconstruction handoff from sales/estimating to project management.
- Before each milestone where responsibility boundaries shift (sitework to foundation, framing to MEP rough-in, rough-in to finishes).

## Inputs needed

- Current plans, specifications, and addenda set.
- Written estimate assumptions and exclusions log.
- Proposed subcontractor scope sheets and quote clarifications.
- Owner-facing selections list (allowances, alternates, undecided finishes).
- Baseline schedule and long-lead procurement list.
- Contract/legal thresholds and notice requirements for residential work in Oregon. Source: ors-701-305, oregon-ccb-guide-to-hiring-a-contractor.

## Outputs produced

- A gap register (missing scope items, owner, due date, resolution path).
- Clarified inclusions/exclusions matrix by trade.
- Updated estimate and contingency rationale.
- Change-order candidates (if needed) before work is impacted.
- Cleaner subcontract package scopes and handoff notes.

## Key decisions

- **Include now vs carry as allowance:** decide what can be priced confidently today.
- **Who owns each interface:** assign one accountable party for each boundary condition.
- **When to escalate ambiguity:** trigger design clarification before field start.
- **How to communicate owner-impact items:** timing of pricing updates, approvals, and documentation.
- **Whether to re-baseline schedule/cost:** if resolved gaps alter critical path or budget.

## Basic workflow

1. **Build a scope map** from plans/specs by trade and phase.
2. **Cross-check estimate lines** against required deliverables and interfaces.
3. **Review exclusions and assumptions** for hidden dependencies.
4. **Run a trade handoff check** (who provides, installs, coordinates, and commissions each item).
5. **Log every gap** in a register with owner/date/action.
6. **Resolve and document** via estimate revision, contract clarification, or formal change order.
7. **Reconfirm compliance triggers** (including written-contract and notice obligations if price/scope changed). Source: ors-701-305, oregon-ccb-contractor-tools.
8. **Issue updated scope package** to field and subcontractors.

## Risks and edge cases

- Owner selections not finalized before procurement deadlines.
- Drawings/specs conflict (e.g., plan shows one product level, spec calls another).
- Utility/service-provider work assumed by contractor but actually owner- or agency-led.
- Permit conditions adding required tasks after estimate lock.
- Small “not-included” items compounding into major budget drift.
- Fast-track starts where field work begins before complete design information.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon residential contract administration is sensitive to documentation quality as scope evolves; keep estimate-to-contract records aligned when scope changes materially. Source: ors-701-305, oregon-ccb-guide-to-hiring-a-contractor.
- Oregon CCB training/exam framing emphasizes business-practice competence, and scope-gap control directly supports that competence in project scenarios. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.
- Ashland projects can carry added coordination pressure from hillside conditions, wildfire-context detailing, and jurisdiction process sequencing; practical GC habit is to run explicit interface checks before each phase handoff.

## Example scenario

A residential GC in Ashland prices a remodel/addition with a new deck and exterior stair replacement.

During scope gap analysis, the team finds three omissions:
1. Temporary weather protection during phased demolition.
2. Finish-grade/drainage restoration at disturbed access paths.
3. Owner-allowance overage risk on exterior door hardware and wildfire-resilience upgrades.

The GC logs each gap, assigns ownership, updates estimate assumptions, and issues a documented scope clarification before mobilization. One item becomes an approved pre-start change order, two items are folded into subcontract scope sheets, and schedule float is adjusted for procurement.

Result: fewer mid-project disputes, cleaner billing logic, and better owner confidence.

## Checklist

- [ ] Confirm latest plan/spec/addenda set is the one being priced.
- [ ] Build a trade-by-trade scope matrix (include/install/coordinate/test).
- [ ] Compare estimate assumptions to each trade quote exclusion list.
- [ ] Flag owner-selection unknowns and assign decision deadlines.
- [ ] Identify every interface handoff and assign one accountable owner.
- [ ] Log scope gaps with due date and resolution status.
- [ ] Update contract/CO documentation if scope changes price or obligations.
- [ ] Re-issue revised scopes to PM, superintendent, and affected subs.

## Quiz questions

1. **What is the main goal of scope gap analysis?**
   - A) Reduce permitting fees
   - B) Find missing scope before it causes cost/schedule disruption
   - C) Replace all subcontract agreements
   - D) Eliminate all project risk
   - **Answer: B**

2. **Which input is most critical for detecting hidden omissions early?**
   - A) Prior year's tax return
   - B) Marketing brochure for the subdivision
   - C) Current plans/specs/addenda plus quote exclusions
   - D) Paint color schedule only
   - **Answer: C**

3. **In Oregon residential work, why should scope changes be tracked carefully in writing?**
   - A) Only to improve internal jobsite morale
   - B) Because scope and price changes can activate contract-documentation duties
   - C) So subcontractors can skip coordination meetings
   - D) To avoid all inspections
   - **Answer: B**
