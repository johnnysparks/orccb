## Definition

Space planning is arranging rooms, circulation, storage, fixtures, and clearances so a home works for daily life and can be built efficiently.

## Why it matters

Good space plans reduce expensive late changes. They improve owner satisfaction, simplify trade routing, and lower conflict between structure, MEP, and finish scope.

## Where it appears in a real project

- Early floor plan iterations.
- Plan review for door swings, clearances, and furniture zones.
- Coordination of plumbing walls, duct routes, and electrical device placement.
- Finish and cabinetry layout decisions.

## Inputs needed

- Owner lifestyle priorities and accessibility goals.
- Site and envelope constraints.
- Structural grid and bearing assumptions.
- Rough MEP zone requirements.
- Budget and schedule constraints.

## Outputs produced

- Dimensioned plan with workable circulation.
- Conflict log for tight areas (bathrooms, kitchens, stairs, utility rooms).
- Trade coordination notes for rough-in.

## Key decisions

1. Room adjacencies and circulation paths.
2. Minimum practical clearances for daily use and maintenance.
3. Built-in vs. flexible furniture strategies.
4. Early allocation of shafts/chases and equipment zones.

## Basic workflow

1. Build a bubble diagram from owner priorities.
2. Convert to dimensioned plan options.
3. Run a clearance and door-swing clash check.
4. Overlay MEP rough zones and revise.
5. Confirm buildability with framing and finish scopes.

## Risks and edge cases

- Beautiful plans with poor furniture usability.
- Door and appliance conflicts found after framing.
- Under-sized utility/maintenance access.
- Additions that create awkward circulation with existing rooms.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

Ashland remodels often involve constrained footprints, hillside geometry, and older homes with irregular framing. Early space-planning checks help avoid permit corrections and field improvisation.

## Example scenario

A GC trainee reviews a small-bath remodel and catches a vanity-door conflict with required circulation. The plan is adjusted before permit, avoiding a change order during finish carpentry.

## Checklist

- [ ] Lifestyle priorities captured.
- [ ] Circulation paths tested.
- [ ] Clearances/door swings checked.
- [ ] MEP rough zones overlaid.
- [ ] Maintenance access confirmed.

## Quiz questions

1. Which step prevents many layout rework costs?  
   A) Delay clearance checks until punch list.  
   B) Run clash checks before permit and framing.  
   C) Ignore MEP zones until drywall.  
   D) Freeze plan before owner review.

2. Space planning primarily affects:  
   A) Only paint colors.  
   B) Usability, coordination, and constructability.  
   C) Tax filing deadlines.  
   D) Bond premium calculations.

3. A frequent edge case in additions is:  
   A) Perfect circulation by default.  
   B) Awkward transitions between old and new rooms.  
   C) No effect on schedule.  
   D) Elimination of rough-in conflicts.

**Answer key:** 1-B, 2-B, 3-B.
