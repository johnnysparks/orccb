## Definition

**Shear** (in residential framing) is the wall and diaphragm system that resists lateral loads (wind and earthquake) and transfers those loads to the foundation.

**Hold downs** are specific hardware/anchor assemblies (usually at wall ends) that resist overturning uplift forces created when shear walls are loaded laterally.

In plain language: shear walls keep the house from racking sideways, and hold downs keep critical wall ends from lifting.

## Why it matters

- Life safety: proper lateral design helps prevent major structural damage or collapse in seismic and wind events.
- Code compliance: lateral-force-resisting systems are required by adopted residential code paths.
- Rework risk: missing hold downs, wrong nailing, or poor anchor placement are common inspection failures.
- Cost/schedule control: early planning avoids expensive retrofits after framing or foundation inspection.

## Where it appears in a real project

You will see shear/hold-down decisions at multiple phases:

1. **Plan/design phase**: determine prescriptive vs engineered lateral system.
2. **Foundation phase**: place anchor bolts/embedded anchors at required locations.
3. **Framing phase**: build designated shear wall segments and install sheathing/nailing pattern.
4. **Hardware phase**: install hold downs, straps, and tie elements with approved fasteners.
5. **Inspection phase**: verify layout, fastening, edge distances, and manufacturer installation requirements.

## Inputs needed

- Structural drawings or prescriptive code tables used by the project.
- Site and load assumptions (seismic/wind exposure, story height, geometry).
- Wall line layout and openings (garage doors, large windows, offsets, etc.).
- Foundation details and anchor/bolt placement plan.
- Approved hardware schedule (manufacturer model numbers, fasteners, capacities).
- Inspection sequence and any jurisdiction-specific submittal requirements.

## Outputs produced

- Defined lateral-force load path from roof/floor diaphragms to foundation.
- Installed and documented shear wall locations and lengths.
- Installed hold-down hardware at required boundary elements.
- Inspection-ready framing package with visible verification points.
- Reduced risk of field corrections and structural call-backs.

## Key decisions

- **Prescriptive vs engineered**: Is the building within prescriptive limits, or does it require engineering?
- **Wall allocation**: Which wall lines and segments are assigned as shear walls?
- **Opening strategy**: How will large openings be handled (portal frames, alternate bracing methods, engineered elements)?
- **Hardware selection**: Which hold down models/capacities fit required loads and framing members?
- **Connection compatibility**: Are anchors, washers, studs/posts, and fasteners compatible and installable in sequence?
- **Inspection timing**: When will each connection be visible and verifiable?

## Basic workflow

1. **Read the structural intent**
   - Confirm design path (prescriptive/engineered).
   - Identify each required shear wall line and hold-down location.
2. **Pre-layout in field**
   - Mark foundation and wall plates with shear segment boundaries.
   - Confirm anchor locations align with studs/posts and hardware clearances.
3. **Frame boundary elements**
   - Install required studs/posts/chords at segment ends.
   - Maintain required minimum segment dimensions.
4. **Install sheathing and nailing**
   - Match panel orientation, edge support, and nailing schedule exactly.
   - Control overdriven/underdriven nails.
5. **Install hold downs and tie components**
   - Use exact specified fasteners and installation sequence.
   - Verify bolt torque/tightness requirements where applicable.
6. **QC before inspection**
   - Walk every designated wall line against plan.
   - Photograph concealed conditions as needed for records.
7. **Address inspection comments quickly**
   - Correct hardware/fastener substitutions only with approved revisions.

## Risks and edge cases

- **Large garage openings**: leaves short wall returns that may not meet prescriptive bracing without specialty details.
- **Anchor misalignment**: bolts cast too close to plate edge or not aligned with boundary members.
- **Wrong fasteners**: common but critical (e.g., substituted nails/screws reduce rated capacity).
- **Mixed hardware systems**: incompatible connector/fastener combinations.
- **Field changes**: moving openings after plan review can invalidate shear layout.
- **Multi-story irregularity**: stacked wall discontinuities increase demand and often trigger engineering.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon residential projects are built under the currently adopted Oregon Residential Specialty Code (ORSC) and related state/local enforcement processes.
- In Ashland (City of Ashland, Jackson County), permit review and inspection are local functions under Oregon’s state code framework; verify current local procedures, submittal expectations, and inspection sequencing before construction.
- Southern Oregon seismic risk makes lateral load path quality a practical field priority, not just a plan-check item.
- For remodels/additions in older housing stock, expect frequent as-built mismatches that require coordination with the building official and, where needed, engineered fixes.

## Example scenario

A contractor is building a 2-story home with a wide front garage opening.

- The plans call out specific shear wall segments at each side of the garage and hold downs at segment ends.
- During foundation pour, one anchor is misplaced and no longer aligns with the required boundary post.
- Before framing proceeds, the contractor submits a correction path (approved retrofit anchor and revised connector detail as required).
- Framing crew installs sheathing per schedule and uses the exact connector fasteners.
- Pre-inspection QC catches one substituted screw type; it is replaced before inspection.

Result: framing inspection passes with minimal delay because the lateral system was managed as a sequence, not an afterthought.

## Checklist

- [ ] Confirm prescriptive vs engineered lateral design path.
- [ ] Mark all required shear wall segments on layout.
- [ ] Verify anchor/bolt placement before concrete set (or verify approved correction).
- [ ] Frame required boundary members/chords.
- [ ] Install sheathing and nailing pattern exactly as specified.
- [ ] Install hold downs and required fasteners per manufacturer listing.
- [ ] Verify continuity from diaphragm to foundation.
- [ ] Complete internal QC walk before calling inspection.
- [ ] Document any field deviations and approvals.
