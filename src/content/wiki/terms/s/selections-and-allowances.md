## Definition

**Selections and allowances** are a contract-and-estimating method used when some finish items (for example cabinets, flooring, tile, plumbing fixtures, lighting, appliances, or hardware) are not fully chosen at bid time. The contract includes allowance amounts (budget placeholders) and selection deadlines so work can start while final choices are still being made. Source: nascla-contractors-guide-or-2e, psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

## Why it matters

A residential general contractor uses allowances to balance two competing needs: lock in a project budget and schedule, while leaving room for owner design decisions that come later. Poorly defined allowances can lead to budget overruns, repeated change orders, owner conflict, and schedule slippage. Source: nascla-contractors-guide-or-2e.

For Oregon CCB exam readiness, this topic reinforces core competencies in estimating clarity, contract drafting, documentation, and owner communication. In practice, selection management is a business-process skill as much as a construction skill. Source: psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors, ccb-contract-law-rules-2014.

## Where it appears in a real project

- Pre-bid and proposal writing: deciding which items are fully specified vs allowance-based.
- Contract formation: listing allowance values, scope assumptions, and adjustment rules.
- Preconstruction meetings: confirming responsibility matrix (owner, designer, GC, vendor).
- Procurement and submittals: converting placeholder budgets into actual purchase commitments.
- Field execution: coordinating lead times and installation sequencing around final selections.
- Change administration and closeout: reconciling allowance differences and documenting final cost impacts.

## Inputs needed

- Owner priorities (quality level, preferred brands, durability expectations).
- Bid documents and drawings with any unresolved finish specs.
- Vendor budget ranges and lead-time data.
- Labor assumptions for installation complexity.
- Contract terms for how allowance differences are adjusted.
- Selection deadline calendar tied to the master schedule.

## Outputs produced

- Allowance schedule by category (material, labor, tax/shipping assumptions as needed).
- Written selection log showing status: pending, approved, ordered, delivered, installed.
- Updated buyout commitments and purchase orders.
- Change-order documentation for allowance overages/credits.
- Budget-to-actual tracking visible to the owner.
- Final reconciliation summary at project closeout.

## Key decisions

- Which items should be allowances versus fully specified before contract execution.
- How much contingency to include above baseline allowance values.
- Who owns each selection decision and by what date.
- How substitutions are evaluated if a chosen product is unavailable.
- Whether markup/overhead/profit treatment on allowance adjustments is explicitly defined.
- What level of supporting documentation (quotes, invoices, cut sheets) is required before approval.

## Basic workflow

1. Identify unresolved finish and fixture categories during estimating.
2. Set realistic allowance values using current vendor pricing and market availability.
3. Write allowance language clearly in the contract (scope, assumptions, adjustment method).
4. Publish a selection deadline schedule linked to procurement lead times.
5. Hold recurring owner selection meetings and log each decision.
6. Convert approved selections into purchase orders and field coordination tasks.
7. Issue change orders promptly for allowance deltas (up or down).
8. Reconcile all allowances against actuals before final payment.

## Risks and edge cases

- **Understated allowances:** bid appears competitive, but later increases erode trust.
- **Late selections:** delayed buyout causes long-lead material bottlenecks.
- **Specification drift:** owner changes style tier repeatedly, creating rework.
- **Scope ambiguity:** allowance covers product only, but labor complexity rises.
- **Vendor discontinuation/backorders:** approved selection becomes unavailable mid-project.
- **Tax/freight surprises:** allowance did not state whether logistics costs were included.
- **Multi-jurisdiction sourcing issues:** equivalent products may still require code/inspection verification.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

In Oregon residential work, allowance language should be clear enough that homeowners can understand what is included, what is not, and how the final price will be adjusted if actual costs differ. Clear written documentation and timely change-order execution help reduce disputes and support CCB-compliant business practices. Source: ccb-contract-law-rules-2014, oregon-ccb-guide-to-hiring-a-contractor.

For Ashland projects, the practical challenge is often lead-time volatility and seasonal access/logistics constraints. A GC should set earlier selection deadlines for critical-path items (for example windows, specialty fixtures, or custom cabinetry) and communicate that missing dates may shift completion milestones.

## Example scenario

A homeowner signs a $420,000 whole-home remodel contract in Ashland with a $22,000 combined allowance for flooring, bath fixtures, and lighting. The GC provides a milestone-based selection calendar. By week 4, fixture choices exceed allowance by $3,400; the GC issues a change order immediately so the owner can decide whether to approve upgrades or value-engineer elsewhere. Later, a lighting line is discontinued; the team documents an equivalent substitution and confirms lead time before order placement. At closeout, final allowance reconciliation shows +$2,150 net increase, fully documented with approved change orders.

## Checklist

- [ ] Allowance categories and dollar amounts are listed in the contract.
- [ ] Each allowance states what is included (materials, labor, tax, freight, markup treatment).
- [ ] Selection due dates are mapped to procurement and installation milestones.
- [ ] Owner decision-maker and approval method are documented.
- [ ] Every approved selection has a dated record and supporting quote.
- [ ] Allowance overages/credits are processed through signed change orders.
- [ ] Budget-to-actual allowance report is updated throughout the project.
- [ ] Final reconciliation is complete before final payment.
