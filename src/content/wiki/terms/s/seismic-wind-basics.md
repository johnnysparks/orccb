## Definition

**Seismic/wind basics** is the practical understanding of how a house resists earthquake and wind forces through a continuous load path, from roof and walls down to the foundation. For residential contractors, this includes knowing where lateral loads act, which connections transfer those loads, and how plans/specs encode those requirements.

## Why it matters

- Lateral-force mistakes are safety-critical because failures can be sudden and progressive.
- These details are easy to miss in production (clips, nailing patterns, holdowns, anchor layout), but they heavily affect performance in storms and earthquakes.
- Inspectors routinely check these items, so weak execution creates avoidable corrections, delays, and rework.
- Mastering this domain builds real contractor competence: reading plans, sequencing trades, and protecting life safety.

## Where it appears in a real project

1. **Plan review/preconstruction:** identify design wind assumptions, seismic design category cues, and all required shear/connection details.
2. **Foundation phase:** set anchor bolts, straps, and holdown hardware in correct locations before concrete placement.
3. **Framing phase:** install shear wall panels, boundary nailing, blocking, ties, and holdowns exactly as detailed.
4. **Roof and diaphragm phase:** complete roof/wall diaphragms and edge/blocking details that distribute lateral loads.
5. **Inspection and close-out:** verify hardware, fasteners, and load-path continuity before concealment.

## Inputs needed

- Approved structural sheets/details from the permit set.
- Applicable Oregon code path and current adopted residential code edition.
- Manufacturer instructions for connectors/anchors/holdowns.
- Framing and foundation layout dimensions tied to control lines.
- Fastener schedule (type, size, spacing, edge distances, corrosion class as applicable).
- Inspection hold-point plan (what must be visible before covering).

## Outputs produced

- A complete and inspectable lateral load path from roof to foundation.
- Installed and documented shear walls, diaphragms, and collectors per plan.
- Correctly placed anchors/holdowns/straps with compliant fasteners.
- Passed framing/structural inspections with minimal corrections.
- Reduced storm and earthquake damage risk for occupants and owners.

## Key decisions

- **Plan path:** prescriptive residential path versus engineered details on the project.
- **Shear wall strategy:** which wall lines and segments carry lateral demand.
- **Connection hardware:** exact connector families and fastener sets that match plans and manufacturer data.
- **Sequencing:** when to set embeds and holdowns so they are accurate and not blocked by later work.
- **Quality control:** how to verify nailing, blocking, and hardware before finishes conceal the work.

## Basic workflow

1. Review structural plans and mark every lateral element on a field copy.
2. Hold pre-task meeting with foundation/framing crews to align on hardware and inspection points.
3. During layout, verify wall lines and holdown/anchor locations against dimensions.
4. Install embeds/anchors at foundation stage; photograph and document prior to pour when possible.
5. Frame shear walls and diaphragms with required blocking and nailing patterns.
6. Install connectors/straps/holdowns with exact fastener counts and types.
7. Perform internal QC walk focused only on lateral system details.
8. Call inspection before concealment and close out any corrections immediately.

## Risks and edge cases

- Wrong fastener (or wrong count) in a connector can invalidate rated capacity.
- Missed blocking at diaphragm/shear boundaries weakens load transfer.
- Field changes to openings/windows can reduce shear capacity unless redesigned.
- Overdriven or poorly spaced nails can reduce panel performance.
- Wet or fire-retardant-treated materials may require adjusted connector/fastener selections.
- Mixed responsibility between designer, supplier, and field crew can cause scope gaps unless explicitly coordinated.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon residential projects must follow the currently adopted state code framework as locally administered; do not rely on memory from prior editions.
- In Ashland, contractors should assume greater attention to lateral resilience because of regional seismic exposure and seasonal wind events.
- Hillside topography common in parts of Ashland can amplify design/coordination complexity at foundation-to-frame interfaces.
- Practical takeaway: verify jurisdiction expectations early, then execute strict QC on connections and inspection sequencing.

## Example scenario

A contractor is building a two-story home in Ashland. The structural sheets show designated shear wall segments at garage and stair-core lines, with holdowns at specific ends.

During framing QC, the lead identifies that one crew used a common nail schedule on a designated shear segment instead of the denser schedule shown in the details. The contractor pauses sheathing progress, corrects nailing in affected segments, and rechecks connector fasteners against manufacturer instructions before calling inspection.

Result: one-day schedule impact, but no concealed defect, a clean inspection, and preserved lateral-system intent.

## Checklist

- [ ] Structural sheets reviewed; lateral elements highlighted on field set.
- [ ] Foundation anchors/holdown locations verified before concrete placement.
- [ ] Shear wall segments framed per geometry and boundary requirements.
- [ ] Blocking and diaphragm edge details complete.
- [ ] Connector models match plan callouts.
- [ ] Fastener type/count/spacing verified for each connector and panel schedule.
- [ ] Field opening changes checked for structural impact before execution.
- [ ] Internal lateral-system QC completed before concealment.
- [ ] Required inspections passed and corrections documented.
