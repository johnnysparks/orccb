## Definition

**Service sizing** is determining the capacity of the home's electrical service so expected loads can be supplied safely with code-compliant overcurrent protection and distribution.

## Why it matters

Undersized service limits electrification choices and creates upgrade costs later. Oversizing without rationale can increase cost without meaningful benefit.

## Where it appears in a real project

- Early utility and meter planning
- Electrical design and permit documentation
- Main panel and feeder decisions
- Future-ready planning (EV, heat pump water heater, PV/battery)

## Inputs needed

- Calculated demand assumptions
- Major appliance/system selections
- Utility service availability
- Future expansion goals
- Jurisdiction requirements

## Outputs produced

- Selected service ampacity
- Main disconnect and panel strategy
- Feeder/conductor planning basis
- Future capacity notes in design documents

## Key decisions

1. Present-load compliance versus future-ready margin.
2. Single large panel versus distributed subpanel strategy.
3. Tradeoffs between immediate budget and upgrade avoidance.
4. Which future loads to pre-plan now.

## Basic workflow

1. Compile existing and planned electrical loads.
2. Coordinate with utility and electrician on service constraints.
3. Evaluate baseline and future-ready service options.
4. Lock panel/service approach for permit set.
5. Verify as-built alignment before final inspection.

## Risks and edge cases

- Late addition of high-demand equipment.
- Service upgrades delayed by utility timelines.
- Detached structures with separate load profiles.
- Renovations where existing service records are incomplete.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

Ashland clients increasingly ask for all-electric and resilience-ready homes. Service sizing decisions should reflect likely future electrical loads, not just minimum current equipment lists.

## Example scenario

A project starts with gas appliances but owner later shifts to electric cooking and heat-pump water heating. Because the GC planned modest future capacity, the project absorbs scope changes without major redesign.

## Checklist

- [ ] Demand assumptions documented
- [ ] Utility constraints confirmed
- [ ] Future loads discussed with owner
- [ ] Service/panel approach locked before permit
- [ ] As-built service verified at closeout
