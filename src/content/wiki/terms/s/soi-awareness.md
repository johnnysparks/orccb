## Definition

**SOI awareness** is a contractor's habit of continuously checking **site observations and inspection implications** before work starts, while work is in progress, and before sign-off.

In practical study terms, SOI awareness means you do not treat field conditions as static. You keep comparing what plans say, what the site is doing, and what the jurisdiction/inspector will require next.

## Why it matters

- It prevents expensive rework by catching conflicts early (grades, drainage routes, utility conflicts, access issues).
- It protects safety by forcing active hazard checks as conditions change.
- It helps you pass inspections in sequence instead of getting stuck at closeout.
- It is a core "general contractor" behavior: connecting paperwork, field reality, and regulatory checkpoints.

## Where it appears in a real project

- **Feasibility and bid:** initial walk, slope/drainage notes, access constraints, and risk pricing.
- **Preconstruction:** permit set review, staging plans, trench/excavation planning, and inspection mapping.
- **Execution:** daily observation cycles, weather-triggered reassessments, and hold points before covering work.
- **Closeout:** final grading/drainage confirmation, life-safety access checks, and documentation package prep.

## Inputs needed

- Approved plans, details, and permit conditions.
- Local jurisdiction inspection process and timing windows under Oregon's statewide/local administration model. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.
- Existing site observations (topography, soils behavior, runoff, adjacent property conditions).
- Safety requirements for excavation/trenching where applicable. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.
- Trade sequence, material lead times, and inspection hold points.

## Outputs produced

- A current SOI log (what changed, what was verified, what action is required).
- Better sequencing decisions and fewer covered-up defects.
- Inspection-ready work packages with fewer corrections.
- Reduced schedule drift from late discovery of field conflicts.

## Key decisions

1. **Escalate now or monitor?** Decide when an observation requires immediate stop/rework vs tracked monitoring.
2. **Inspect before cover-up?** Confirm whether a hold point is needed before backfill, pour, or enclosure.
3. **Revise scope or sequence?** Choose between changing the plan detail or changing execution order.
4. **Who owns resolution?** Assign a single accountable person for each issue (GC, sub, engineer, owner).
5. **Document threshold:** Decide what must be photo-documented and noted for future disputes/closeout.

## Basic workflow

1. **Plan review pass:** identify high-risk locations (grade transitions, trench crossings, drainage exits, setbacks).
2. **Pre-task site walk:** verify conditions against drawings before crews mobilize.
3. **Set hold points:** define "do not cover" triggers for inspections and internal QC.
4. **Run daily SOI checks:** record changes in weather, soils behavior, and trade interfaces.
5. **Close the loop fast:** issue field directives, update sequence, and communicate impacts the same day.
6. **Pre-inspection mock check:** verify likely inspector focus items before requesting inspection.
7. **Archive evidence:** store photos/notes tied to date, location, and decision.

## Risks and edge cases

- **Weather shift after layout:** sudden rain can invalidate previous assumptions on access or trench stability.
- **Late utility reroute:** new routing can destroy completed site work if not caught at hold points.
- **Boundary surprises:** fence/driveway tie-ins can conflict with actual property-line conditions.
- **Hidden soft spots:** soil variability can appear only under load and alter grading/foundation assumptions.
- **Inspection queue delays:** local scheduling delays can force resequencing and create trade stacking risk.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon's code framework is statewide, but project administration and inspections are local; GC workflow must adapt to local process details. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.
- In Ashland-area work, sloped lots and seasonal rain patterns make drainage and access observations especially important for SOI discipline.
- If excavation is involved, ongoing soil-condition and protective-system awareness is not optional. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.

## Example scenario

A residential GC in Ashland is preparing to pour driveway and entry flatwork. During the pre-task SOI walk, the superintendent notices utility trench settlement beginning near the garage approach after overnight rain.

Instead of pouring on schedule, the GC pauses flatwork, requires recompaction verification, rechecks drainage fall, and reschedules inspection timing. The one-day delay avoids a likely failed drainage outcome and future ponding callback.

## Checklist

- [ ] I reviewed plans and permit notes for site-sensitive inspection triggers.
- [ ] I completed a pre-task field walk and compared findings to drawings.
- [ ] I marked hold points for work that cannot be covered without verification.
- [ ] I logged weather/soil changes that affect means, methods, or safety.
- [ ] I assigned owner/responsibility for each SOI issue discovered.
- [ ] I performed a pre-inspection mock check before calling for inspection.
- [ ] I archived photos and notes tied to date, area, and decision.
