## Definition

**Gravity/lateral systems** are the two core structural behaviors in a house:

- **Gravity system:** carries vertical loads (roof, floors, walls, people, furniture, snow) safely down to the soil.
- **Lateral system:** resists sideways loads (wind and earthquake) and keeps the structure from racking, sliding, or overturning.

Contractor competence means understanding both systems separately and as one connected load path.

## Why it matters

- Most residential failures come from missing load-path continuity at transitions (roof-to-wall, wall-to-floor, floor-to-foundation).
- You cannot schedule framing, inspections, and corrections well unless you know which elements are gravity-critical vs lateral-critical.
- This domain is foundational for reading plans, coordinating subs, and avoiding expensive late-stage structural fixes.

## Where it appears in a real project

1. **Plan review:** identifying beams, bearing walls, shear walls, diaphragms, and connectors.
2. **Foundation phase:** footing/anchor layout that receives both vertical and lateral demand.
3. **Framing phase:** bearing paths, headers, sheathing, blocking, and hardware installation.
4. **Inspection phase:** confirming gravity support and lateral continuity before concealment.

## Inputs needed

- Approved plan set (architectural + structural details).
- Current Oregon adopted code references and local enforcement context. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption.
- Site conditions (slope, soil assumptions, exposure).
- Material and connector schedules.
- Framing/foundation sequencing plan.

## Outputs produced

- A mapped and buildable load path for vertical and lateral forces.
- Installed framing and connections that match structural intent.
- Inspection-ready conditions with fewer correction notices.
- Better coordination between concrete, framing, roofing, and envelope scopes.

## Key decisions

- Which walls are **bearing**, which are **shear**, and which are both.
- Where load concentrations need posts, beams, collectors, or special connectors.
- Prescriptive path vs engineered path at irregular conditions.
- When field changes require design clarification before proceeding.

## Basic workflow

1. Mark load-path elements on plan set (gravity and lateral in different colors).
2. Verify foundation/interface locations before framing starts.
3. Build and check gravity path first (bearing continuity).
4. Install lateral components (sheathing, blocking, straps/hold-downs, anchors) to detail.
5. Perform focused QC walk for transitions and connector fasteners.
6. Call inspection before concealment and close corrections quickly.

## Risks and edge cases

- Misidentifying a bearing wall during layout changes.
- Large openings reducing available shear wall length.
- Hardware substitutions that reduce lateral capacity.
- Slope/hillside conditions increasing foundation-to-frame complexity.
- Multi-story offsets that break simple stacked load assumptions.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon uses a statewide adopted-code framework; always verify current cycle before relying on prior practice. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-orsc-adoption.
- Ashland-area projects often include slopes and seismic sensitivity, making load-path continuity especially important at foundation interfaces.
- Local inspection administration can vary, so confirm authority/process early in scheduling.

## Example scenario

A two-story house has a wide great-room opening that shifts roof and floor loads to fewer points, while the same wall line is also part of the lateral system.

You flag this early, confirm the structural detail package with the field team, and stage framing so bearing elements and lateral hardware are both visible during QC before inspection.

Result: no concealed load-path breaks and a cleaner inspection cycle.
