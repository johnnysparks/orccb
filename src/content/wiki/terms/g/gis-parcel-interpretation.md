## Definition

**GIS parcel interpretation** is the process of reading map-based parcel data (property boundaries, zoning layers, overlays, utilities, topography cues, and jurisdiction notes) to make early project decisions before design, permitting, and field work.

## Why it matters

- It helps a residential GC identify feasibility problems early, before money is committed to full plans.
- It reduces avoidable redesign by exposing constraints like setbacks, easements, slope challenges, or access limitations.
- It improves estimating accuracy because scope assumptions are tied to site reality, not guesswork.
- It supports cleaner permit submittals by aligning plans with jurisdiction expectations from the start.

## Where it appears in a real project

- **Lead intake / feasibility:** first-pass site viability check for a remodel, addition, ADU, or new home.
- **Preconstruction planning:** establish likely building envelope and site logistics assumptions.
- **Design coordination:** communicate constraints to designer, engineer, surveyor, and owner.
- **Permit strategy:** verify which jurisdiction is likely to review and inspect the project. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.
- **Construction execution:** confirm that field layout and access plans still match approved assumptions.

## Inputs needed

- Site address and tax lot/parcel identifier.
- County or city GIS parcel viewer output (parcel lines, dimensions if shown, adjacent right-of-way context).
- Jurisdiction mapping (city vs county authority, local program boundaries). Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.
- Current code-administration lookup resources for applicable building-code pathways. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.
- Any available supporting records (plat map, legal description, title report, survey, prior permits).

## Outputs produced

- A preliminary parcel constraints brief (buildable area assumptions, access assumptions, key unknowns).
- A risk list for due diligence (survey confirmation, geotechnical need, utility conflicts, easement verification).
- A permit-path recommendation (who to call first, what to confirm before design lock).
- Better preliminary estimate allowances tied to site conditions.

## Key decisions

1. **Go / no-go on pursuit:** Is the parcel likely suitable for the client’s goals?
2. **Survey timing:** Is GIS confidence sufficient for concept pricing, or is a boundary/topographic survey needed now?
3. **Design handoff detail level:** What constraints must the designer treat as hard limits?
4. **Contingency level in budget:** How much uncertainty remains around grading, access, and utility scope?
5. **Jurisdiction contact sequence:** Which building department or planning desk should be contacted first?

## Basic workflow

1. Pull parcel map layers and identify parcel geometry, frontage, and surrounding context.
2. Cross-check jurisdiction and code-administration authority for the site location. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.
3. Flag likely constraints (setback-sensitive shape, narrow access, slope exposure, apparent easement corridors).
4. Convert map observations into assumptions and open questions.
5. Validate high-risk assumptions with primary records (survey, title, jurisdiction response).
6. Update estimate and schedule risk ranges based on confirmed vs unconfirmed conditions.
7. Document decisions in the preconstruction file so later team members see the same baseline.

## Risks and edge cases

- GIS parcel lines may be approximate and not a legal boundary.
- Layer currency can lag recent lot-line adjustments or recorded easements.
- Split jurisdiction conditions (city limits, urban-growth boundary edges, or special districts) can change review path.
- Legacy driveways/utilities may appear in field reality but not clearly in GIS overlays.
- Steep or irregular parcels can create optimistic early assumptions that fail under survey-grade detail.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon projects operate under statewide code adoption, while permitting and inspections are locally administered; GIS interpretation must include "who has authority here" and not just "where is the lot." Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online, oregon-bcd-jurisdiction-renewals-oar-918-020.
- In Ashland-area residential work, hillside topography and constrained lots can make early parcel interpretation especially valuable for retaining-wall scope, driveway design feasibility, drainage planning, and construction access assumptions.
- Ashland-adjacent projects may also involve city/county context questions, so confirming the exact permitting path early reduces schedule churn.

## Example scenario

A GC is asked to price a detached ADU on a lot outside central Ashland. Before commissioning full drawings, the GC reviews GIS parcel layers and notices a narrow frontage and apparent utility corridor near the preferred ADU location. The GC marks these as high-risk assumptions, requests early survey/title confirmation, and schedules a jurisdiction pre-application call. The final concept shifts the ADU footprint to preserve access and avoid costly redesign after permit comments.
