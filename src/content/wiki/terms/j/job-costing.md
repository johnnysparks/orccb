## Definition

**Job costing** is the discipline of planning, tracking, and comparing the real cost of labor, materials, equipment, subcontractors, permits, and overhead for each construction project and each phase of work. In residential general contracting, it connects your estimate to field reality so you can manage profit instead of guessing.

## Why it matters

- It tells you early whether a project is on track, over budget, or under budget.
- It supports better decisions on staffing, procurement timing, change orders, and schedule recovery.
- It helps you bid future jobs more accurately because your historical costs become reliable data.
- It protects business stability: profitable projects fund warranty work, payroll, insurance, and growth.
- It is a core competency bridge from “builder” to “business-minded residential general contractor.”

## Where it appears in a real project

1. **Preconstruction:** building estimate line items and cost codes.
2. **Contract setup:** confirming scope, allowances, and budget assumptions.
3. **Construction execution:** daily/weekly posting of labor hours, invoices, and subcontract bills.
4. **Progress billing:** comparing percent complete versus cost-to-date.
5. **Closeout:** final cost reconciliation and lessons learned for the next bid.

## Inputs needed

- Signed scope and pricing structure (fixed price, cost-plus, or time-and-materials with controls).
- Estimate broken into practical cost codes (site work, concrete, framing, mechanical, etc.).
- Labor tracking method (timesheets by task/cost code, including burden assumptions).
- Material commitments and invoices (POs, delivery tickets, vendor bills).
- Subcontract agreements and payment applications.
- Equipment/tool usage assumptions if tracked separately.
- Permit, inspection, and fee costs.
- Overhead allocation approach (project-level vs company-level recovery).
- Change order log and allowance usage log.

## Outputs produced

- Cost-to-date by cost code.
- Budget-versus-actual report.
- Estimated cost-to-complete and projected final cost.
- Gross profit forecast by job and by phase.
- Variance notes explaining over/under runs.
- Historical unit-cost data for future estimating.

## Key decisions

- **Cost code detail level:** too broad hides problems; too granular creates admin drag.
- **Update cadence:** weekly updates are a practical baseline for small-to-mid residential projects.
- **Labor burden assumptions:** whether taxes, workers’ comp, and small-tool burden are included consistently.
- **Change order timing:** whether costs hit before paperwork is approved (risk to cash flow and margin).
- **Allowance handling:** how and when allowance overages are communicated and authorized.
- **Contingency strategy:** whether contingency is held centrally or distributed across codes.

## Basic workflow

1. Start with your estimate and map every line item to a cost code.
2. Build a job budget baseline in your spreadsheet or job-cost software.
3. Set field rules: labor hours and receipts must be coded daily.
4. Enter committed costs (purchase orders/subcontracts) and actual costs (invoices/payroll).
5. Reconcile weekly: compare budget, committed, actual, and percent complete.
6. Investigate variances immediately (scope gap, productivity issue, waste, price escalation, or missed change order).
7. Issue corrections: production plan changes, procurement adjustments, or client-facing change orders.
8. Forecast final cost and margin at least monthly (weekly on high-risk jobs).
9. At closeout, archive final job-cost report and feed lessons into estimating templates.

## Risks and edge cases

- **Cost-code drift:** team members code similar costs to different buckets, obscuring trends.
- **Unapproved change work:** field performs extra work before written approval, creating collection risk.
- **Supplier price shock:** lead-time purchases differ from estimate assumptions.
- **Schedule compression:** overtime and re-sequencing increase labor cost faster than expected.
- **Hidden small losses:** many minor overruns across trades erase margin even if no single category appears catastrophic.
- **Warranty callbacks:** if not tracked, they quietly reduce true project profitability.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon residential contractors operating under CCB expectations should treat documentation and record discipline as part of professional practice, not optional back-office work. Good job costing supports cleaner dispute handling and better contract administration.
- In Ashland and nearby Southern Oregon markets, wildfire-hardening scope choices, seasonal weather constraints, and hillside/site-access complexity can change labor productivity and equipment needs; job costing helps separate normal variance from true estimating errors.
- Smaller regional subcontractor pools can cause pricing volatility and schedule gaps. A current job-cost system helps you update future bids with local reality instead of stale assumptions.

## Example scenario

A residential GC in Ashland signs a $480,000 remodel/addition contract. The framing estimate assumed 620 labor hours and a stable lumber package.

By week 5, job-cost tracking shows:
- Framing labor at 58% of budget while only 40% complete.
- Lumber package up 9% from estimate due to supplier repricing.
- Two owner-requested scope shifts started in field but not yet approved as change orders.

The GC responds in the same week:
1. Reassigns a lead carpenter to reduce rework and improve crew productivity.
2. Issues and secures written change-order approval for scope shifts.
3. Reforecasts projected final framing cost and updates overall margin forecast.
4. Adjusts upcoming bid templates for current lumber pricing behavior.

Result: margin compression is reduced early, cash flow risk is contained, and the team exits with better production data for the next project.

## Checklist

- [ ] Job budget is set from estimate before field work starts.
- [ ] Cost codes are clear, practical, and used consistently by office and field teams.
- [ ] Labor hours are entered at least daily and reviewed weekly.
- [ ] All invoices and subcontract bills are assigned to the correct code.
- [ ] Change orders are tracked, priced, and approved before extra work proceeds.
- [ ] Budget-versus-actual review happens on a fixed weekly cadence.
- [ ] Variances include written root-cause notes, not just numbers.
- [ ] Forecast-to-complete is updated regularly (monthly minimum).
- [ ] Final job-cost report is completed at closeout.
- [ ] Estimating templates are updated using closeout data.
