## Lesson

### Oregon safety authority stack for contractors

The legal framework starts with ORS 654.010 and ORS 654.022, which require safe and healthful workplaces and compliance with Oregon safety standards. Construction-specific duties are then implemented through Division 3 rules. Source: ors-chapter-654, oar-437-division-3-construction.

The CCB's laws-and-rules gateway reinforces that contractors are expected to navigate multiple authority sets, including CCB licensing rules and Oregon OSHA safety rules. This is why safety questions can appear as compliance-navigation scenarios on the pre-license exam. Source: oregon-ccb-laws-rules, psi-cib-oregon-construction-contractors.

### Accident-prevention responsibility and competent-person oversight

Subdivision C (including adopted 1926.20) places accident-prevention responsibility on the employer and requires frequent and regular inspections of jobsites, materials, and equipment by competent persons. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety.

In practical terms, a competent person <!-- TODO: link competent-person --> is not optional on active hazard work. Exam scenarios often test whether inspections are occurring with sufficient frequency and by someone qualified to identify hazards and take corrective action. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety, oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.

### Fall protection and training

Oregon construction rules use Oregon-specific fall-protection duty language in OAR 437-003-1501 and require employer training for employees exposed to fall hazards under OAR 437-003-0503. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-m-fall-protection.

For exam purposes, separate two questions: "Is fall protection required here?" and "Has the exposed worker been trained?" Both are enforceable duties. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-m-fall-protection.

### PPE hazard assessment and employee instruction

Subdivision E requires employers to assess PPE hazards, ensure PPE is provided/used/maintained in sanitary and reliable condition, remove defective PPE from use, and train employees on PPE use and limits. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-e-ppe.

The exam typically does not require memorizing every PPE type, but it does expect you to know the structure: hazard assessment first, then proper selection/use/maintenance, then training. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-e-ppe.

### Excavation and trench rules

Subdivision P contains high-frequency excavation triggers. Trenches 4 feet or more deep must have safe egress arranged so workers do not travel more than 25 feet laterally. Daily inspections of excavations, adjacent areas, and protective systems must be performed by a competent person before work and as needed through the shift. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.

Protective systems are generally required unless the excavation is less than 5 feet deep and a competent person finds no indication of a possible cave-in. Utility locations must also be estimated before excavation and exactly located by safe means when approaching underground installations. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.

### Ladder basics that are easy to miss

Subdivision X requires removing or tagging defective ladders out of service. It also requires portable ladder side rails to extend at least 3 feet above the upper landing unless an equivalent grasping support method is used. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-x-stairways-ladders.

In exam scenarios, these ladder questions are often framed as quick compliance checks: Was a damaged ladder left in use? Was the access ladder extension adequate? Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-x-stairways-ladders.

## Audio Script

<!-- Estimated listening time: 8 minutes -->

In the Oregon CCB blueprint, Jobsite Safety is a small but important section: four scored items. Because there are only a few questions, the test tends to target high-yield rules that you should apply quickly.

Start with the authority map. ORS Chapter 654 gives the broad duty to provide a safe workplace and comply with safety rules. Oregon OSHA Division 3 then gives the construction-specific requirements.

Next, remember the big five safety buckets likely to show up.

First, competent-person oversight. Employers must run accident-prevention efforts and perform frequent and regular inspections through competent persons.

Second, fall protection. Oregon uses its own fall-protection duty language, and employees exposed to fall hazards must be trained.

Third, PPE. Employers must assess hazards, provide suitable protective equipment, keep it reliable, and train employees on use and limits.

Fourth, excavation safety. At four feet and deeper, safe egress is required. Excavations and protective systems need daily competent-person inspections, and protective systems are usually required unless a shallow-excavation exception applies.

Fifth, ladder safety. Defective ladders must be removed or tagged out, and extension ladders generally need the 3-foot rail extension above a landing.

On test day, treat safety questions as duty triggers: who must do what, when, and under which Oregon rule.

## Key Terms

**Competent Person** — A person who can identify existing and predictable hazards in surroundings or working conditions and has authority to take prompt corrective measures, used repeatedly in construction inspection requirements.

**Fall Protection** — Oregon construction requirements for preventing worker falls, including duty-to-protect and employee training obligations in Subdivision M.

**Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)** — Protective equipment selected from hazard assessment, maintained in reliable condition, and supported by worker training under Subdivision E.

**Excavation Safety** — Oregon trench and excavation controls including egress, protective systems, utility-location precautions, and competent-person inspections.

**Ladder Safety** — Oregon ladder-use requirements including removing defective ladders from service and meeting setup/extension standards.

## Common Mistakes

Assuming federal numbering always means federal language applies unchanged. Oregon sometimes adopts, modifies, or replaces federal provisions, so check the Oregon rule text in Division 3. Source: oar-437-division-3-construction, oar-437-003-subdivision-m-fall-protection.

Treating safety training as optional once PPE or fall gear is available. Division 3 rules require training where exposure exists, not only gear availability. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-m-fall-protection, oar-437-003-subdivision-e-ppe.

Confusing excavation thresholds. The 4-foot trigger concerns safe egress, while the less-than-5-foot condition is part of a limited protective-system exception based on competent-person findings. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.

Missing ladder housekeeping basics. A single defective ladder left in service can make the scenario noncompliant even if all other controls are in place. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-x-stairways-ladders.
