## Definition

**Exterior site improvements** are the non-building elements that make a residential project functional, safe, and code-compliant outside the house footprint: grading, drainage paths, driveways, walkways, retaining elements, utility trench restoration, exterior stairs/landings, fencing, and final site stabilization.

## Why it matters

- It directly affects water management, access, safety, and long-term durability.
- Poor site work can trigger failed inspections, neighbor/property disputes, erosion issues, and expensive rework.
- It is where many contractors lose schedule control near project closeout because site work is often weather-sensitive and coordinated late.

## Where it appears in a real project

- **Preconstruction:** site observations, topo/survey review, rough drainage concept.
- **Permit phase:** grading/drainage details in permit set (when required by jurisdiction).
- **Construction phase:** trench backfill, rough and finish grading, flatwork, access improvements, and stabilization.
- **Closeout:** final drainage check, safe egress/access confirmation, and punch-list corrections.

## Inputs needed

- Approved plans and any civil/site details.
- Jurisdiction requirements and inspection triggers for grading, drainage, and exterior access. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.
- Existing site conditions (slope, soils behavior, runoff patterns, utilities).
- Trade scopes (excavation, concrete, utilities, landscaping) and sequencing plan.
- Safety planning for trenches and excavations. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.

## Outputs produced

- A site that drains away from structures and routes water intentionally.
- Safe, usable exterior circulation (walks, stairs, landings, driveway transitions).
- Completed and documented exterior scopes ready for inspection and turnover.
- Reduced call-backs tied to puddling, settlement, and access hazards.

## Key decisions

1. **Drainage strategy:** where water goes in storms and how overflow is handled.
2. **Elevation control:** finish grades at slab/entry/garage and tie-in points.
3. **Scope boundaries:** what is contractor scope vs owner/landscape scope.
4. **Material standards:** compaction expectations, concrete details, base prep, and tolerances.
5. **Inspection timing:** when to request rough/final checks to avoid rework.

## Basic workflow

1. Walk the site and mark existing flow lines, low points, and conflict zones.
2. Confirm permit drawings and jurisdiction notes before field layout. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.
3. Coordinate utility trenches, backfill, and compaction before finish surfaces.
4. Set rough grades and verify drainage intent before placing hardscape.
5. Build flatwork/site elements and protect them from construction damage.
6. Perform final grade/stabilization and complete inspection prep package.
7. Do a final water-flow and access safety walkthrough before turnover.

## Risks and edge cases

- Unexpected subsurface conditions causing over-excavation or settlement risk.
- Storm events during construction that erode unfinished slopes.
- Drainage conflicts at property lines or shared drive approaches.
- Late utility changes that cut through finished site work.
- Trench safety and cave-in risk if controls are weak. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

- Oregon uses a statewide code framework, but administration and inspection are local-jurisdiction functions; always confirm how your permitting authority applies site-related submittal and inspection requirements. Source: ors-chapter-455, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.
- In Ashland-area projects, hillside lots, seasonal rain patterns, and wildfire-conscious site planning can make exterior improvements a critical part of permit strategy and homeowner risk reduction.

## Example scenario

A contractor is building a new single-family home on a sloped lot near Ashland. Framing is nearly complete, and the driveway and side-yard drainage are deferred until late stage. The GC runs a coordination meeting with excavation, concrete, and utility trades, then re-sequences work so trench backfill/compaction happens before flatwork, and rough grades are verified in a rain event simulation. Final work includes driveway placement, swale shaping, entry walk transitions, and erosion stabilization. The project avoids a common closeout failure: ponding at the garage apron.

## Checklist

- [ ] Approved site details are in the field set.
- [ ] Drainage paths are mapped and verified against finished floor/entry elevations.
- [ ] Utility trench restoration and compaction are complete before hardscape.
- [ ] Exterior access routes are safe, continuous, and inspection-ready.
- [ ] Property-line and tie-in conditions are documented.
- [ ] Site stabilization/erosion controls are in place for closeout.
- [ ] Final walkthrough confirms no ponding at critical points.
