## Definition

Earthwork and excavation is the planning and field work used to cut, fill, trench, and grade soil so a house can be built safely, drained correctly, and supported by stable bearing conditions. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations, oregon-residential-specialty-code.

For Oregon exam prep, it combines two lenses: (1) production sequencing for site prep, and (2) safety controls required by Oregon OSHA excavation rules. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations, oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety.

## Why it matters

If earthwork is wrong, everything above it is harder: footings may need redesign, drainage can fail, schedule slips increase, and rework gets expensive.

On the jobsite safety side, trenches and excavations can become immediately life-threatening without inspection, egress, and protective systems when cave-in risk exists. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.

## Where it appears in a real project

In a typical residential build, this domain appears after preconstruction layout and before concrete foundations:

1. Site walkthrough, utility locate coordination, and rough staking.
2. Clearing/grubbing and temporary erosion-control setup.
3. Rough grading for access and pad elevation.
4. Excavation for foundations, retaining elements, and utilities.
5. Subgrade prep, compaction, and moisture conditioning.
6. Final grading and drainage shaping before closeout landscaping.

Study guidance: treat earthwork as a "handoff phase" that connects civil/site constraints to structural and code-required foundation work.

## Inputs needed

- Approved plans, geotechnical recommendations (if provided), and benchmark elevations.
- Permit constraints and jurisdiction requirements for site disturbance and inspections. Source: oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.
- Utility locate information and excavation limits.
- Equipment plan (excavator, compactor, haul strategy) and staffing.
- Safety plan with a designated competent person for excavation inspections. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.
- Weather window assumptions and drainage/temporary protection strategy.

## Outputs produced

- Buildable pad and trench geometry at design elevation.
- Subgrade condition suitable for planned footing/foundation work.
- Documented excavation safety checks and corrections directed by the competent person. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.
- Drainage pattern that routes water away from structure areas consistent with code intent for protecting the building envelope and foundation. Source: oregon-residential-specialty-code.
- Clean handoff package to concrete/foundation crews (layout verification, access, and tolerance confirmation).

## Key decisions

- Cut/fill balance versus import/export hauling cost.
- Whether field conditions require design clarification before digging deeper.
- Excavation support method selection when cave-in risk is present (sloping, shoring, shielding) and when to pause for reassessment. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.
- Dewatering and storm-response plan if soils are saturated.
- Sequence tradeoff: utilities first vs. foundations first in tight sites.

## Basic workflow

1. **Pre-task verification:** confirm plans, locate marks, permit conditions, and crew briefing.
2. **Hazard scan:** competent person reviews soil and surrounding hazards before work starts. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.
3. **Controlled excavation:** dig in lifts/segments that preserve access and inspection visibility.
4. **Protection and access:** provide required protective system and safe egress where rules trigger (for example, trenches at specified depths). Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.
5. **Verification loop:** check line/grade/elevation, correct deviations early, and re-inspect as conditions change.
6. **Handoff:** release area to footing/foundation work with drainage and stability checks complete.

## Risks and edge cases

- **Hidden water pathways:** perched water or unexpected seepage can destabilize trench walls.
- **Weather shock:** sudden rain can invalidate prior soil assumptions and require new inspections. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.
- **Utility conflicts:** unknown lines force redesign of trench paths and sequence.
- **Over-excavation:** digging below design elevation can trigger engineered correction and delay.
- **Boundary/tolerance drift:** minor staking errors compound into footing placement problems.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

Oregon contractors should study earthwork through both code and safety compliance:

- Oregon OSHA construction rules set excavation inspection and protection duties, including competent-person oversight. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations, oar-437-003-subdivision-c-general-safety.
- Residential construction must align with the Oregon Residential Specialty Code as adopted by the state and local jurisdiction. Source: oregon-residential-specialty-code, oregon-bcd-adopted-codes-online.

Ashland-specific study guidance: before mobilizing, verify current local permit submittal and inspection expectations through the city building program, because local administrative steps can change over time even when state code basis is stable.

## Example scenario

A small infill lot in Ashland has limited access and mixed soil after prior site disturbance.

- The superintendent sequences narrow-trench utility runs first to avoid crossing newly compacted footing zones.
- The competent person performs pre-shift and condition-change inspections; after rain overnight, trench access and sidewall condition are reassessed before anyone re-enters. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.
- Layout checks catch an elevation mismatch early, avoiding concrete rework.
- Final rough grade directs runoff away from the future structure footprint pending finish grading. Source: oregon-residential-specialty-code.

## Quiz questions

1. Which role must inspect excavations before work begins and as conditions change on Oregon jobsites?
   - A. Building official
   - B. Competent person
   - C. Excavator operator
   - D. Homeowner
   - **Answer:** B. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.

2. In residential earthwork, what is the best reason to verify grade and elevation before foundation placement?
   - A. To reduce survey costs only
   - B. To avoid any need for permit inspections
   - C. To prevent downstream footing/layout rework
   - D. To eliminate drainage planning
   - **Answer:** C (study guidance based on workflow risk management).

3. After heavy rain, what is the most competent next step before trench re-entry?
   - A. Continue if yesterday's inspection passed
   - B. Wait for the owner to approve
   - C. Reassess trench conditions and protective measures
   - D. Backfill immediately without review
   - **Answer:** C. Source: oar-437-003-subdivision-p-excavations.
