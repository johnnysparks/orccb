## Summary

Emergency response is the practical plan for what crews do in the first minutes of a crisis. Residential projects face variable risks: falls, medical events, fire, electrical incidents, and weather-related threats.

Prepared teams reduce harm by clarifying roles, communications, access, and decision authority before an emergency occurs.

## Lesson

### Definition
Emergency response is the coordinated set of immediate actions taken to protect life, summon help, control hazards, and stabilize a site after a critical event.

### Why it matters
- First minutes strongly influence outcomes.
- Clear roles reduce panic and confusion.
- Fast hazard control prevents secondary injuries.

### Where it appears in a real project
- Medical emergencies and traumatic injuries.
- Fire or smoke events.
- Structural instability, trench incidents, or severe weather.

### Inputs needed
- Site address details and access map.
- Emergency contacts and role assignments.
- First-aid/medical resources and extinguisher locations.
- Communication tools and backup methods.

### Outputs produced
- Posted emergency-action plan.
- Drill records and crew readiness checks.
- Post-incident improvement actions.

### Key decisions
- When to stop work and evacuate.
- Who calls emergency services and who directs responders.
- When area is safe for re-entry.

### Basic workflow
1. Recognize event and call stop-work.
2. Protect people from immediate hazards.
3. Contact emergency services and provide exact location.
4. Assign a guide to meet responders.
5. Preserve scene as appropriate after life-safety actions.
6. Debrief and update plan after incident.

### Risks and edge cases
- Incomplete address details delaying response.
- Blocked site access from materials or parked vehicles.
- Single-point communication failure (dead phone, no backup).

### Oregon/Ashland relevance
Oregon jobsite safety duties support proactive emergency planning and safe conditions. In Ashland, wildfire season and heat conditions can require rapid adaptation of evacuation routes, muster points, and smoke-response thresholds.

### Example scenario
A framing worker suffers heat-related distress during a summer shift. The foreman initiates stop-work, moves crew to shade, calls for medical support with exact location, and directs a worker to clear access for responders. The company later updates hydration and heat-break triggers for all jobs.

### Checklist
- [ ] Emergency contacts and exact site location are posted.
- [ ] Roles are assigned (caller, responder guide, area control).
- [ ] Access routes remain clear for emergency vehicles.
- [ ] Crew has practiced basic response drills.
- [ ] Post-incident debrief and updates are completed.

### Quiz questions
1. Why must emergency roles be assigned before an incident?
2. What information should the caller provide first?
3. How can blocked access change emergency outcomes?

## Audio Script

<!-- Estimated listening time: 7 minutes -->

Emergency response is won or lost before the emergency. If your team has clear roles and practiced actions, the first minutes become organized instead of chaotic.

At minimum, every site needs exact location information, emergency contacts, clear access routes, and assigned responsibilities. During an event, call stop-work, protect people from immediate harm, and contact emergency services with precise details.

Someone should be assigned to meet responders so they are not searching for access points. After life safety is stabilized, preserve scene facts as appropriate and begin internal documentation.

Then close the loop. Debrief what worked and what failed. Update the plan so the next response is faster and safer.

## Key Terms

**Jobsite safety** — The planning and execution system that includes emergency readiness, response, and continuous improvement.

## Common Mistakes

Assuming people will “figure it out” without assigned roles.

Not posting accurate address and access details.

Skipping drills because emergencies are infrequent.
