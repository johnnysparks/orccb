## Definition

Energy, water, and fire resilience detailing combines envelope and systems decisions so homes are efficient, moisture-managed, and better protected against ignition and spread pathways.

## Why it matters

Treating these as separate scopes causes conflicts (for example, air-sealing choices that trap moisture, or exterior details that ignore ember exposure). Integrated detailing reduces lifecycle risk and owner operating cost.

## Where it appears in a real project

- Assembly selection during design development.
- Detail sheets for cladding, vents, soffits, roofs, and penetrations.
- Mechanical sizing/ventilation coordination.
- Commissioning, punch, and owner handoff documentation.

## Inputs needed

- Climate/wildfire exposure assumptions.
- Energy target and insulation/air-sealing strategy.
- Drainage and drying path assumptions.
- Exterior material specifications and interface details.
- Mechanical ventilation and combustion strategy.

## Outputs produced

- Integrated detail set with explicit continuity notes (air, water, thermal, ignition resistance).
- Inspection hold points for high-risk interfaces.
- Owner maintenance guidance for resilience features.

## Key decisions

1. Where control layers stay continuous at each transition.
2. Which exterior assemblies balance durability and fire-awareness.
3. Ventilation/dehumidification strategy for seasonal performance.
4. Which details need mockups due to compound risk.

## Basic workflow

1. Map control layers (water, air, thermal, ignition-sensitive zones).
2. Select assemblies and compatible products.
3. Draw and review interface details with all affected trades.
4. Install with staged QA before concealment.
5. Verify performance assumptions at closeout.

## Risks and edge cases

- Airtightness gains without moisture-safe drying strategy.
- Wildfire-aware upgrades that break ventilation intent.
- Incomplete detailing at penetrations and service entries.
- Owner maintenance gaps that degrade resilience features.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

Oregon projects face seasonal moisture and varying exposure contexts. Ashland-area teams may also evaluate wildfire-aware detailing strategies in site and material decisions. A GC should verify current jurisdiction requirements and guidance during design and permit review.

## Example scenario

On a hillside project, a trainee GC coordinates soffit vent screening, cladding termination, and window flashing so airflow, drainage, and ember-aware detailing all stay intact. The result is fewer trade conflicts and better long-term performance.

## Checklist

- [ ] Control layers mapped and continuous.
- [ ] Assembly compatibility reviewed.
- [ ] High-risk interfaces detailed and assigned.
- [ ] Pre-cover inspections documented.
- [ ] Closeout includes maintenance instructions.
