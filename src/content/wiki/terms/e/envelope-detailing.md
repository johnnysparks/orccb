## Definition

Envelope detailing is the design and execution of transitions where exterior assemblies meet: foundation-to-wall, wall-to-roof, penetrations, openings, decks, and terminations. It makes the building shell watertight, durable, and serviceable.

## Why it matters

Most callback risk is at interfaces, not in the middle of a field-applied material. Good detailing prevents leaks, rot, mold, and energy waste.

## Where it appears in a real project

- Plan details and manufacturer detail selection.
- Preconstruction coordination and mockups.
- In-progress inspections before concealment.
- Warranty closeout and troubleshooting.

## Inputs needed

- Climate exposure and site drainage conditions.
- Wall/roof assembly specs and product instructions.
- Structural geometry and movement assumptions.
- Penetration schedule and accessory selections.

## Outputs produced

- Detail set for each critical interface.
- Sequencing notes showing “who installs what, when.”
- Hold-point checklist and photo documentation standards.

## Key decisions

1. Drainage strategy continuity at all transitions.
2. Material compatibility and fastener/corrosion concerns.
3. Tolerance strategy for field variability.
4. Mockup requirements for high-risk details.

## Basic workflow

1. Identify all envelope transitions.
2. Assign default detail per transition.
3. Verify compatibility with structure and finishes.
4. Prebrief crews with sequencing and QA photos.
5. Inspect before cover and log corrections immediately.

## Risks and edge cases

- Reverse laps and discontinuous flashing paths.
- Balcony/deck ledger tie-in moisture traps.
- Overlooked penetrations added late by trades.
- Legacy wall tie-ins during remodels.

## Oregon/Ashland relevance

Oregon’s wet-season exposure makes drainage-first detailing critical. In Ashland, transitions may also need to account for wildfire-resilient exterior choices and maintenance access around steep or constrained sites.

## Example scenario

A GC trainee runs a pre-siding envelope walk and finds two unflashed penetrations and one reversed WRB lap at a deck intersection. Corrections are made before cladding, avoiding likely leak callbacks.

## Checklist

- [ ] Critical transitions identified and detailed.
- [ ] Compatibility checks completed.
- [ ] Crew sequence and ownership clarified.
- [ ] Mockup performed where risk is high.
- [ ] Pre-cover inspection and photos completed.
- [ ] Punch corrections closed before cladding.

## Quiz questions

1. Envelope failures most often occur:  
   A) In large uninterrupted wall fields.  
   B) At assembly transitions and penetrations.  
   C) Only at roof ridges.  
   D) Only in new construction.

2. Which action most reduces interface failures?  
   A) Skip sequencing notes.  
   B) Define detail ownership and inspect before cover.  
   C) Delay compatibility checks.  
   D) Ignore late penetrations.

3. Why are mockups useful?  
   A) They replace all inspections.  
   B) They reveal install issues before production scale.  
   C) They are only for commercial towers.  
   D) They eliminate manufacturer requirements.

**Answer key:** 1-B, 2-B, 3-B.
